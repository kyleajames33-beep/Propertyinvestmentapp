# Inspection and Due Diligence for New Property Investors

The inspection and due diligence stage for an investment property differs from an owner-occupier purchase. Your focus shifts from personal preferences to financial performance, tenant appeal, and compliance obligations. This guide explains what new investors should prioritise during due diligence in NSW.

## Building and Pest Inspection: Investment-Specific Concerns

A building and pest inspection remains essential for investment purchases, but your interpretation of the findings should reflect your role as a landlord rather than an occupant.

### Structural Issues and Immediate Costs

Structural defects affect your bottom line directly. Foundation problems, roof defects, and significant water damage require capital expenditure that may not be tax-deductible immediately. Distinguish between repairs (generally deductible) and capital improvements (depreciated over time). The Australian Taxation Office provides clear guidance on this distinction, and your accountant can advise on specific items.

### Health and Safety Obligations

As a landlord, you have a duty of care to your tenants. Defects that create safety hazards must be addressed promptly. These include:

- Loose or damaged balcony railings and decking
- Exposed asbestos materials
- Faulty electrical switchboards or wiring
- Gas leaks or poorly maintained gas appliances
- Structural instability of retaining walls, fences, or outbuildings

Failing to address safety hazards can expose you to liability claims and tribunal orders. The NSW Residential Tenancies Act 2010 requires landlords to provide and maintain the premises in a reasonable state of repair.

### Items That Affect Rentability

Some defects affect your ability to attract and retain tenants more than others. Inoperable heating in alpine regions, inadequate cooling in Western Sydney, or a non-functional oven are examples of defects that directly impact rental appeal. Consider the local rental market and tenant expectations when assessing these items.

### Pest Damage and Ongoing Risk

Active termite damage is a serious finding that requires immediate treatment. Beyond the repair cost, consider the ongoing management strategy. Properties with significant termite history may require annual monitoring and barrier systems. Factor these costs into your holding cost calculations.

## Rental Appeal Assessment: Will Tenants Want to Live Here?

An investment property only performs if tenants want to live in it. During inspections, assess the property from a tenant's perspective rather than your own preferences.

### Location Factors Tenants Consider

- **Proximity to transport**: Walkability to train stations, bus stops, and major road access.
- **School catchments**: Family-friendly suburbs with good public schools attract longer-term tenants.
- **Employment hubs**: Proximity to hospitals, universities, business parks, and industrial areas drives rental demand.
- **Amenities**: Shops, cafes, parks, gyms, and healthcare facilities within walking distance.
- **Safety**: Street lighting, neighbourhood presentation, and local crime rates.

### Property-Specific Appeal Factors

- **Floor plan and layout**: Functional layouts with good flow rent better than awkward configurations.
- **Natural light and orientation**: North-facing living areas and good natural light are highly valued.
- **Storage**: Built-in wardrobes, linen cupboards, kitchen storage, and garage space are important to tenants.
- **Parking**: Off-street parking is a significant advantage in most Sydney markets and many regional centres.
- **Outdoor space**: Courtyards, balconies, and gardens add appeal, though consider maintenance implications.
- **Kitchen and bathroom condition**: Modern, clean, and functional wet areas attract quality tenants.
- **Noise factors**: Proximity to major roads, flight paths, train lines, and commercial premises affects livability.

### Rental Market Research

Before purchasing, conduct thorough rental market research:

1. Check comparable rental listings on major portals for similar properties in the area.
2. Note the average days on market for rental properties. Properties that rent quickly indicate strong demand.
3. Review vacancy rate data from sources such as SQM Research. Vacancy rates below 2 per cent indicate a tight rental market.
4. Speak with local property managers about tenant demand, typical profiles, and any oversupply concerns.

Be realistic about rental income. Agents may quote optimistic figures. Base your calculations on conservative estimates supported by actual comparable rentals.

## Maintenance Cost Estimation: Short, Medium, and Long-Term

Every property requires ongoing maintenance. Accurately estimating these costs is essential for cash flow planning.

### Short-Term Maintenance (0 to 2 Years)

These are items likely to require attention soon after purchase:

- Painting and cosmetic touch-ups between tenancies
- Carpet cleaning or replacement
- Minor plumbing repairs (taps, toilets)
- Garden and grounds maintenance
- Appliance replacement (hot water systems have an average lifespan of 8 to 12 years)
- Smoke alarm compliance and testing

Budget approximately 1 to 2 per cent of the property value annually for maintenance on a standard residential property in average condition.

### Medium-Term Maintenance (2 to 7 Years)

These larger items come up less frequently but require planning:

- Kitchen and bathroom renovations or refreshes
- Floor covering replacement
- Roof maintenance or gutter replacement
- Fence replacement or repair
- Air conditioning servicing or replacement
- Window and door maintenance

Review the building inspection report to identify which of these items may be due during your intended holding period.

### Long-Term Maintenance (7 to 15 Years)

These are major capital items:

- Roof replacement
- Structural repairs
- Major plumbing or electrical upgrades
- Driveway and path replacement
- External painting of weatherboard or rendered homes
- Tree removal or major landscaping

Properties over 30 years old should have a sinking fund or maintenance reserve funded adequately to cover these costs. If not, you will need to set aside funds yourself.

### Special Considerations for Different Property Types

**Houses** generally have higher maintenance costs than apartments due to larger areas of responsibility, gardens, and external elements. You are responsible for all maintenance and repairs.

**Apartments and townhouses** share maintenance costs through strata levies. Review the capital works fund to assess whether the owners corporation is adequately funded for upcoming works. Special levies can significantly impact your cash flow.

**Older properties** (pre-1960s) often have charm and character but can carry higher maintenance costs due to aging infrastructure. Heritage-listed properties have additional restrictions and potentially higher maintenance requirements.

## Renovation Cost Estimation: Getting Quotes Before You Buy

Many investors purchase with a renovation strategy in mind. Whether you plan a cosmetic refresh or a substantial renovation, obtaining reliable cost estimates before purchase is essential.

### Types of Renovations

**Cosmetic renovations** typically include painting, new floor coverings, updated window furnishings, new fixtures and fittings in kitchens and bathrooms, and garden improvements. These range from $10,000 to $50,000 depending on the property size and scope.

**Structural renovations** involve removing or adding walls, extending rooms, or reconfiguring layouts. These require building approval and typically cost $100,000 or more.

**Kitchen renovations** range from $15,000 for a basic update to $50,000 or more for a full replacement with quality appliances and finishes.

**Bathroom renovations** typically range from $12,000 to $35,000 depending on size, fixtures, and tiling requirements.

### Getting Quotes Before Exchange

It is possible to obtain quotes before you exchange contracts. Arrange access through the selling agent for tradespersons to inspect and quote. Most tradespersons are familiar with this process. Be transparent with the agent about your intentions.

When obtaining quotes:

- Request itemised quotes so you can identify areas where costs can be adjusted.
- Allow a contingency of 15 to 20 per cent for unexpected issues.
- Confirm whether quotes include or exclude GST.
- Clarify whether building approval is required and who will obtain it.
- Check that tradespersons are appropriately licensed and insured.

### Renovation Value Add

Not all renovations add equal value. Focus on improvements that tenants value and that increase rental yield. Kitchens, bathrooms, air conditioning, and outdoor living spaces typically provide the strongest returns in the rental market. Consult local property managers for advice on what tenants in the area prioritise.

## Strata Report Analysis for Investment Apartments

For investment apartment purchases, the strata report requires particular attention to financial and governance factors that affect your ongoing costs and the property's value.

### Financial Health Indicators

**Levy amounts and trends**: Review the history of strata levy increases. Consistent above-inflation increases may indicate poor financial management or deferred maintenance catching up.

**Administrative fund balance**: This fund covers day-to-day expenses. A healthy balance indicates good cash flow management.

**Capital works fund (sinking fund)**: For established buildings, check that the sinking fund has been reviewed against a 10-year capital works plan. An unfunded or underfunded plan means special levies are likely.

**Special levies**: Multiple special levies in recent years are a red flag. Investigate what they were for and whether further levies are anticipated.

**Outstanding debts**: High levels of owner arrears suggest financial stress within the scheme and may affect the ability to fund maintenance.

### Building Condition and Compliance

Review minutes and reports for mentions of:

- **Fire safety compliance**: Required upgrades to fire safety systems can cost millions in larger buildings.
- **Facade and waterproofing defects**: Common in buildings constructed between 1995 and 2015 due to non-compliant cladding and waterproofing failures.
- **Defect claims against builders**: Check whether the owners corporation is pursuing the builder for defects under statutory warranty periods.
- **Asbestos management**: Older buildings require asbestos management plans.
- **Lifts and essential services**: Lift replacement costs $100,000 or more per lift.

### Governance and Management

Poorly managed strata schemes experience higher costs, more disputes, and lower capital growth. Indicators of poor governance include:

- Infrequent committee meetings
- High strata manager turnover
- Frequent disputes between owners
- Failure to enforce by-laws
- Inadequate insurance coverage

Well-managed schemes with proactive committees and adequate reserves protect your investment value and reduce the risk of unexpected costs.

## Compliance and Insurance Obligations for Landlords

Investment property ownership comes with legal obligations that owner-occupiers do not face. Understanding these before purchase helps you budget and plan appropriately.

### Smoke Alarm Compliance

NSW law requires all residential rental properties to have working smoke alarms installed in specified locations. Alarms must be tested and batteries replaced in accordance with the regulations. Failure to comply can result in penalties.

### Water Safety

Properties with swimming pools must comply with the Swimming Pools Act 1992, including registration, certification of barriers, and regular inspections. This applies to tenants and landlords alike.

### Electrical Safety

While not yet mandatory in all circumstances, landlords should ensure electrical installations are safe. Consider periodic safety checks by a licensed electrician, particularly for older properties.

### Asbestos Management

Properties built before 1990 may contain asbestos materials. Landlords have a duty of care to manage asbestos risks. An asbestos register and management plan may be required for investment properties.

### Landlord Insurance

Standard building insurance does not cover risks specific to rental properties. Landlord insurance typically covers:

- Loss of rental income due to tenant default or property damage
- Tenant damage to contents and the property
- Legal liability
- Some policies cover rent default and eviction costs

Obtain quotes from multiple providers. Policies vary significantly in coverage, exclusions, and premiums. Insurance Council of Australia data shows that landlord insurance claims for tenant damage and rent default have increased substantially in recent years.

### Fair Trading and Tribunal Obligations

Familiarise yourself with the NSW Residential Tenancies Act 2010 and your obligations as a landlord. The NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT) handles disputes between landlords and tenants. Understanding your obligations helps you avoid disputes and orders against you.

## Tax Depreciation Schedule: Engaging a Quantity Surveyor

Tax depreciation is one of the most significant deductions available to property investors. A tax depreciation schedule prepared by a qualified quantity surveyor can improve your after-tax cash flow substantially.

### What Is Tax Depreciation?

Tax depreciation allows you to deduct the decline in value of the building structure and plant and equipment items over time. There are two components:

- **Capital works deduction** (Division 43): The building structure and fixed items, depreciated at 2.5 per cent per year for 40 years from construction.
- **Plant and equipment** (Division 40): Removable fixtures and fittings such as carpet, appliances, blinds, and air conditioning units, depreciated over their effective life.

### Who Can Prepare a Depreciation Schedule?

Only a qualified quantity surveyor recognised by the Australian Institute of Quantity Surveyors (AIQS) or a similarly qualified professional can prepare a tax depreciation schedule for the Australian Taxation Office. Your accountant cannot prepare this schedule unless they are also a qualified quantity surveyor.

### When to Engage a Quantity Surveyor

You can engage a quantity surveyor before settlement to prepare a schedule based on the property details and your proposed improvements. Alternatively, engage one shortly after settlement. The cost of the schedule (typically $400 to $800) is itself tax-deductible.

### Pre-Purchase Depreciation Estimates

Some quantity surveyors offer pre-purchase depreciation estimates based on the property address and description. These estimates can help you compare the after-tax cash flow of different properties during your decision-making process.

## Due Diligence Checklist for Investment Properties

Use this checklist to structure your due diligence process:

**Building and structural**
- [ ] Building and pest inspection completed
- [ ] Major defects identified and costed
- [ ] Safety hazards noted and remediation planned
- [ ] Compliance of structures with council approvals verified
- [ ] Age and condition of major systems assessed (roof, plumbing, electrical)

**Financial analysis**
- [ ] Rental income estimate confirmed with comparable evidence
- [ ] Vacancy rate for the suburb reviewed
- [ ] All holding costs estimated (council rates, water, strata levies, insurance, property management, maintenance reserve)
- [ ] Depreciation schedule estimate obtained
- [ ] Cash flow projection prepared under realistic and conservative scenarios

**Strata and community title (if applicable)**
- [ ] Strata inspection report obtained and reviewed
- [ ] Sinking fund adequacy assessed
- [ ] Special levy history reviewed
- [ ] Pending maintenance and defect claims identified
- [ ] By-laws reviewed for restrictions

**Compliance and legal**
- [ ] Section 10.7 planning certificate reviewed
- [ ] Easements and restrictions on title identified
- [ ] Flood and bushfire zone checks completed
- [ ] Landlord compliance requirements confirmed
- [ ] Insurance quotes obtained (building, landlord, public liability)

**Market and location**
- [ ] Neighbourhood visited at different times
- [ ] Local infrastructure and development plans reviewed
- [ ] Comparable sales support the purchase price
- [ ] Local employment drivers and demand factors assessed

**Property management**
- [ ] Local property managers interviewed
- [ ] Management fee structures compared (typically 5 to 8 per cent of rental income plus GST)
- [ ] Letting fees and advertising costs confirmed
- [ ] Property manager's tenant screening process reviewed

## How to Quantify Defects in Dollar Terms for Negotiation

Inspection findings are most valuable when you can translate them into specific dollar amounts for price negotiation.

### Obtaining Repair Quotes

For each major defect identified in the building inspection:

1. Contact at least two licensed tradespersons for quotes.
2. Request itemised quotes specifying materials, labour, and any provisional sums.
3. Ask about timeframes, as delays may affect rental income.
4. Confirm whether building approval is required and include associated costs.

### Building Your Negotiation Position

Document all costs systematically:

| Item | Estimated Cost | Urgency | Approach |
|------|---------------|---------|----------|
| Roof repair | $8,000 | Before next winter | Vendor repair or price reduction |
| Subfloor ventilation | $3,500 | Within 12 months | Price reduction |
| Kitchen update | $22,000 | Cosmetic | Acknowledge but not negotiate |
| Termite barrier | $2,500 | At purchase | Price reduction |

Present this information to the selling agent with a clear request. Options include:

- Requesting a price reduction equal to or greater than the repair cost (accounting for your time and risk in managing repairs).
- Requesting the vendor complete specified repairs before settlement, with appropriate verification.
- Requesting a reduction that reflects both repair costs and the inconvenience to you as the purchaser.

### When to Negotiate and When to Walk Away

Negotiate when defects are manageable, quantifiable, and the property otherwise meets your investment criteria. Walk away when:

- Defects suggest fundamental structural problems.
- The cost of repairs exceeds your available capital and finance capacity.
- The vendor or agent has been deceptive about the property's condition.
- Multiple serious defects suggest widespread neglect or poor construction.

Remember that every dollar saved on the purchase price reduces your stamp duty and ongoing land tax liability (where applicable). Negotiation based on genuine defects is a standard part of property transactions.
