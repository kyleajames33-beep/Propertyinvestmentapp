# How to prepare your finances before buying your first home

Getting your finances in order is the single most important step you can take before you start inspecting properties. Without a clear picture of your borrowing capacity, deposit position, and lender requirements, you risk disappointment and wasted time. This guide explains how lenders assess first home buyers, what you need to save, and the common pitfalls that can derail your application.

## How lenders assess your application: the full picture

When you submit a home loan application, the lender looks at three things: your income, your expenses, and your existing debts. Each carries weight in the assessment process, and understanding how they fit together helps you present a stronger application.

### Income

Lenders want evidence of stable, ongoing income. This includes your base salary, overtime, bonuses, commissions, and any rental income from existing investment properties. If you are self-employed, most lenders require two years of tax returns and financial statements to verify your earnings. Casual and contract workers may face additional scrutiny, though some lenders will accept 12 months of consistent income history. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

Not all income is treated equally. Base salary is typically accepted at 100%, while overtime and bonuses may be shaded to 80% depending on consistency. Rental income from investments is usually counted at around 80% of gross rent to allow for vacancies and expenses. Government benefits like Family Tax Benefit may be accepted if they are ongoing and verifiable. [Source: Canstar Home Loan Statistics, 2024]

### Expenses

Lenders do not simply trust you to self-report your living costs. They use a combination of declared expenses and a benchmark called the Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) to estimate your outgoings. We cover the HEM in detail below, but the key point is this: if your declared expenses are lower than the HEM benchmark, the lender will use the HEM figure in their calculations. This can reduce your borrowing capacity. [Source: HEM Survey Data, 2024]

### Existing debts

Every existing debt reduces your borrowing capacity. Credit cards, personal loans, car loans, and Buy Now Pay Later accounts all count against you. Importantly, lenders assess credit card limits, not just your outstanding balance. A $10,000 credit card limit is treated as if you owe the full amount, even if you pay it off monthly. This can shave tens of thousands off your borrowing capacity. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

## The deposit: how much you really need

The standard advice is to save a 20% deposit. On a $700,000 property, that means $140,000 plus costs. In practice, many first home buyers purchase with less.

### Why 20% is the benchmark

A 20% deposit avoids Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI), gives you a stronger negotiating position, and demonstrates to the lender that you can manage your finances. It also provides a buffer if property values soften after you purchase. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

### Options below 20%

Several pathways exist for buyers with smaller deposits:

**The First Home Guarantee** allows eligible first home buyers to purchase with a deposit as low as 5% without paying LMI. The Australian Government guarantees up to 15% of the property value. Places are limited and released each financial year. [Source: Australian Government First Home Guarantee, 2024]

**Family guarantee or family pledge** arrangements allow a family member to use equity in their own home as additional security for your loan. This can effectively let you borrow up to 100% or more of the purchase price, though not all lenders offer this product and the guarantor takes on real risk. [Source: Canstar Home Loan Statistics, 2024]

**Lenders Mortgage Insurance** (discussed below) allows you to borrow up to 95% of the property value. You pay a premium that protects the lender, not you, if you default on the loan. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

### Deposit costs beyond the property price

Remember that your deposit is not the only cash you need. You will also need funds for stamp duty (unless exempt), conveyancing, building and pest inspections, loan application fees, and moving costs. Budget an additional 2% to 5% of the purchase price on top of your deposit. [Source: State Revenue Office Victoria, 2024]

## Lenders Mortgage Insurance: what it costs and when it applies

LMI is a one-off insurance premium that protects the lender if you default and the sale of the property does not cover the outstanding loan balance. It applies when you borrow more than 80% of the property value.

The premium is calculated based on your loan-to-value ratio (LVR) and loan amount. On a $700,000 property with a 10% deposit ($70,000), you might pay between $12,000 and $18,000 in LMI depending on the lender and insurer. The premium is typically capitalised, meaning it is added to your loan amount and you pay it off over the life of the loan. [Source: Canstar Home Loan Statistics, 2024]

LMI is not transferable between lenders. If you refinance to a new lender while your LVR remains above 80%, you may need to pay LMI again. Some lenders offer "LMI waivers" for certain professions, including medical practitioners, accountants, and lawyers, though eligibility criteria vary. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

You cannot shop around for LMI directly. The lender arranges it with their preferred insurer, typically either Helia or QBE in Australia. Different lenders may use different insurers with different premium scales, which is one reason why comparing loans on the total cost over time matters more than just the interest rate.

## Genuine savings requirements and what counts

Most lenders want to see evidence of "genuine savings" when your deposit is less than 20%. This demonstrates financial discipline and the ability to manage ongoing mortgage repayments.

### What counts as genuine savings

Genuine savings typically includes:

- Regular deposits into a savings account held for at least three months
- Shares or managed funds held for at least three months
- Extra repayments into an existing mortgage
- Funds accumulated through the First Home Super Saver Scheme (FHSSS)
- Term deposits held for at least three months [Source: Canstar Home Loan Statistics, 2024]

### What does not count

Funds that have appeared recently in your account may not satisfy the genuine savings test. This includes lump sum gifts from family, inheritances, tax refunds, or the sale of assets. Some lenders will accept non-genuine savings if your LVR is 90% or below, or if you can show a consistent savings pattern alongside the lump sum. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

The FHSSS is a notable exception. Contributions made through this scheme, even if recent, are recognised by most lenders as genuine savings because they required a deliberate, ongoing commitment. [Source: ATO First Home Super Saver Scheme, 2024]

## Credit score: how to check and improve yours before applying

Your credit score is a numerical representation of your creditworthiness, typically ranging from 0 to 1,000 or 0 to 1,200 depending on the credit reporting body. The three main credit reporting bodies in Australia are Equifax, Experian, and illion. [Source: Equifax Australia, 2024]

### How to check your score

You are entitled to a free credit report every three months from each reporting body. You can request this directly through Equifax, Experian, or illion. Some third-party services also provide free scores, though they may use estimates rather than the exact figure a lender sees. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

### What affects your score

Your score reflects your credit history, including:

- Repayment history on loans and credit cards
- The number of credit applications you have made (each application creates a "hard enquiry" that can lower your score)
- Defaults, bankruptcies, and court judgments
- The type and age of your credit accounts
- Whether you pay bills on time, including utilities and telco accounts under comprehensive credit reporting [Source: Equifax Australia, 2024]

### How to improve your score

If your score is below what you would like, take these steps before applying for a home loan:

1. Check your report for errors and dispute any incorrect listings
2. Pay all bills and existing loan repayments on time
3. Reduce credit card limits or close cards you do not need
4. Avoid applying for new credit in the six months before your home loan application
5. Pay down existing debts where possible
6. Ensure you are listed on the electoral roll [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

A higher credit score will not always get you a lower interest rate with mainstream lenders, but it can affect your approval chances and may unlock better rates with certain non-bank lenders.

## Reducing expenses: the HEMS benchmark and why it matters

The Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) is a benchmark used by Australian lenders to estimate your living expenses. It is based on data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics and factors in your location, household size, income level, and lifestyle (whether you are a "basic," "moderate," or "lavish" spender). [Source: HEM Survey Data, 2024]

### How HEM affects your borrowing power

When assessing your application, the lender compares your declared living expenses against the HEM figure for your household profile. They use the higher of the two figures in their serviceability calculation. This means that even if you genuinely spend less than the HEM benchmark, the lender may still assess you as if you spend at that level. [Source: Reserve Bank of Australia, 2024]

### Strategies to improve your position

While you cannot control the HEM benchmark itself, you can take steps to improve how a lender views your expenses:

- Reduce discretionary spending in the three to six months before applying, as bank statements will show this
- Cancel or reduce unused subscriptions and memberships
- Pay off or consolidate personal loans and car loans
- Reduce credit card limits, as these directly impact serviceability
- If your actual expenses are genuinely above the HEM, focus on increasing income or reducing debt instead [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

After the banking royal commission, lenders tightened expense verification significantly. Expect to provide three to six months of transaction account and credit card statements, with the lender categorising your spending against the HEM. [Source: Reserve Bank of Australia, 2024]

## First Home Buyer Assistance: how stamp duty concessions affect your deposit gap

Stamp duty is a state-based tax on property transactions and can be one of the largest upfront costs you face. Fortunately, every state and territory in Australia offers some form of concession or exemption for first home buyers, which can significantly reduce the total cash you need at settlement. [Source: State Revenue Office Victoria, 2024; Revenue NSW, 2024]

### How stamp duty concessions work

The specifics vary by state, but most jurisdictions offer either a full exemption or a concessional rate for first home buyers purchasing below a certain price threshold. For example, in Victoria, first home buyers pay no stamp duty on properties up to $600,000 and a concessional rate on properties between $600,000 and $750,000. In New South Wales, the First Home Buyer Assistance scheme offers full exemptions on new homes up to $800,000 and existing homes up to $650,000. [Source: State Revenue Office Victoria, 2024; Revenue NSW, 2024]

### Impact on your deposit gap

These concessions can reduce your upfront cash requirement by tens of thousands of dollars. On a $650,000 existing home in New South Wales, the stamp duty would ordinarily be approximately $24,500. With the first home buyer concession, this falls to zero. That $24,500 saving can form part of your deposit or cover other purchase costs. [Source: Revenue NSW, 2024]

You generally cannot use the stamp duty saving as part of your deposit in the lender's eyes, because it reduces your costs rather than increasing your cash. However, it means you need less total cash to complete the purchase, which may allow you to put more of your saved funds toward the deposit itself.

### First Home Owner Grant

Some states also offer a First Home Owner Grant for buyers of new homes. In Queensland, for example, eligible first home buyers can receive $30,000 toward the purchase of a new home, unit, or townhouse under certain value caps. Check NSW revenue office for current thresholds and eligibility. [Source: Revenue NSW, 2024]

## Pre-approval: what it is, what it is not, and how long it lasts

Pre-approval (also called conditional approval or approval in principle) is a lender's preliminary assessment that they would be willing to lend you a specific amount, subject to certain conditions being met.

### What pre-approval gives you

Pre-approval provides a realistic budget for your property search and signals to real estate agents and sellers that you are a serious buyer. It typically involves a full credit check, income verification, and expense assessment, though the lender has not yet valued a specific property. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

### What pre-approval does not guarantee

Pre-approval is not a guarantee of final loan approval. Key conditions usually include:

- A satisfactory valuation of the property you intend to purchase
- No material change in your financial circumstances between pre-approval and final application
- Verification of the deposit source
- Meeting the lender's requirements for the specific property type and location

If your circumstances change, if interest rates rise significantly, or if the lender updates their policies, your pre-approval may no longer be valid even within the stated expiry period. [Source: Canstar Home Loan Statistics, 2024]

### How long pre-approval lasts

Most pre-approvals are valid for 90 days, though some lenders offer 60-day or 120-day periods. If your pre-approval expires before you find a property, you can usually renew it, though the lender will re-assess your position and may perform another credit check. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

## Documents you will need to gather

Lenders require extensive documentation. Gathering these before you apply will speed up the process and reduce stress:

### Identification

- Primary photo ID (passport or driver's licence)
- Secondary ID (Medicare card, birth certificate, or citizenship certificate)
- Proof of address (utility bill or bank statement)

### Income evidence

- Last two payslips
- Group certificate or tax return for the most recent financial year
- If self-employed: two years of tax returns, financial statements, and notice of assessments
- Rental income evidence: lease agreements and bank statements showing rent received
- Other income: Centrelink statements, dividend statements, or trust distribution notices

### Asset and liability statements

- Three to six months of statements for all transaction accounts
- Three to six months of statements for all credit cards
- Statements for existing loans (personal, car, investment)
- Evidence of savings and deposit (three to six months of statements)
- Superannuation statements
- Share or investment portfolio statements

### Property-related documents

- Contract of sale for the property you are purchasing
- Evidence of deposit payment (bank transfer receipt)
- Building and pest inspection reports
- If refinancing: six months of statements for the loan being refinanced [Source: Canstar Home Loan Statistics, 2024]

## Red flags that can derail your application

Certain patterns and circumstances can cause lenders to decline your application or request additional information. Be aware of these common red flags:

### Gambling transactions

Regular gambling transactions on your bank statements, even small amounts, can concern lenders. They may view this as a risk to your ability to meet repayment obligations. If you gamble recreationally, consider reducing or stopping in the months before applying. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

### Undisclosed debts

If the lender discovers debts you did not declare, such as a credit card you forgot about or a Buy Now Pay Later account, this raises questions about your honesty and financial management. Pull your credit report before applying to ensure you have disclosed everything.

### Large unexplained deposits

Cash deposits that cannot be explained or traced may trigger anti-money laundering scrutiny. Lenders need to verify the source of all funds. If you receive a gift from family, obtain a signed gift letter stating the funds do not need to be repaid.

### Job changes

Starting a new job shortly before applying can be problematic, particularly if you move from a permanent role to a probationary period or casual employment. Some lenders require you to be out of probation before they will accept your income. If a job change is unavoidable, speak to your broker about which lenders have more flexible policies. [Source: Canstar Home Loan Statistics, 2024]

### Recent credit applications

Multiple credit enquiries in a short period suggest financial stress. Each enquiry remains on your credit file for five years. Avoid applying for credit cards, personal loans, or car finance in the six months before your home loan application.

### Overdrawn accounts and dishonours

Regular overdrawn fees, direct debit dishonours, or late payment fees on your bank statements signal poor cash flow management. Lenders review the last three to six months of statements carefully. Clean up your account behaviour well in advance. [Source: Reserve Bank of Australia, 2024]

## How a mortgage broker can help

A mortgage broker who specialises in first home buyers can guide you through the preparation phase, identify which lenders are most likely to approve your specific situation, and help you avoid common mistakes. Brokers have access to multiple lenders and products, including some that are not directly available to consumers. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart, 2024]

When choosing a broker, ask about their experience with first home buyers, how many lenders they have on their panel, and whether they charge a fee (most brokers are paid by the lender, but some charge additional fees for complex situations). A good broker will explain your options, not push you toward a particular lender or product.
