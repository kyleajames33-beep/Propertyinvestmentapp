# Strata title vs torrens title in NSW

When you buy property in NSW, the type of title determines what you own, what you control, and what ongoing obligations you take on. Torrens title and strata title are the two most common forms of property ownership in the state. Understanding the difference between them is essential before you make an offer or sign a contract.

This guide explains each title type, how they differ in practice, and what every buyer should know about strata scheme finances, rules, due diligence, and common issues.

---

## What is torrens title

Torrens title is the most straightforward form of property ownership in NSW. Under the Real Property Act 1900, you hold a registered title that gives you sole ownership of the land and any buildings on it, from boundary to boundary. [Source: NSW Land Registry Services]

### What you own

With torrens title, you own:

- the land itself, as defined by the survey plan
- all buildings, structures, and improvements on that land
- the airspace above and the subsoil below, subject to statutory limitations

### Typical property types

Torrens title applies to:

- freestanding houses
- some townhouses and dual occupancies on separate lots
- vacant land
- rural and semi-rural properties

### Key characteristics

- **Sole responsibility**: you are solely responsible for all maintenance, insurance, and repairs
- **No shared costs**: there are no strata levies or shared funds
- **Renovation freedom**: you can renovate, extend, or redevelop subject only to council approvals and planning controls
- **No collective decision-making**: you do not need anyone else's permission to make changes to your property

## What is strata title

Strata title applies to properties where ownership is split between individual lots and shared common property. It was introduced in NSW in 1961 and is now governed by the Strata Schemes Management Act 2015 and the Strata Schemes Development Act 2015. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 3; NSW Fair Trading]

### What you own

With strata title, you own:

- your individual lot (your apartment, townhouse, or villa), which typically includes the interior of the unit but not the external walls
- a share of the common property, proportional to your unit entitlement

### Typical property types

Strata title applies to:

- apartment buildings and unit blocks
- townhouse complexes and villa estates
- some dual occupancies and duplexes
- mixed-use buildings with residential and commercial lots

### The strata scheme structure

A strata scheme is the legal framework that binds all lot owners together. When a strata plan is registered, an owners corporation is automatically created. That corporation is the body responsible for managing the scheme, maintaining common property, and enforcing by-laws. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 8]

Common property includes everything that is not part of an individual lot. This typically covers:

- external walls, roofs, and foundations
- driveways, gardens, lobbies, and stairwells
- lifts, pools, gyms, and shared facilities
- pipes, wiring, and ducts that serve more than one lot

## Key differences at a glance

| Aspect | Torrens title | Strata title |
|---|---|---|
| What you own | Land and building outright | Your lot plus share of common property |
| Maintenance responsibility | You pay for everything | Shared via levies; owners corporation handles common property |
| Ongoing fees | None (council rates and insurance only) | Quarterly strata levies |
| Renovation control | Council approval only | Council approval plus owners corporation consent for some work |
| Collective decisions | None required | Voting at general meetings required for major decisions |
| Typical property | Houses, some townhouses | Apartments, townhouses, villas |

## Strata scheme components

Understanding the key components of a strata scheme helps you navigate ownership, responsibilities, and decision-making.

### Lots

A lot is the individual unit you own. It is defined on the registered strata plan. The boundaries of your lot matter because they determine what is yours to maintain and what falls to the owners corporation. Common property boundaries are usually shown on strata plans by thick black lines. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, Buying a strata property]

### Common property

Common property is everything on the parcel of land that is not part of a lot. The owners corporation has a statutory duty to properly maintain and keep common property in a state of good and serviceable repair under section 106 of the Strata Schemes Management Act 2015. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 106]

This duty exists regardless of whether the owners corporation has enough money in its funds. Financial constraints do not remove the obligation to maintain common property.

### Owners corporation

The owners corporation is the legal entity made up of all lot owners in the scheme. It is automatically created when the strata plan is registered. The owners corporation:

- maintains and repairs common property
- administers the finances of the scheme
- takes out building insurance and public liability insurance
- makes and enforces by-laws
- holds general meetings to make decisions

### Strata committee

The strata committee is elected by the owners corporation at each annual general meeting. It handles day-to-day decisions on behalf of the owners corporation. The committee must appoint office bearers: a chairperson, secretary, and treasurer. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, ss 29-41]

Committee members are volunteers. Decisions about major expenditure, changes to by-laws, and appointment of strata managers must go to a general meeting of the owners corporation.

### Strata manager

A strata manager (also called a strata managing agent) is a professional appointed by the owners corporation to handle administrative and operational tasks. In NSW, strata managers must hold a licence issued by NSW Fair Trading and complete ongoing professional development. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, Who's who in strata]

A strata manager's duties typically include:

- coordinating meetings and preparing agendas
- managing finances, budgets, and levy collection
- arranging insurance and maintenance
- maintaining records and handling correspondence
- ensuring compliance with legislation

### Building manager

A building manager (sometimes called a caretaker) looks after the physical common property. They handle tasks such as gardening, minor repairs, cleaning coordination, and general site safety. Unlike a strata manager, a building manager does not have authority over owners corporation funds or by-law enforcement. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, Who's who in strata]

## Strata levies

Strata levies are regular contributions paid by lot owners to fund the operation and maintenance of the strata scheme. They are calculated based on unit entitlement, which reflects the relative value of each lot. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, ss 73-89; NSW Fair Trading]

### Administrative fund

The administrative fund covers day-to-day operating expenses. These are regular, recurring costs that keep the building running smoothly. Common expenses include:

- cleaning of common areas
- gardening and landscaping
- minor repairs and routine maintenance
- electricity and water for shared areas
- building insurance and public liability insurance
- strata management fees
- fire safety inspections and compliance costs
- lift servicing

### Capital works fund

The capital works fund (formerly known as the sinking fund) is set aside for major repairs and long-term maintenance. It is designed to prevent large, unexpected costs from hitting owners all at once. Capital works fund expenses commonly include:

- external painting and facade repairs
- roof repairs or replacement
- lift refurbishment or replacement
- major plumbing or electrical upgrades
- waterproofing works
- renewal of shared fixtures and fittings

Every strata scheme in NSW must have a 10-year capital works fund plan. This plan must be reviewed at least every five years and helps the owners corporation forecast future expenditure and set levies accordingly. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 80]

### Special levies

A special levy is an additional contribution raised when the administrative fund or capital works fund does not have enough money to cover an unexpected or significant expense. Special levies may be needed for:

- urgent repairs or emergency works
- building defects discovered after inspection
- fire safety upgrades
- cost overruns on approved capital works
- legal proceedings

Special levies must be approved at a general meeting by ordinary resolution and can only be used for the purpose specified in the resolution. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 83]

### How levies are calculated

The total annual budget is divided among lot owners according to their unit entitlement, as shown on the registered strata plan. If the total budget is $200,000 and your lot has a unit entitlement of 120 out of a total scheme entitlement of 10,000, your annual contribution would be $2,400 (or $600 per quarter). [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 81]

### Payment and arrears

Levies are usually paid quarterly. Owners must receive at least 30 days written notice before payment is due. If a levy relates to urgent repairs affecting health or safety, at least 14 days notice is required. Unpaid levies can attract interest of up to 10% per annum (if approved by the owners corporation) and may lead to recovery action through the NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT). [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 85]

## Strata by-laws

By-laws are the rules that govern behaviour and use within a strata scheme. All owners, tenants, and visitors must comply with them. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, Part 7]

### What by-laws cover

By-laws commonly address:

- keeping of pets
- noise and nuisance
- parking and use of visitor spaces
- short-term rental accommodation
- use of common facilities such as pools and gyms
- rubbish disposal
- hanging laundry on balconies
- renovation and alteration approvals
- smoking

### How by-laws are enforced

The owners corporation can issue notices to comply with a by-law. If the breach continues, the owners corporation can apply to NCAT for an order requiring compliance. NCAT can also impose fines for breaches of by-laws. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 135]

### Changing by-laws

By-laws can be added, amended, or repealed by special resolution at a general meeting of the owners corporation. A special resolution requires that no more than one-quarter of votes cast are against the motion. Any change to by-laws must be registered with NSW Land Registry Services within six months to be enforceable. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 141]

### Pets in strata

Under NSW law, an owners corporation cannot prohibit a resident from keeping a pet. A by-law that bans all pets is not valid. However, by-laws may require owners to notify the owners corporation before getting a pet, and a pet can be objected to if it causes unreasonable interference with another resident's use and enjoyment of their lot. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, Pets in strata]

Owners corporations cannot charge a bond, fee, or insurance requirement for keeping a pet. Assistance animals cannot be banned from a strata scheme under any circumstances.

## Strata reports and due diligence

Before buying into a strata scheme, you should always obtain a strata report (also called a strata inspection or Section 182 inspection) and a Section 184 certificate. These documents reveal the financial health, governance, and maintenance history of the scheme. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, ss 182-184]

### Section 184 certificate

A Section 184 certificate (formerly Section 109) provides essential information about a lot within a strata scheme, including:

- names and contact details of strata committee members, the strata manager, and building manager
- current levies payable by owners
- any outstanding levies on the lot
- proposals for funding the 10-year capital works fund plan
- details of recently made by-laws
- whether a strata renewal committee has been established
- whether the scheme has an embedded network for utilities

If a levy is outstanding before the certificate is issued and the amount is not shown on the certificate, the purchaser is not responsible for paying it. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, Buying a strata property]

### What a strata search reveals

A comprehensive strata inspection (Section 182) reviews the owners corporation records and typically includes:

- financial statements and budgets from the last two years
- current balances of the administrative fund and capital works fund
- the 10-year capital works fund plan
- minutes from general meetings and strata committee meetings
- any outstanding or pending legal proceedings
- current insurance policies and building sum insured
- registered by-laws
- details of any special levies raised or proposed
- building maintenance records and defect history
- correspondence about disputes or compliance issues

### Why you need a strata report

A strata report functions like a building inspection for an apartment. It can reveal problems that are not visible during a property inspection, such as:

- underfunded capital works funds and deferred maintenance
- a history of special levies indicating poor financial planning
- ongoing building defects such as waterproofing failures or cladding issues
- disputes between owners or with the strata manager
- non-compliance with fire safety or other regulations

The cost of a strata report (typically $200 to $400) is a small investment compared to the risk of buying into a scheme with hidden financial or structural problems. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, Buying a strata property]

## Strata management: self-managed vs professional

Every owners corporation must decide whether to self-manage or appoint a professional strata manager.

### Self-managed strata

In a self-managed scheme, the strata committee handles all administrative, financial, and operational tasks. This includes managing records, collecting levies, paying invoices, organising meetings, coordinating repairs, and ensuring legislative compliance. [Source: NSW Fair Trading]

Self-management can work for small schemes with engaged owners who have the necessary time and expertise. The main advantage is lower costs because no management fees are paid. However, self-management carries significant risks:

- committee members are unpaid volunteers who may lack time or expertise
- complex legislation and compliance requirements are difficult to keep up with
- financial mismanagement can devalue the property
- disputes between neighbours can escalate without a neutral third party
- levy arrears can be awkward to pursue among neighbours
- mandatory Strata Hub reporting requirements add administrative burden

### Professional strata management

Most strata schemes in NSW appoint a licensed strata manager. Professional management provides:

- expert knowledge of the Strata Schemes Management Act 2015 and regulatory changes
- proper financial management, budgeting, and levy collection
- organised, compliant meetings
- access to networks of qualified contractors
- impartial dispute resolution
- compliance with insurance and safety requirements
- handling of Strata Hub annual reporting

Professional strata managers in NSW must hold a Certificate of Registration or a full licence from NSW Fair Trading, including a Certificate IV in Property Services, and must complete ongoing professional development. [Source: NSW Fair Trading]

### Making the choice

The decision depends on the size and complexity of the scheme, the skills and availability of owners, and the risk tolerance of the owners corporation. Large schemes, buildings with extensive facilities, and schemes with a history of disputes generally benefit from professional management.

## Buying strata: due diligence checklist

Use this checklist before committing to buy a strata property:

1. **Obtain a Section 184 certificate** to confirm levies, outstanding amounts, and scheme governance
2. **Arrange a strata records inspection** (Section 182) to review financial statements, meeting minutes, and maintenance history
3. **Check the 10-year capital works fund plan** to assess whether future maintenance is adequately funded
4. **Review the current administrative and capital works fund balances** against upcoming maintenance needs
5. **Read the registered by-laws** to ensure they suit your lifestyle, especially regarding pets, renovations, and short-term rental
6. **Inspect the building thoroughly** and consider a separate building inspection for your lot
7. **Check for any special levies** recently raised or proposed
8. **Look for evidence of building defects** in meeting minutes and correspondence, particularly waterproofing and cladding
9. **Verify insurance coverage** including building replacement value and public liability
10. **Check for any strata renewal proposals** or collective sale processes underway
11. **Understand the strata management arrangement** and any building manager agreements in place
12. **Review the unit entitlement** for your lot, as this determines your share of levies and voting power

## Common strata issues

### Special levies

Special levies are a leading source of financial stress for strata owners. They arise when the capital works fund is insufficient to cover major repairs. Schemes with low levies and underfunded sinking funds are at higher risk. Before buying, review the levy history and capital works plan to assess this risk. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 83]

### Building defects

Building defects are a significant issue in NSW strata, particularly in newer buildings. Common problems include:

- **Waterproofing failures**: leaking balconies, bathrooms, and roofs. Waterproofing membranes are typically common property, making the owners corporation responsible for repair. [Source: Strata Schemes Management Act 2015, s 106]
- **Cladding**: combustible external cladding has required costly remediation across many buildings
- **Structural defects**: cracking, movement, or foundation issues
- **Fire safety non-compliance**: inadequate fire doors, exits, or alarm systems

Under the Home Building Act 1989, major defects (structural elements and critical systems such as waterproofing and fire safety) carry a six-year statutory warranty period. Minor defects carry a two-year warranty. The Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020 also imposes a statutory duty of care on certain individuals involved in construction work. [Source: Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020; Home Building Act 1989]

### Disputes

Disputes in strata schemes commonly arise over noise, parking, pets, renovations, and levy payments. The NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT) has jurisdiction to hear and resolve strata disputes. Mediation through NSW Fair Trading is usually required before applying to NCAT. [Source: NSW Fair Trading]

### Pets

As noted above, blanket pet bans are no longer valid in NSW. However, owners corporations can set reasonable conditions around pet ownership. Always check the by-laws before buying if keeping a pet is important to you. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, Pets in strata]

## Community title and company title

Beyond torrens and strata title, buyers in NSW may encounter community title and company title arrangements.

### Community title

Community title is a hybrid between torrens title and strata title. It allows land to be subdivided into conventional lots with shared community property and facilities. Unlike strata, which involves vertical ownership (units above and below each other), community title typically applies to horizontal developments such as estate subdivisions with shared roads, parks, or drainage. [Source: Community Land Development Act 1989; Community Land Management Act 2021]

Community schemes can include:

- **Community schemes**: the top tier, covering the whole development
- **Precinct schemes**: a middle tier for larger staged developments
- **Neighbourhood schemes**: the lowest tier, covering smaller groups of lots

The Community Land Management Act 2021 replaced the 1989 Act and brought community title laws into closer alignment with strata legislation. Community associations perform a similar function to owners corporations, and community property is the equivalent of common property.

### Company title

Company title is an older form of property ownership that predates strata title. Under this structure, a company holds the legal title to the entire building and land. When you buy a company title unit, you purchase shares in that company, and the shares entitle you to exclusive occupation of a particular apartment. [Source: Revenue NSW, Owners of company title units]

Key features of company title:

- you do not hold a certificate of title for your unit; you hold shares
- transfer of shares usually requires board approval, which can restrict saleability
- governance is through the company's constitution and board of directors, not the Strata Schemes Management Act 2015
- company title units are typically valued lower than equivalent strata apartments due to the restrictions on sale and lending

Lenders are often more cautious with company title properties because there is no direct property title to mortgage. Some banks will not lend against company title at all, while others may require higher deposits. [Source: Revenue NSW]

## Summary: which title type suits you

**Torrens title may suit you if** you want full control over your property, plan to renovate or extend, prefer not to pay ongoing strata levies, and are comfortable with sole responsibility for maintenance and insurance.

**Strata title may suit you if** you want a lower entry price, prefer shared maintenance of common areas and facilities, value location and lifestyle (particularly in inner-city or coastal areas), and are comfortable with collective decision-making and compliance with by-laws.

Regardless of which title you choose, always conduct thorough due diligence. For strata purchases, a strata report and Section 184 certificate are essential tools for understanding the financial health and governance of the scheme you are buying into.
