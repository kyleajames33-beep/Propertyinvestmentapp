# Understanding your contract of sale as a new property investor

Buying an investment property involves contract considerations that go beyond those faced by owner-occupiers. As a new investor, you need to understand not just the standard terms of the Contract for Sale and Purchase of Land 2022 edition, but also how investment-specific issues such as existing tenancies, tax timing, trust structures, and body corporate obligations affect your position. This guide covers the key contract matters that every new investor in NSW should understand. The content is educational only. Always engage a licensed conveyancer and a qualified accountant to advise on your specific transaction.

## Investment-specific contract considerations

When you buy a property as an investment, your goal is to generate rental income and capital growth. The contract should support this goal by protecting your ability to earn income from day one and by minimising risks that could erode your returns. Several standard contract clauses take on additional significance when you are buying for investment purposes.

The settlement date is critical for investors because it determines when you start earning rent and when you become responsible for outgoings such as council rates, strata levies, and insurance. The contract should clearly specify how these outgoings are adjusted between the vendor and you at settlement. Your conveyancer will check that the adjustments are calculated correctly and that you are not paying for periods before settlement.

If the property is already tenanted, the contract must include a copy of the lease agreement and a transfer of tenancy. The tenant's bond should be transferred to you through the NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal. Your conveyancer will arrange this and ensure the rent ledger is up to date so you know exactly what rent is owing at settlement.

Tax considerations also influence contract terms. The date of settlement affects which financial year your stamp duty, loan costs, and other deductions fall into. Your accountant can advise on whether settling before or after the end of the financial year has advantages for your tax position. This is particularly relevant if you are claiming depreciation or immediate deductions for repairs and maintenance.

## Leaseback arrangements and existing tenancies

A leaseback arrangement occurs when the vendor agrees to lease the property back from you after settlement. This can be attractive if the vendor needs time to move out or if you want immediate rental income. However, leaseback clauses require careful drafting to protect your interests.

The leaseback terms should be set out in a separate residential tenancy agreement that complies with the Residential Tenancies Act 2010. Do not rely on a brief clause in the contract of sale. The lease should specify the rent, the term, the bond amount, and the responsibilities of both parties. Your conveyancer can draft or review the leaseback agreement to ensure it is enforceable.

Be cautious of informal occupancy arrangements where the vendor stays in the property after settlement without a formal lease. This can create legal complications if the vendor refuses to move out and may affect your insurance coverage. Always insist on a proper tenancy agreement, even for a short period.

If the property has an existing tenant, review the lease carefully before you commit. Check the rent amount against current market rates, the length of the remaining term, and any special conditions in the lease. A tenant paying below-market rent on a long-term lease reduces your yield. Look for clauses that allow rent increases, the process for lease renewal, and any tenant rights to purchase the property.

## Special conditions for investment properties

Special conditions in investment property contracts often address matters that do not arise in owner-occupier transactions. Vendors may include conditions about rent guarantees, where the vendor guarantees a minimum rental income for a period after settlement. While this sounds appealing, read the fine print carefully. Rent guarantees often require you to use a specific property manager or to maintain the property to a certain standard. If the guarantee is from a third party, check their financial capacity to honour it.

Some contracts include conditions about furniture or chattels remaining in the property. If you are buying a furnished investment property, the contract should include a full inventory with the condition and value of each item. Furniture packages offered by developers or vendors are often overpriced. Have the inventory reviewed and consider whether you could source equivalent items more cheaply yourself.

Special conditions may also address the vendor's obligations regarding outstanding repairs or maintenance. If the tenant has reported defects, the vendor should either rectify them before settlement or compensate you. Your conveyancer can negotiate a retention from the purchase price to cover the cost of repairs if the vendor has not completed them.

## Tax-related clauses and settlement timing

The timing of settlement can have significant tax consequences for investors. Stamp duty is payable on settlement and is a capital cost that forms part of your cost base for capital gains tax purposes. If you settle just before the end of the financial year, your stamp duty and other acquisition costs will be in that year's records, which may affect your tax planning.

If you are claiming interest deductions, the timing of your first loan drawdown affects when you can start claiming. Interest on loans used to acquire an investment property is generally deductible from the time the loan is used to purchase the property. Your accountant can advise on the optimal timing to maximise your deductions and ensure you comply with Australian Taxation Office requirements.

Some contracts include clauses about goods and services tax. Residential property sales are generally input-taxed, meaning GST does not apply. However, if you are buying a new residential property from a developer or a property that includes a commercial component, GST may be payable. The contract should clearly state whether the price is inclusive or exclusive of GST. Your accountant can review the GST treatment and advise on whether you are eligible for any GST concessions.

Land tax is another consideration. In NSW, land tax is assessed on the value of land you own as at 31 December each year. If you settle on a property close to the end of the calendar year, you may be assessed for land tax for that year even though you only owned the property for a short period. Your conveyancer will arrange a land tax certificate to check whether the vendor has paid all outstanding land tax, and your accountant can advise on your land tax obligations going forward.

## Trust purchase: contract naming and trustee requirements

Many investors choose to purchase property through a trust, such as a discretionary trust, unit trust, or self-managed superannuation fund. If you are buying through a trust, the contract must name the correct purchaser from the outset. Getting this wrong can be expensive to fix.

The purchaser on the contract should be the full legal name of the trustee, followed by the words "as trustee for" and the name of the trust. For example, "John Smith as trustee for the Smith Family Trust." If you are using a corporate trustee, the purchaser should be the company name followed by "as trustee for" and the trust name. Your conveyancer can confirm the correct wording.

If you sign the contract in your personal name and later decide to transfer the property into a trust, you will pay stamp duty twice on the transfer. This is a common and costly mistake for new investors. Decide on your ownership structure before you make an offer and ensure the contract reflects this.

The contract should also include a clause confirming that the trustee has the power to purchase property under the trust deed. Most standard contracts include this, but your conveyancer will verify that it is present and correctly worded. If you are borrowing through a trust, your lender will also require a copy of the trust deed and may have specific requirements for the contract and settlement.

## Body corporate disclosure requirements

If you are buying a unit, townhouse, or villa in a strata scheme, the contract must include body corporate disclosure documents. These documents give you critical information about the financial health of the scheme, upcoming maintenance, and any disputes that could affect your investment.

The disclosure documents should include the strata scheme's financial statements for the past two years, the current administrative and sinking fund balances, any special levies approved or proposed, the minutes of the last two annual general meetings, a copy of the insurance policy, and details of any outstanding legal claims or disputes. Your conveyancer will review these documents and flag any concerns.

Pay particular attention to the sinking fund balance. A healthy sinking fund indicates that the owners corporation has been saving for future maintenance. A low or negative balance suggests that special levies may be imposed soon, which would reduce your net rental yield. The minutes of recent meetings may reveal disputes about maintenance, noise complaints, or governance issues that could affect tenant satisfaction.

If the building is more than 10 years old, check whether a building defects inspection has been conducted. Major defects in cladding, waterproofing, or structural elements can lead to six-figure special levies. Your conveyancer can arrange for a strata inspection report if the disclosure documents are incomplete or raise concerns.

## Due diligence certificate requirements

Due diligence is the process of investigating the property before you commit to buying. While not a formal legal requirement in all transactions, many investor-focused contracts include a due diligence clause that gives you a period to conduct investigations and rescind if you are not satisfied.

A due diligence clause typically allows you to rescind the contract within a specified period if you are not satisfied with the results of your investigations. These investigations might include a building and pest inspection, a strata inspection, review of the lease documentation, verification of rental income, and confirmation of development approvals. Unlike a finance clause, a due diligence clause does not require you to specify what you found unsatisfactory. You can rescind for any reason within the due diligence period.

If the contract does not include a due diligence clause, your conveyancer can negotiate one. This is particularly important for new investors who may not have the experience to assess a property's condition and investment potential from a brief inspection. The cost of a due diligence clause is nil, but it can save you from a poor investment decision.

## Insurance obligations from exchange

Risk in the property passes to you on exchange of contracts, not settlement. This means that if the property is damaged by fire, storm, or other insured events between exchange and settlement, you are still obliged to complete the purchase. For this reason, you must arrange building insurance effective from the date of exchange.

If the property is tenanted, you should also arrange landlord insurance. Landlord insurance covers risks that standard building insurance does not, such as loss of rental income, tenant default, and damage caused by tenants. Some policies also cover legal expenses for tenancy disputes. Your insurance broker can advise on the appropriate coverage for your investment property.

If the property is in a strata scheme, the owners corporation arranges building insurance for the common property and the building structure. You should still arrange contents insurance for any fixtures, fittings, or furniture you own, and landlord insurance for the rental-related risks. Request a copy of the strata insurance policy at contract review to confirm the level of coverage and whether any exclusions apply.

## Building and pest report as contract conditions

A building and pest inspection is essential for any investment property purchase, even if the property appears to be in good condition. Structural defects, termite damage, and non-compliant work can cost tens of thousands of dollars to rectify and may make the property uninhabitable until repairs are completed.

Your contract should include a building and pest clause that makes the purchase conditional on you receiving a satisfactory report. The clause should specify the timeframe for obtaining the report, usually 14 days from exchange, and should allow you to rescind or negotiate a price reduction if significant defects are found.

When reviewing the report, pay attention to structural issues such as foundation cracks, roof defects, and water damage. These are expensive to repair and can affect the property's value. Termite damage is also a serious concern in many parts of NSW. Even if the report shows no current activity, check whether preventative treatment has been applied and whether the property is in a high-risk area.

If the property is a strata unit, the building inspection is less critical for the interior, as structural repairs are the owners corporation's responsibility. However, you should still inspect the condition of the lot itself and review the strata records for any building defects that affect the scheme as a whole.

## Conveyancer and accountant referral

Contract review for investment properties requires expertise beyond the standard residential transaction. A conveyancer with experience in investment properties can identify lease-related issues, negotiate appropriate special conditions, and ensure your trust or ownership structure is correctly reflected in the contract. A qualified accountant can advise on the tax implications of settlement timing, ownership structures, deductions, and GST treatment. Together, these professionals form the foundation of your investment advisory team.

If you do not yet have a conveyancer or accountant with property investment experience, you can request a referral to licensed professionals who work with investors. The cost of professional advice is modest compared to the value of avoiding a poorly structured contract or missed tax deduction.
