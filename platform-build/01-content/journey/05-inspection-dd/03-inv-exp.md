# Advanced Inspection and Due Diligence for Experienced Investors

Experienced investors approach due diligence with a focus on value creation, risk quantification, and portfolio alignment. This guide covers the advanced assessment techniques and specialist engagements that sophisticated investors use to gain an edge in competitive markets.

## Advanced Due Diligence: Development Approval Potential

For investors who acquire properties with a view to future development or redevelopment, assessing development potential is a core component of due diligence. This goes far beyond standard building inspection and requires a systematic analysis of planning controls, site constraints, and feasibility.

### Planning Controls Assessment

Begin by obtaining and analysing the full suite of planning instruments affecting the site:

**Section 10.7 certificates**: Order both the 10.7(2) and 10.7(5) certificates. The 10.7(2) certificate shows the zoning, minimum lot size, and whether the land is bushfire or flood prone. The 10.7(5) certificate provides additional information that council holds about the land, including hazardous conditions, coastal hazards, and mine subsidence.

**Local Environmental Plan (LEP)**: Review the zoning, floor space ratio (FSR), height limits, and heritage controls. Check whether the property is in a special character area, heritage conservation area, or subject to design excellence requirements. Note any development incentives such as affordable housing bonuses or transferrable development rights.

**Development Control Plan (DCP)**: The DCP contains detailed controls for setbacks, landscaping, parking, building articulation, and design quality. These controls can significantly affect what can be built and the cost of construction.

**State Environmental Planning Policies (SEPPs)**: Check whether any SEPPs apply. The Housing SEPP, Transport and Infrastructure SEPP, and Bushfire Protection SEPP commonly affect development feasibility in NSW.

### Site Analysis for Development

A development feasibility assessment should consider:

- **Site area and dimensions**: Check the survey for exact boundaries and area. Corner blocks, wide frontages, and deep blocks often offer better development potential.
- **Slope and topography**: Significant slopes increase construction costs and may affect design options.
- **Easements and restrictions**: Check whether any easements restrict building locations. Drainage easements in particular can limit usable site area.
- **Neighbouring development patterns**: Examine recent development approvals on comparable sites in the immediate area. This indicates what council is likely to approve.
- **Infrastructure capacity**: Contact the local council and utility providers to confirm water, sewer, and stormwater capacity for increased development.
- **Heritage constraints**: Heritage listings and conservation areas can severely restrict development options and increase costs.

### Feasibility and Timing

Experienced investors build detailed feasibility models before committing to development sites. Key inputs include:

- Current land value and estimated end value of developed product
- Construction costs per square metre (obtain quantity surveyor estimates)
- Holding costs during design, approval, and construction
- Consultant costs (architect, town planner, engineer, quantity surveyor)
- Council contributions and fees
- Contingency allowances (typically 10 to 15 per cent for established investors)
- Sales and marketing costs for sell-down

Development approvals in NSW can take 6 to 18 months depending on complexity and council. Factor this holding period into your feasibility and ensure you have appropriate finance facilities in place.

## Structural Assessment: When to Engage a Structural Engineer

A standard building inspection is a visual assessment only. For certain properties and circumstances, engaging a structural engineer provides essential additional assurance.

### When to Engage a Structural Engineer

Consider a structural assessment when:

- The building inspection identified signs of significant structural movement, including large cracks in walls or foundations, sagging roof structures, or out-of-level floors.
- The property has a history of foundation problems or has been underpinned.
- You plan structural renovations, including removing load-bearing walls or adding upper floors.
- The property is in an area known for reactive soils, mine subsidence, or coastal erosion.
- The building is heritage-listed or constructed using unusual methods or materials.
- You are purchasing a commercial or industrial property where the structural requirements differ from residential standards.
- The property has suffered damage from flood, fire, or storm events.

### What a Structural Engineer Provides

A structural engineer will:

- Inspect the foundation system, footings, and slab for adequacy and distress.
- Assess the structural frame, including walls, beams, columns, and roof structure.
- Identify any structural modifications and assess their adequacy.
- Provide an opinion on the cause and severity of any defects.
- Recommend remedial works with estimated scopes and, in some cases, indicative costs.
- Provide a formal report that can be used for negotiation, insurance, or future building approval purposes.

Structural engineer reports typically cost between $800 and $3,000 depending on the complexity of the property and the scope of assessment. For development sites or complex properties, this cost is a necessary investment in risk management.

### Cracking and Movement Classification

Structural engineers classify cracks according to severity. The commonly used crack width classification system helps quantify the problem:

- Hairline cracks (less than 0.1 mm): Generally not structurally significant.
- Fine cracks (0.1 to 1 mm): May indicate minor movement. Monitor and repair as needed.
- Moderate cracks (1 to 5 mm): Indicate measurable movement. Investigation and likely repair required.
- Wide cracks (5 to 15 mm): Significant structural distress. Remedial works required.
- Very wide cracks (15 to 25 mm): Major structural failure. Extensive remediation needed.
- Structural failure (greater than 25 mm): Severe structural damage. Major rebuilding may be required.

Understanding this classification helps you interpret both the building inspection report and any subsequent structural engineer's advice.

## Quantity Surveyor: Depreciation Maximisation Strategies

For experienced investors with substantial portfolios, tax depreciation represents a significant annual deduction. A strategic approach to depreciation maximisation requires specialist quantity surveyor advice beyond a standard schedule.

### Splitting Plant and Equipment

When acquiring a property that has been substantially renovated, a quantity surveyor can identify and value plant and equipment items that qualify for immediate or accelerated depreciation. Items such as carpet, blinds, hot water systems, and air conditioning units can often be written off more quickly than the building structure.

### The Low-Value Pool

The Australian Taxation Office allows low-cost assets (under $1,000) and low-value assets (written down to under $1,000) to be pooled and depreciated at an accelerated rate. A skilled quantity surveyor will identify all qualifying items and ensure they are allocated correctly.

### Scrapping Schedules

If you plan to demolish or remove existing plant and equipment as part of a renovation, a scrapping schedule identifies the residual value of items being disposed of. This residual value can be claimed as an immediate deduction in the year of removal. This is particularly valuable for properties undergoing significant renovation.

### New Construction and Substantial Renovation

For investors undertaking new construction or substantial renovation (where more than substantially all of the existing building is replaced), different depreciation rules apply. New residential properties attract full Division 43 capital works deductions over 40 years. Plant and equipment in new builds is also depreciable.

### Portfolio-Level Depreciation Strategy

Experienced investors should review their entire portfolio with a quantity surveyor periodically. Strategies include:

- Prioritising renovations in properties with the most depreciation potential.
- Timing scrapping claims to align with high-income years.
- Considering the interaction between depreciation and capital gains tax when selling.
- Reviewing grandfathered depreciation claims on properties purchased before the 2017 legislative changes.

Engage a quantity surveyor who understands investment property strategy and can advise across your portfolio rather than preparing isolated schedules for individual properties.

## Value-Add Opportunity Quantification

Experienced investors distinguish between properties that are cheap because they are poor investments and properties that are underpriced because their value has not yet been unlocked. Quantifying value-add opportunities requires systematic analysis.

### Common Value-Add Strategies

**Renovation and repositioning**: Calculate the cost of renovation against the expected increase in rental income and capital value. Use comparable renovated properties to establish the post-renovation value. A common rule of thumb is that every dollar spent on renovation should add at least two dollars in value, though this varies by market.

**Reconfiguration**: Converting a three-bedroom house to a four-bedroom, adding a study, or creating dual living spaces can significantly increase rental yield. Check council requirements for minimum room sizes, natural light, and parking.

**Granny flats and secondary dwellings**: NSW planning policy facilitates secondary dwellings on many residential blocks. A well-constructed granny flat can add substantial rental income. Check minimum lot sizes, setback requirements, and private open space requirements under the local planning controls.

**Strata subdivision**: Some properties can be subdivided into strata title, creating multiple saleable or rentable units. This requires careful feasibility analysis and professional advice.

**Development site aggregation**: Combining adjoining properties can create development opportunities not available to individual lots. This requires coordinated acquisition, joint venture structures, and detailed planning advice.

### Quantification Framework

For each value-add strategy, build a detailed financial model that includes:

- All costs: Construction, consultants, council fees, holding costs, and contingency.
- Expected revenue: Increased rent, increased sale price, or development profit.
- Timeline: From acquisition to completion and stabilisation.
- Risk factors: Approval risk, construction risk, market risk, and funding risk.
- Return metrics: Return on cost, return on equity, yield on cost, and internal rate of return.

## Portfolio-Level Risk: Concentration Assessment

Experienced investors with multiple properties must assess risk at the portfolio level, not just the individual property level.

### Concentration Risks to Monitor

**Geographic concentration**: Holding multiple properties in the same suburb or region exposes your portfolio to single-market downturns, infrastructure changes, or local economic shocks. Review your portfolio mapping periodically and consider diversification into different markets.

**Property type concentration**: Overweighting in one property type (for example, all high-rise apartments or all regional houses) creates vulnerability to sector-specific downturns. A balanced portfolio includes a mix of property types.

**Tenant profile concentration**: If all your properties target the same tenant demographic (for example, students or young professionals), a downturn in that segment affects your entire portfolio. Diversify across tenant profiles where possible.

**Financing concentration**: Multiple loans with the same lender create concentration risk if the lender changes policy or appetite. Spread your borrowing across lenders and review your loan structures regularly.

**Macroeconomic sensitivity**: Assess how your portfolio would perform under scenarios of rising interest rates, rising unemployment, population decline, or reduced immigration. Stress test your cash flow under adverse conditions.

### Risk Mitigation Strategies

- Maintain liquidity reserves across the portfolio, not just per property.
- Review insurance coverage for all properties annually and in response to natural disaster events.
- Consider cross-collateralisation risks and structure loans to maximise flexibility.
- Document portfolio performance metrics and review against benchmarks quarterly.

## Insurance Portfolio Review

Insurance for experienced investors goes beyond standard building and landlord policies. A portfolio-level insurance strategy ensures adequate coverage, minimises gaps, and manages cost.

### Types of Coverage to Review

**Building and replacement cover**: Ensure sum insured values reflect current rebuilding costs. Construction cost inflation in Australia has been significant, and underinsurance is a growing risk. Obtain periodic professional valuations for replacement cost.

**Landlord insurance**: Policies vary significantly. Review coverage for rent default, tenant damage, legal liability, and loss of rent due to insured damage. Check excess structures and whether they apply per claim or per policy period.

**Public liability**: Minimum cover of $20 million is standard for residential investment. Commercial properties may require higher limits depending on tenant use.

**Commercial property insurance**: For commercial or mixed-use holdings, insurance requirements are more complex. Business interruption, plate glass, machinery breakdown, and workers compensation may be relevant depending on lease structures.

**Flood and natural hazard coverage**: Review flood coverage carefully. Many insurers have reduced appetite for flood-prone areas. Some policies exclude flood or apply sub-limits. The Insurance Council of Australia publishes data on non-insurance and underinsurance rates by region.

**Title insurance**: Consider title insurance to protect against unknown title defects, encroachments, and fraud. This is particularly relevant for properties with complex histories or boundary uncertainties.

### Portfolio Insurance Strategy

- Consolidate policies with a single broker where appropriate to obtain portfolio discounts.
- Review all policies annually before renewal, not just at renewal.
- Document claims history across the portfolio as this affects premiums and coverage availability.
- Obtain catastrophe modelling for portfolios in high natural hazard areas.

## Exit Strategy Analysis: Who Will Buy This From You?

Experienced investors evaluate every purchase with the exit in mind. Understanding your likely buyer profile informs your hold period, improvement strategy, and timing.

### Buyer Segments to Consider

**Owner-occupiers**: Properties that appeal to owner-occupiers typically achieve higher prices than pure investor stock. Character homes, quality locations, and family-friendly features attract this segment. Your improvements should target owner-occupier preferences if this is your exit strategy.

**First home buyers**: Entry-level properties in growth corridors attract first home buyers. Stamp duty concessions and government grants influence demand in this segment. Ensure your property meets the eligibility criteria for relevant concessions.

**Investors**: Other investors look for yield, depreciation, and low maintenance. Properties with strong rental returns and minimal ongoing costs appeal to this segment. Keep detailed records of income and expenses to support the investment case.

**Developers**: Developer buyers pay based on development feasibility, not current use. They require development potential, appropriate zoning, and adequate site dimensions. Your hold strategy may involve securing or improving development rights before selling to this segment.

**Downsizers**: The growing downsizer market (empty nesters and retirees) values single-level living, low maintenance, proximity to services, and quality finishes. Apartments and townhouses in lifestyle locations appeal to this segment.

### Market Timing Considerations

Monitor local market conditions, including days on market, clearance rates, and stock levels. Plan your exit to coincide with favourable market conditions where possible, while recognising that market timing is imperfect.

## Due Diligence for Commercial or Mixed-Use Properties

Commercial and mixed-use properties require additional due diligence beyond standard residential inspection.

### Lease Analysis

The lease is the primary asset in a commercial property investment. Review:

- **Lease term and options**: Remaining lease term, tenant options to renew, and expiry profile.
- **Rent and reviews**: Current rent, review mechanisms (CPI, fixed, market), and review dates.
- **Outgoings recovery**: What outgoings the tenant pays and how they are recovered.
- **Bank guarantees and bonds**: Security held against tenant default.
- **Permitted use**: What the tenant can use the premises for.
- **Maintenance obligations**: Who is responsible for what maintenance and repairs.
- **Make good obligations**: Tenant obligations to restore the premises at lease end.

### Tenant Covenant Assessment

Evaluate the tenant's financial capacity to meet lease obligations. For small business tenants, review financial statements or obtain director guarantees. For larger tenants, consider credit ratings and trading history.

### Compliance and Regulatory Matters

- **Fire safety**: Commercial properties require annual fire safety statements.
- **Disability access**: The Disability Discrimination Act may require access improvements.
- **Environmental issues**: Former industrial sites may have contamination issues requiring assessment.
- **Council requirements**: Check zoning, parking requirements, and any change of use restrictions.
- **Work health and safety**: Landlords have obligations regarding premises safety.

### Commercial Building Inspection

Commercial building inspections assess the same elements as residential but apply different standards. Consider engaging an inspector with commercial property experience. Particular attention should be paid to:

- Loading capacity of floors and structures if relevant to tenant use.
- Adequacy of services (power, water, data) for the tenant's requirements.
- Condition of plant and equipment (air conditioning, lifts, fire systems).
- Compliance with the Building Code of Australia for the current use.
- Remaining useful life of major building elements and systems.

### Yield and Return Analysis

Commercial properties are valued primarily on income yield. Calculate:

- Net operating income (rental income less outgoings and vacancy allowance).
- Capitalisation rate by comparing net income to purchase price.
- Equivalent yield and internal rate of return over the investment horizon.
- Sensitivity of returns to vacancy, rent variation, and interest rate changes.

## Negotiation Leverage from Inspection Findings

Experienced investors use inspection findings not merely to decide whether to proceed, but as a structured negotiation tool.

### Building a Negotiation Dossier

Compile a professional dossier that includes:

1. The building inspection report with all major defects highlighted.
2. Pest inspection findings and treatment recommendations.
3. Structural engineer's report (if engaged).
4. Itemised quotes for rectification works from licensed tradespersons.
5. Photographs of significant defects.
6. Summary of the total estimated rectification cost and timeline.

### Negotiation Approaches

**Price reduction**: Request a reduction in purchase price equal to the estimated rectification cost plus a contingency margin (typically 15 to 25 per cent). This acknowledges that the buyer assumes the risk and responsibility of managing repairs.

**Vendor rectification**: Request that specific defects are repaired before settlement, with evidence of completion by licensed tradespersons. This approach works best for straightforward, well-defined repairs.

**Settlement adjustment**: Structure the price reduction as an adjustment at settlement rather than a contract variation. Your conveyancer can advise on the appropriate mechanism.

**Delayed settlement**: Request a longer settlement period to allow the vendor time to complete agreed works.

### Timing and Presentation

Present your negotiation position promptly after receiving inspection reports. Delays weaken your position and may cause the vendor to question your commitment. Be factual and unemotional in your communication. Refer to specific report findings and quote amounts rather than making general claims about the property's condition.

If the vendor rejects your request, be prepared to walk away. Experienced investors know that there are always other opportunities, and overpaying for a property with significant defects undermines long-term returns.

### Using Due Diligence Findings Beyond Price

Inspection findings can also be used to negotiate:

- **Deposit terms**: A reduced deposit or deposit bond in recognition of the risk you are assuming.
- **Due diligence extension**: Additional time to complete specialist assessments.
- **Inclusion of items**: Request that the vendor includes appliances, furnishings, or equipment that might otherwise be excluded.
- **Access arrangements**: Pre-settlement access for your tradespersons to prepare quotes and schedules.

Every finding from your due diligence process is potential leverage. The key is to present it professionally, support it with evidence, and be clear about what you are requesting and why.
