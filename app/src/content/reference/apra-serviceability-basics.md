# APRA Serviceability and Lending Basics

> Last updated: July 2025. Guide to how Australian lenders assess borrowing capacity and the regulatory framework governing mortgage lending. Educational content only, not financial advice.

---

## What is APRA?

The Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) is Australia's prudential regulator of banks, credit unions, building societies, general insurance and life insurance companies, and superannuation funds. APRA oversees lending standards to ensure the stability of Australia's financial system [Source: APRA, 2024].

APRA does not directly approve or decline individual loan applications. Instead, it sets the prudential standards and guidelines that banks and lenders must follow when assessing loan applications. These standards are expressed in Prudential Practice Guides (notably APG 223 Residential Mortgage Lending) and Information Papers [Source: APRA, APG 223, 2024].

## The serviceability buffer

### What is the serviceability buffer?

The serviceability buffer is the margin that lenders must add to the advertised interest rate when assessing whether a borrower can afford a loan. It is a stress test to ensure borrowers could still meet repayments if interest rates rise [Source: APRA, 2021].

### Current buffer rate

APRA has confirmed the serviceability buffer remains at **3 percentage points** above the advertised rate for all new mortgage lending [Source: APRA, July 2025].

This means:

| Advertised rate | Assessment rate (with buffer) |
|---|---|
| 5.00% | 8.00% |
| 5.50% | 8.50% |
| 6.00% | 9.00% |
| 6.50% | 9.50% |

[Source: APRA Information Paper, 2025]

### Minimum floor rate

In addition to the 3% buffer, lenders must also apply a minimum floor rate (also called a minimum assessment rate), typically around 7-8% depending on the lender. The borrower is assessed at the higher of [Source: APRA, APG 223]:

- The advertised rate plus 3 percentage points, OR
- The lender's minimum floor rate.

For example, if the advertised rate is 5% and the minimum floor rate is 7.5%, the assessment rate would be 8% (5% + 3% = 8%, which is higher than the 7.5% floor). If the advertised rate is 4% and the floor is 7.5%, the assessment rate would be 7.5% (4% + 3% = 7%, but the floor of 7.5% applies) [Source: APRA, APG 223].

### History of the buffer

| Period | Buffer rate |
|---|---|
| Pre-2019 | 2.00% |
| 2019 to October 2021 | 2.50% |
| October 2021 to present | 3.00% |

APRA increased the buffer to 3% in October 2021 during the COVID-era property boom to cool aggressive lending at historically low interest rates [Source: APRA, 2021].

### APRA's rationale for maintaining the buffer

APRA Chair John Lonsdale has stated that "high household debt is a key vulnerability in our financial system, which has more exposure to residential mortgages than any comparable country." APRA maintains the buffer to protect both borrowers and the financial system from future rate rises [Source: APRA, July 2025].

## How banks assess serviceability

### Income assessment

Lenders assess all forms of income including [Source: APRA APG 223; NCCP Act 2009]:

- Salary and wages (typically verified via payslips and PAYG summaries).
- Self-employment income (typically verified via two years of tax returns and financial statements).
- Rental income from investment properties (typically assessed at 70-80% of gross rental income to allow for vacancies and expenses).
- Investment income (dividends, interest).
- Government benefits (some may be accepted at reduced percentages).
- Child support and maintenance income (where applicable).

Lenders apply shading or discounting to non-salary income types to account for variability.

### Expense assessment

Lenders use three methods to verify living expenses [Source: NCCP Act 2009; Industry Practice]:

1. **Self-declared expenses**: The borrower completes a detailed living expense declaration.
2. **Bank statement verification**: Lenders review 3-6 months of bank statements to cross-check declared expenses against actual spending.
3. **HEMS benchmark**: The Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) is used as a minimum floor for living expenses.

The lender uses the higher of the HEMS benchmark or the borrower's actual/declared expenses [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

### HEMS benchmark

The Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) was developed by the Melbourne Institute of Applied Economics. It represents median household expenditure on "basic" items across different household types [Source: Melbourne Institute, 2011; Industry Practice].

HEM categories:

- **Absolute basics**: food, clothing, utilities, transport, communications.
- **Discretionary basics**: takeaway food, restaurants, childcare, entertainment (25% of spending included).
- **Non-basics**: luxury items (excluded from HEM).

HEM is assessed against household type (single, couple, with children) and income level. Approximately 70-80% of Australian lenders use HEM as a benchmark [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

Note: HEM does NOT include rent, mortgage repayments, private school fees, or gambling losses. These are assessed separately [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

### Existing commitments

Lenders factor in all existing debt commitments [Source: APRA APG 223]:

- Existing mortgage repayments (assessed at the higher of actual rate + buffer or floor rate).
- Credit card limits (typically assessed at 3% of the limit per month as a minimum repayment).
- Personal loans and car loans (full monthly repayment).
- Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL) commitments.
- HECS/HELP debt (some lenders factor this in, typically at 1-8% of income depending on earnings).

## Loan-to-value ratio (LVR) and LMI

### LVR explained

The Loan-to-Value Ratio (LVR) is the loan amount expressed as a percentage of the property value. For example, borrowing $480,000 to buy a $600,000 property gives an LVR of 80% [Source: Industry Practice].

### LMI thresholds

Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI) is generally required when the LVR exceeds 80%. LMI protects the lender (not the borrower) if the borrower defaults [Source: Industry Practice].

| LVR | Typical lender treatment |
|---|---|
| Up to 80% | Standard lending, no LMI |
| 80.01% to 90% | LMI required, potentially higher interest rate |
| 90.01% to 95% | LMI required, stricter criteria, some lenders may decline |
| Above 95% | Generally not available except under government schemes |

[Source: Industry Practice, 2025]

### High LVR lending caps

APRA does not currently impose a formal cap on the percentage of new loans that can be written above 90% LVR. However, APRA expects ADIs to exercise prudent restraint in high-LVR lending and monitors this through quarterly reporting [Source: APRA, APG 223, 2024].

Previously, APRA had a 10% speed limit on high-LVR lending, but this was removed. The current approach relies on lender discretion within prudential standards rather than hard caps [Source: APRA, 2024]. Note: Some secondary sources still reference the 10% cap, but this no longer applies as a formal regulatory limit.

## Genuine savings requirements

### What counts as genuine savings

When borrowing more than 80-85% of the property value, most lenders require evidence of "genuine savings" [Source: Industry Practice, 2025]:

| Savings type | Generally accepted? | Minimum holding period |
|---|---|---|
| Savings account deposits | Yes | 3 months |
| Term deposits | Yes | 3 months |
| Shares/managed funds | Yes | 3 months |
| First Home Super Saver contributions | Yes | N/A |
| Equity in existing property | Yes | N/A |
| Gift held in account | Sometimes | 3 months |
| Rental payment history | Some lenders | 12 months |

[Source: Industry Practice, 2025]

### What does NOT count as genuine savings

- First Home Owner Grant.
- Recent windfalls (gift not held for 3+ months).
- Proceeds from sale of non-property assets (for example, a car).
- Borrowed funds.
- Short-term savings held for less than 3 months [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

### Genuine savings amount

Most lenders want to see at least 5% of the purchase price held as genuine savings when the LVR exceeds 85%. The remaining deposit can come from other sources such as gifts or grants [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

## Interest-only lending restrictions

### Current status

APRA removed the formal 30% benchmark on interest-only lending in December 2018. Interest-only lending is now governed by general prudential standards rather than a specific quantitative cap [Source: APRA, 2018; APG 223, 2024].

### Historical context

| Period | Restriction |
|---|---|
| March 2017 to December 2018 | New interest-only lending capped at 30% of total new mortgage lending |
| December 2018 onwards | Case-by-case removal of benchmark, contingent on board assurances |

[Source: APRA, 2017; APRA, 2018]

During the benchmark period, APRA also expected lenders to place strict internal limits on interest-only lending at LVRs above 80% and to provide strong scrutiny for interest-only loans above 90% LVR [Source: APRA, 2017].

### Current practice

While the formal cap is gone, interest-only loans remain more heavily scrutinised. Most lenders [Source: Industry Practice, 2025]:

- Charge higher interest rates for interest-only loans (typically 0.2-0.5% premium).
- Limit interest-only terms (typically 5 years for owner-occupiers, 10 years for investors).
- Require stronger serviceability evidence.
- Apply lower LVR limits for interest-only approvals.

## Debt-to-income ratio considerations

### What is DTI?

The Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio is total debt divided by gross annual income. For example, a borrower with $600,000 in debt and $100,000 in income has a DTI of 6 [Source: Industry Practice].

### APRA's monitoring

APRA does not currently impose a formal DTI cap. However, APRA monitors DTI trends and has indicated it may introduce DTI-based restrictions if macro-financial risks rise significantly [Source: APRA, 2024].

APRA has noted it will "consider additional limits, including investor-specific limits, if we see macro-financial risks significantly rising or a deterioration in lending standards" [Source: APRA, 2024].

### Lender DTI guidelines

Most Australian lenders apply internal DTI guidelines [Source: Industry Practice, 2025]:

| DTI ratio | Typical lender treatment |
|---|---|
| Below 6 | Generally acceptable |
| 6 to 7 | Requires additional scrutiny |
| Above 7 | Often declined or requires exceptions |
| Above 9 | Very rarely approved |

Note: These are internal lender guidelines, not APRA-mandated limits. Individual lender policies vary.

## Net income surplus

Lenders calculate net income surplus (also called Uncommitted Monthly Income or UMI) by subtracting living expenses and debt commitments from net income. A positive surplus is required for loan approval [Source: Industry Practice].

The minimum surplus varies by lender but typically ranges from $0 to a few hundred dollars per month. Some lenders apply higher minimums for higher-risk borrowers or higher loan amounts [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

## How pre-approval works

### What pre-approval means

Pre-approval (also called conditional approval or approval in principle) is a lender's preliminary assessment that you would likely be approved for a loan up to a specified amount, subject to certain conditions being met [Source: Industry Practice].

Pre-approval is NOT a guarantee of final approval. Common conditions include [Source: Industry Practice, 2025]:

- Satisfactory valuation of the property.
- No material change in the borrower's financial circumstances.
- Verification of all documentation.
- The property being acceptable security to the lender.

### Pre-approval validity

| Lender type | Typical validity period |
|---|---|
| Most lenders | 3 to 6 months |
| Some online lenders | 60 days (renewable once) |
| Major banks | Typically 90 days |

[Source: Industry Practice, 2025]

### What pre-approval is NOT

- It is not a binding commitment to lend.
- It does not include a property valuation.
- It does not guarantee the interest rate will remain the same.
- It is subject to the lender's credit policy at the time of final approval [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

## Common reasons finance falls through at the last minute

### Changes in borrower's circumstances

- Change of employment (new job, probation period, reduced hours).
- Taking on new debt (credit card, car loan, personal loan) after pre-approval.
- Missing credit card or loan repayments.
- Spending deposit savings.
- Change in income (bonus not received, reduced overtime).
- Divorce or separation affecting joint applications [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

### Property-related issues

- Valuation comes in lower than purchase price (LVR increases).
- Property deemed unacceptable security (for example, small apartments, hobby farms, certain postcodes).
- Issues with title or zoning.
- Building defects discovered in strata reports.
- Unapproved structures or renovations [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

### Lender policy changes

- Lender changes credit policy between pre-approval and final approval.
- Interest rate changes affecting serviceability.
- Lender withdraws certain products.
- APRA introduces new macroprudential measures [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

### Documentation issues

- Inability to verify income (particularly for self-employed borrowers).
- Incomplete or inconsistent documentation.
- Discovery of undisclosed debts or liabilities.
- Issues with the contract of sale [Source: Industry Practice, 2025].

## Conflicting information noted

- **High LVR lending cap**: Several sources reference a "10% cap on loans above 90% LVR" for first home buyers. APRA removed this formal speed limit. Some lenders may maintain internal policies, but there is no regulatory 10% cap. Current guidance relies on prudential discretion [Source: APRA, 2018; APRA, 2024].
- **Interest-only restrictions**: Some sources suggest interest-only lending is "restricted to 30% of new lending." This benchmark was removed in December 2018. Current restrictions are lender-imposed, not APRA-mandated [Source: APRA, 2018].
- **Genuine savings period**: Most sources cite 3 months. Some lenders may require 6 months for certain products. The 3-month figure is the most commonly applied standard.
- **HEMS usage**: Some sources claim HEMS is used by "all" lenders. Industry estimates suggest 70-80% of lenders use HEMS, with some using alternative benchmarks or their own calculations.

## 2024/2025 updates confirmed

- APRA confirmed the 3% serviceability buffer will remain unchanged in 2025 [Source: APRA, July 2025].
- Home Guarantee Scheme expanded from 1 October 2025: uncapped places, no income caps, higher property price caps [Source: Housing Australia, 2025].
- NSW price cap under Home Guarantee Scheme increased to $1,500,000 (Sydney/regional centres) and $800,000 (rest of NSW) from 1 October 2025 [Source: Housing Australia, 2025].
- APRA monitoring DTI trends and has flagged potential for investor-specific limits if credit growth accelerates [Source: APRA, 2024].
- Interest-only lending remains uncapped at the regulatory level but subject to lender discretion and higher pricing [Source: APRA, APG 223, 2024].
