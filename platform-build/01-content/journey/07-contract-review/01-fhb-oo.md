# Understanding your contract of sale as a first home buyer

The contract of sale is the legal document that sets out the terms and conditions for buying a property. For first home buyers in New South Wales, understanding what is in this document and what to look for can save you from costly mistakes. This guide explains the key parts of the Contract for Sale and Purchase of Land 2022 edition, the standard contract used in most NSW residential transactions. The content here is for educational purposes. You should always engage a licensed conveyancer or solicitor to review your specific contract before you sign.

## What is the Contract for Sale and Purchase of Land 2022 edition

The Contract for Sale and Purchase of Land 2022 edition is the standard form contract published by the Law Society of New South Wales and the Real Estate Institute of NSW. Most residential property sales in NSW use this form, though the vendor or their agent may attach special conditions that change the standard terms. The 2022 edition replaced the 2019 edition and introduced several changes that buyers should be aware of, including updated clauses on electronic execution, deposit handling, and risk.

The contract contains two main parts: the standard conditions, which are the pre-printed terms that apply to every sale unless changed, and the special conditions, which the vendor or agent adds to modify the standard terms. Your conveyancer will check both parts and flag any conditions that put you at a disadvantage. Never assume that the standard form is fair or balanced. Special conditions can significantly shift risk from the vendor to the buyer, and first home buyers are often the least equipped to spot these changes.

## Standard clauses every first home buyer should understand

The standard contract runs to over 30 clauses, but several are particularly important for first home buyers to understand. Clause 1 deals with the property and includes the land, buildings, fixtures, and any exclusions the vendor has listed. Fixtures are items attached to the property, such as built-in wardrobes, ovens, and ceiling fans. The vendor may exclude certain items, so check the schedule carefully to ensure the dishwasher, garden shed, or other fixtures you expect to remain are not listed as exclusions.

Clause 2 covers the purchase price and deposit. In NSW, the standard deposit is 10 percent of the purchase price, though vendors sometimes accept 5 percent by negotiation. The deposit is held in the real estate agent's trust account or by the vendor's solicitor until settlement. Clause 3 addresses the balance of the purchase price, which you pay at settlement after your deposit and any government grants or loan funds are applied.

Clause 4 deals with the title and any encumbrances. This clause requires the vendor to give you clear title at settlement, subject to any encumbrances disclosed in the contract. Encumbrances might include easements, covenants, or restrictions on use. An easement gives someone else the right to use part of the land, such as a shared driveway or utility access. A restrictive covenant limits what you can do with the property, such as a ban on multi-storey buildings or certain building materials.

Clause 5 covers the vendor's warranties. The vendor warrants that the details in the contract are true, that they have the right to sell the property, and that any structures have been built with proper approval. Clause 8 deals with risk, which passes to you on exchange of contracts unless the property is damaged before then. This means if the house burns down between exchange and settlement, you still have to complete the purchase, though you would normally claim on insurance.

## Special conditions: what to look for and what to negotiate

Special conditions are additional clauses added to the standard contract. They are not standard and can vary significantly between transactions. Vendors and their agents use special conditions to protect their interests, and first home buyers often sign without understanding the implications.

Look carefully for any special condition that tries to shorten or remove the cooling off period. In NSW, residential buyers have a five business day cooling off period after exchange, during which you can rescind the contract by paying 0.25 percent of the purchase price. Some vendors add special conditions that waive this right or require you to sign a section 66W certificate, which gives up your cooling off rights entirely. Your conveyancer can advise whether waiving the cooling off period is appropriate in your situation.

Another common special condition requires the buyer to accept the property in its existing condition. This might seem harmless, but it can limit your ability to claim if you discover defects after exchange. Some special conditions also impose penalty interest if you delay settlement, even for reasons beyond your control. Check whether the penalty rate is reasonable and whether the condition gives you any grace period.

Special conditions may also restrict your ability to make claims for misdescription or errors in the contract documents. The standard contract allows you to rescind if the property is substantially different from what was described. Some special conditions narrow this right or require you to accept minor discrepancies without remedy. Your conveyancer can identify these conditions and suggest amendments.

If you are buying at auction, there is no cooling off period. The contract is binding on the fall of the hammer. Have your conveyancer review the contract before the auction date and raise any concerns. You cannot negotiate terms after you have bid.

## Section 10.7 certificate: what it tells you about the property

A section 10.7 certificate, formerly known as a section 149 certificate, is issued by the local council under the Environmental Planning and Assessment Act 1979. It provides information about the planning controls and restrictions that apply to the property. Every contract of sale in NSW must include a section 10.7 certificate, and understanding what it says is essential before you commit to buying.

The certificate has two parts. Part 10.7(2) is mandatory and covers the zoning of the property under the relevant environmental planning instrument, whether the land is affected by any planning policies or proposed changes, and whether the council has any information about hazardous materials, bushfire risk, or coastal erosion on the land. Part 10.7(5) is optional but usually included. It provides additional information such as building height restrictions, floor space ratios, heritage listings, and whether any development contributions are owing.

Pay close attention to the zoning. If the property is zoned residential, check whether it is R1, R2, R3, or another classification, as this affects what you can build and how the property can be used. Look for any notation about proposed rezoning or draft planning instruments. A property that suits your needs today may be affected by a future road widening, density increase, or infrastructure project.

Check for bushfire zoning, especially if you are buying on the urban fringe or in regional NSW. Properties in bushfire-prone areas may have additional building requirements and higher insurance costs. Flood zoning is equally important. A property in a flood planning area may be subject to development restrictions and may be difficult or expensive to insure.

Your conveyancer will review the section 10.7 certificate and explain any implications. If you have concerns about future development or risks, you can also speak directly with the council's planning department. The certificate is current only as at the date of issue, so check whether it is recent. Councils update planning information regularly, and an older certificate may not reflect current zoning or proposed changes.

## Strata bylaws and special bylaws for apartments

If you are buying an apartment or townhouse in a strata scheme, the contract must include a copy of the bylaws and any special bylaws that apply to the scheme. The Strata Schemes Management Act 2015 sets out the standard bylaws, but these can be changed by special resolution of the owners corporation. Understanding the bylaws before you buy will help you avoid surprises about what you can and cannot do in your new home.

Standard bylaws cover matters such as noise, parking, the keeping of animals, and the use of common property. A common issue for first home buyers is the pets bylaw. Some schemes prohibit pets entirely, while others require approval from the owners corporation. If you have a dog or cat, check the bylaws carefully and consider whether the restrictions suit your situation.

Special bylaws are additional rules that a particular scheme has adopted. These might restrict short-term letting, specify the colour of window coverings visible from the street, prohibit the installation of air conditioning units on balconies, or impose rules about floor coverings to reduce noise transmission. Read every special bylaw and consider how it affects your intended use of the property.

The contract should also include a strata inspection report, which reveals the financial health of the owners corporation, any special levies planned, and the maintenance history of the building. If the report shows significant building defects, pending litigation, or underfunded capital works, these are warning signs. Your conveyancer can arrange a strata inspection report if one is not included in the contract.

## Building compliance certificates

Building compliance certificates confirm that structures on the property were built with proper approval and comply with the Building Code of Australia. The contract should include certificates for any recent renovations, extensions, or new structures. Common certificates include occupation certificates, compliance certificates from private certifiers, and section 81A certificates from the council.

If the vendor has added a deck, granny flat, or major renovation without council approval, this is a warning sign. Unapproved structures may not comply with building standards, may be unsafe, and may require you to obtain retrospective approval or even demolish the work. Your conveyancer will check whether all structures on the property have proper approval and will flag any issues.

Swimming pools are a particular concern. In NSW, all swimming pools must be registered on the NSW Swimming Pool Register and must have a valid certificate of compliance or occupation certificate less than three years old. If you are buying a property with a pool, ensure the certificate is current. If it is not, you may be responsible for bringing the pool fence and barriers up to standard after settlement.

## Finance clause: timing and what happens if finance falls through

Most first home buyers include a finance clause in the contract, also known as a subject to finance condition. This clause makes the contract conditional on you obtaining formal loan approval from your lender within a specified period, usually 14 to 21 days from exchange. If you cannot obtain finance, you can rescind the contract and have your deposit refunded in full.

The exact wording of the finance clause matters. Some clauses require you to use "best endeavours" or "reasonable endeavours" to obtain finance, which creates an obligation to actively pursue a loan. Others allow you to rescind simply by giving notice that finance was not approved. Your conveyancer and mortgage broker can explain the difference and negotiate wording that protects your interests.

Be aware that pre-approval is not the same as formal approval. Pre-approval is an indication of what a lender may be willing to lend based on initial checks. Formal approval requires a full valuation of the property and verification of your financial documents. Do not exchange contracts relying solely on pre-approval. Wait for formal written approval from your lender before you commit.

If your finance falls through, act quickly. Notify the vendor or their agent in writing within the timeframe specified in the clause. Your conveyancer will handle this for you. If you miss the deadline, the condition may lapse and you could be bound to complete the purchase regardless of whether you have the funds.

## Cooling off period: your rights and the penalty

In NSW, residential property buyers have a cooling off period of five business days starting from the date of exchange. During this period, you can rescind the contract for any reason. You do not need to explain why. The vendor keeps 0.25 percent of the purchase price as a penalty, and the balance of your deposit is refunded to you.

The cooling off period is a valuable protection for first home buyers, but it has limitations. It does not apply to properties bought at auction. It also does not apply if you sign a section 66W certificate, which waives the cooling off period. Vendors sometimes pressure buyers to waive the cooling off period to secure a quick sale. Your conveyancer can advise whether this is appropriate in your circumstances. In most cases, retaining the cooling off period is in your best interests.

To rescind during the cooling off period, you must give written notice to the vendor or their agent before 5pm on the fifth business day. Your conveyancer can prepare and serve this notice for you. Do not delay if you have doubts. Missing the deadline by even a few minutes can make the contract binding.

Note that the cooling off period is not a substitute for proper due diligence. You should have your conveyancer review the contract, order building and pest reports, and confirm your finance before you exchange contracts wherever possible. Relying on the cooling off period to do your research is risky and may cost you the 0.25 percent penalty if you decide not to proceed.

## Exchange versus settlement: the difference

Exchange and settlement are two separate events in a property transaction, and first home buyers often confuse them. Exchange is the point at which the signed contracts are swapped between the buyer and the vendor, making the agreement legally binding. Settlement is the point at which the balance of the purchase price is paid, title is transferred, and you receive the keys.

Exchange can happen in person or electronically through the PEXA platform. At exchange, you pay the deposit and the contracts are dated. The contract will specify a settlement period, typically 42 days from exchange, though this can be negotiated. During the period between exchange and settlement, your conveyancer arranges for your lender to prepare the loan documents, conducts final searches, and prepares for the transfer of title.

At settlement, your lender pays the balance of the purchase price to the vendor, and the title is transferred into your name. If you are a first home buyer eligible for stamp duty exemptions or concessions, your conveyancer will arrange for these to be applied at settlement. After settlement, the change of ownership is registered at NSW Land Registry Services.

Understanding the difference matters because risk passes to you at exchange, not settlement. If the property is damaged between exchange and settlement, you are still obliged to complete the purchase. For this reason, you should arrange building insurance from the date of exchange, even though you do not yet own the property. Your conveyancer can confirm the exact timing and what insurance you need.

## What your conveyancer does during this stage

Your conveyancer or solicitor is your most important adviser during the contract review stage. Their role is to protect your interests by reviewing the contract, identifying risks, and ensuring you understand what you are signing. A good conveyancer will explain the contract in plain language, answer your questions, and negotiate changes to the contract on your behalf.

During contract review, your conveyancer will read every page of the contract, including all special conditions, certificates, and attached documents. They will check the section 10.7 certificate for zoning issues, review the strata reports if you are buying an apartment, verify that building approvals are in place, and ensure the title is clear of unexpected encumbrances. They will also liaise with your lender to confirm the finance clause is properly drafted.

If your conveyancer identifies problems, they will advise you on your options. These might include asking the vendor to amend a special condition, rescinding the contract during the cooling off period, or negotiating a price reduction to account for a defect. Your conveyancer cannot make the decision for you, but they will give you the information you need to make an informed choice.

The cost of conveyancing for a standard residential purchase in NSW typically ranges from $1,200 to $2,500 plus disbursements. Disbursements are out-of-pocket costs for searches, certificates, and settlement fees. While it may be tempting to choose the cheapest quote, the quality of advice varies significantly. Look for a conveyancer who is a member of the Australian Institute of Conveyancers and who has experience with first home buyers.

## Common contract traps for first home buyers

First home buyers are particularly vulnerable to contract traps because they have less experience with property transactions and may be under pressure to act quickly in a competitive market. Here are the most common traps to watch for.

**Compressed timeframes.** Some contracts include short finance clauses or require settlement within 21 days. These timeframes may be unrealistic if your lender needs longer to process your application. Your conveyancer can negotiate an extension before you sign.

**Unfavourable special conditions.** Vendors sometimes add conditions that limit your rights to rescind for misdescription, require you to accept the property with all faults, or impose excessive penalty interest for delayed settlement. Read every special condition and ask your conveyancer to explain any that you do not understand.

**Inadequate disclosure.** The vendor must disclose certain information in the contract, including any known defects, zoning issues, and owners corporation disputes. If the disclosure is incomplete or misleading, you may have grounds to rescind, but proving this can be difficult and costly.

** strata levies and special contributions.** If you are buying an apartment, check the strata records for any special levies that have been approved but not yet paid. You may be liable for these after settlement. Your conveyancer can negotiate for the vendor to pay any outstanding contributions up to the date of settlement.

**Purchasing off the plan.** Off-the-plan contracts are complex and heavily weighted in favour of the developer. Sunset clauses, changes to plans, and delayed construction are common issues. If you are considering an off-the-plan purchase, seek specialised advice beyond standard conveyancing.

**Verbal promises.** Anything the agent or vendor promises you that is not written into the contract is not enforceable. If the agent says they will fix the fence, include a special condition requiring this. Do not rely on verbal assurances.

## Conveyancer referral

Contract review is the most critical stage at which to have professional advice. A licensed conveyancer or solicitor will review your contract before you sign, identify risks you might miss, and negotiate terms that protect your interests. If you do not yet have a conveyancer, you can request a referral to a licensed professional in your area. The cost of a contract review is modest compared to the financial commitment you are about to make, and good advice at this stage can save you significant problems later.
