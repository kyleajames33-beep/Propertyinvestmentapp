# Offer & Negotiation Checklist: First Home Buyer (Owner-Occupier)

## Comparable sales research
- [ ] Pull at least 5 comparable sales from the last 6 months in the same suburb with similar size and condition
- [ ] Adjust for differences: land size, bedrooms, condition, parking, and proximity to amenities
- [ ] Check whether the market is rising, stable, or falling: this affects how much you can negotiate
- [ ] Note how long the property has been on the market: stale listings often have more flexible vendors
- [ ] Research the vendor's situation if possible: deceased estate, divorce, investor offload, or developer

## Offer strategy
- [ ] Set your walk-away price before you make your first offer: write it down and stick to it
- [ ] Decide your opening offer: typically 5-10% below your maximum if negotiating
- [ ] Determine your increment strategy: how much you will increase by and how many times
- [ ] Prepare a written offer including price, deposit amount, settlement period, and any conditions
- [ ] Include a finance clause unless you have unconditional approval: protect yourself
- [ ] Include a building and pest inspection clause even if you have already done one: it gives you leverage
- [ ] Set an expiry time on your offer: 24 to 48 hours creates urgency

## Private treaty negotiation
- [ ] Submit your offer in writing via email to the selling agent, not just verbally
- [ ] Include your pre-approval letter to show you are a serious, finance-ready buyer
- [ ] Be prepared for the agent to push back: hold your second offer in reserve
- [ ] Do not reveal your maximum price to the agent under any circumstances
- [ ] If the vendor counters, take at least 4 hours to respond: do not negotiate in real time
- [ ] Ask the agent to justify their price with recent comparable sales, not just their opinion
- [ ] Be willing to walk away: the best negotiating tool is a genuine willingness to lose the deal

## Auction preparation
- [ ] Attend at least 3 auctions in the area before bidding to understand the rhythm and psychology
- [ ] Register as a bidder before auction day: check NSW's registration requirements
- [ ] Arrange pre-approval and have your deposit ready: auctions are unconditional
- [ ] Set your maximum bid and write it on a card in your pocket: do not exceed it
- [ ] Decide on your bidding strategy: open strong, wait and observe, or incremental increases
- [ ] Arrange for someone to bid on your behalf if you are uncomfortable: a friend, family member, or buyer's agent
- [ ] Inspect the contract and have your conveyancer review it at least 3 days before the auction
- [ ] If the property is passed in, be ready to negotiate immediately with the highest bidder's right

## Cooling off and contract conditions
- [ ] Understand NSW's cooling off period: typically 2-5 business days after exchange
- [ ] Note that cooling off does not apply to auctions in most states: except for the highest bidder if passed in
- [ ] Calculate the cooling off penalty if you withdraw: usually 0.25% of the purchase price
- [ ] Ensure your deposit cheque or transfer is ready before you sign: typically 10% of the purchase price
- [ ] Confirm the settlement period: 42 days is standard but you can negotiate 30, 60, or 90 days
- [ ] Keep a copy of the signed contract and the agent's statement of adjustments

## Emotional management
- [ ] Do not inspect properties above your budget: it distorts your sense of value
- [ ] Bring a rational second person to every negotiation or auction
- [ ] If you lose a property, wait 48 hours before pursuing another: avoid panic buying
- [ ] Remember: there is always another property. FOMO is the most expensive emotion in real estate
