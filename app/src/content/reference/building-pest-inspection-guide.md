# Building and pest inspection guide for NSW buyers

A pre-purchase building and pest inspection is one of the most important steps in the property buying process. In NSW, where property prices are high and building stock varies from heritage terraces to modern apartments, understanding exactly what you are buying can save tens of thousands of dollars in unexpected repairs. This guide explains what inspections cover, how to choose an inspector, how to read the report, and what to do when defects are found.

---

## What is a building inspection

A pre-purchase building inspection is a visual assessment of a residential property's condition conducted before you commit to buying it. The inspection follows Australian Standard AS 4349.1-2007, which sets out minimum requirements for the inspection and reporting process [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007].

### Scope of a building inspection

Under AS 4349.1, the inspector examines the property's accessible areas to identify major defects, significant defects, and safety hazards. The inspection covers:

- **Structural elements**: foundations, load-bearing walls, beams, columns, roof and floor framing
- **Roof exterior**: roof covering, flashings, gutters, downpipes
- **Roof space**: roof structure, insulation, sarking (where access is available)
- **External walls**: brickwork, cladding, render, damp-proof courses
- **Subfloor**: bearers, joists, stumps, ventilation, drainage (where accessible)
- **Interior**: walls, ceilings, floors, windows, doors, wet areas
- **External areas**: driveways, paths, retaining walls, steps, balconies
- **Outbuildings**: garages, carports, sheds

The inspector performs a visual assessment and may conduct functional testing of doors, windows, taps, shower recesses, and tiled surfaces [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007].

### What is not covered

A standard building inspection has important limitations. The following items are excluded:

- **Plumbing systems**: water supply, sewerage, and drainage beyond visual checks
- **Electrical systems**: wiring, switchboards, and safety switches (a separate electrical inspection by a licensed electrician is recommended)
- **Household appliances**: ovens, air conditioners, hot water systems
- **Swimming pools and spas**: compliance with pool safety laws
- **Smoke alarms**: compliance with current legislation
- **Pest activity**: termites, borers, and wood rot (these require a separate pest inspection under AS 4349.3)
- **Asbestos identification**: while the inspector may note suspect materials, laboratory testing is required for confirmation
- **Compliance with building regulations**: the inspection does not certify compliance with the National Construction Code

The standard explicitly states that an estimate of rectification costs is not required, though some inspectors may provide indicative figures as a guide only [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007].

## What is a pest inspection

A timber pest inspection assesses a property for damage caused by timber-destroying organisms. It is conducted under Australian Standard AS 4349.3-2010 [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.3-2010].

### What pests are covered

The standard inspection targets three categories of timber pests:

1. **Subterranean termites**: the most destructive termite species in Australia, which nest in soil and travel to timber structures via mud tubes. They can cause catastrophic structural damage if undetected.
2. **Dampwood termites**: attracted to moisture-damaged timber, these termites are less common but still significant in poorly ventilated spaces.
3. **Borers of seasoned timber**: wood-boring insects such as furniture beetles and powderpost beetles that tunnel through structural and decorative timbers.
4. **Wood decay fungi**: fungal rot that breaks down timber fibres, weakening structural members over time.

### What is not covered

AS 4349.3 does not require inspection for drywood termites or mould. Drywood termites are excluded due to the extreme difficulty in locating their small colonies and their rare incidence in Australia [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.3-2010]. Chemical delignification and underground inspection of elements like house stumps below ground level are also outside the scope.

The inspection is visual and non-invasive, meaning the inspector cannot cut into walls, lift floor coverings, or move heavy furniture. Areas that are concealed or obstructed cannot be inspected [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.3-2010].

## When to get inspections

### Pre-purchase timing

The property inspection should be undertaken as early in the buying process as practicable. This way, the purchaser will know about the condition of the property and will be able to make a better informed decision as to whether to proceed with the purchase [Source: NSW Fair Trading]. There is little point spending money on conveyancing until you know the condition of the property.

Most inspection consultants need a minimum of 2 to 3 days' notice to conduct a building inspection. You should get the vendor's permission to have the property inspected as early in the sale negotiations as possible.

### Cooling off period strategy

When you buy a property in NSW, there is a 5 business day cooling-off period after you have exchanged contracts. During this period, you may get out of the sale as long as you give written notice to the seller's real estate agent, lawyer, or conveyancer. You will forfeit 0.25% of the purchase price if you withdraw [Source: NSW Fair Trading].

To get a building inspection done during the cooling-off period, give the consultant as much notice as possible. They will need to complete the inspection, prepare the report, and still give you time to decide whether to proceed or withdraw. This tight timeframe is why many buyers arrange inspections before exchange where possible.

### Auction properties

A cooling-off period does not apply if you buy a property at auction, or if you exchange contracts on the same day as the auction after the property is passed in [Source: NSW Fair Trading]. If you are buying at auction, you should arrange your building and pest inspection before the auction date. This means paying for the inspection even if you do not end up as the successful bidder, but it protects you from committing to a property with unknown defects.

Always check with your solicitor or licensed conveyancer that you have a cooling-off period, and have the process explained to you before you exchange.

## Who can conduct inspections

### Licensing requirements in NSW

The building inspection industry in NSW is largely unregulated, and there is no specific licence required to conduct pre-purchase building inspections [Source: NSW Fair Trading]. There is no legal requirement for building inspectors to hold formal building qualifications. This makes it essential that buyers verify an inspector's credentials before engaging them.

For building certifiers and inspectors involved in regulated building work, the NSW Design and Building Practitioners scheme requires registration, including endorsed contractor licences under the Home Building Act 1989, practical experience, and mandatory Construct NSW learning modules [Source: NSW Government, 2025]. However, this scheme primarily applies to construction work rather than pre-purchase inspections.

### Qualifications to look for

When choosing a building inspector, look for:

- **Certificate IV in Building and Construction (Building)** or **Diploma of Building Surveying**
- **Licensed builder** status under the NSW Home Building Act 1989
- Membership of a professional body such as the Australian Institute of Building Surveyors or the Institute of Building Inspectors
- **Professional indemnity insurance** appropriate to the value of work they assess
- Extensive practical experience in the building industry (many reputable inspectors have 10+ years as builders or surveyors)

For pest inspectors, look for:

- **Licence from NSW Fair Trading** as a pest management technician (pest inspections are more regulated than building inspections)
- Accreditation with the Australian Environmental Pest Managers Association (AEPMA)
- Specific training in timber pest detection and reporting

## What inspectors look for

### Structural issues

Inspectors examine the primary structural elements for signs of movement, cracking, or deterioration. This includes foundations, load-bearing walls, floor framing, roof trusses, and support beams. Crack widths are assessed against the classifications in Appendix E of AS 4349.1, ranging from hairline cracks (negligible) through to cracks exceeding 25mm (extreme structural damage) [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007].

### Water damage and moisture

The inspector looks for evidence of water ingress, including stained ceilings, swollen plaster, musty odours, and deteriorated sealants in wet areas. Moisture metres may be used to check moisture levels in walls adjoining showers and bathrooms.

### Roofing

The roof exterior, roof space, and ceiling linings are examined for damaged or displaced tiles, deteriorated flashings, blocked gutters, and signs of leaks. In Sydney's climate, with heavy rainfall events and strong winds, roof defects are a common finding.

### Subfloor and foundations

Where accessible, the subfloor is inspected for adequate ventilation, drainage, timber condition, and signs of termite activity or wood rot. Poor subfloor ventilation is a significant issue in many NSW properties.

### Electrical and plumbing (visual only)

While a building inspector does not test electrical or plumbing systems, they will note visibly unsafe conditions such as exposed wiring, missing switchboard covers, or obvious leaks. NSW Fair Trading recommends all new owners obtain a separate report on the electrical installation from a suitably qualified person [Source: NSW Fair Trading].

## Reading the report

Building inspection reports classify findings into categories under AS 4349.1. Understanding these classifications helps you assess the seriousness of each issue.

### Minor defects

Minor defects are maintenance issues or cosmetic problems that do not affect structural stability. Examples include small plaster cracks, worn sealants, minor timber deterioration, and loose fittings [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007]. Most properties will have minor defects; these are expected and usually inexpensive to address.

### Major defects

A major defect is defined as "a defect of sufficient magnitude where rectification has to be carried out in order to avoid unsafe conditions, loss of utility or further deterioration of the property" [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007]. Major defects affect structural integrity, require urgent attention, compromise safety, or need a licensed trade to repair. Examples include structural cracking, significant roof damage, active termite damage, and severe foundation movement.

### Safety hazards

A safety hazard is any defect that creates an immediate risk to occupants. Unsafe balustrades, imminent structural collapse, or dangerous electrical conditions are examples. The standard requires that safety hazards be reported in a manner that is not easily overlooked by the reader [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007].

### Conditions conducive to structural damage

The report will also note conditions that, while not yet causing damage, increase the risk of future problems. These include poor drainage, timber in contact with soil, excessive vegetation near the building, and inadequate subfloor ventilation [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007].

### Cost estimates

AS 4349.1 does not require inspectors to provide cost estimates for rectification work. The standard notes that the cost of rectification is notoriously difficult to estimate reliably because the actual extent of work cannot be known before commencement [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007]. Some inspectors provide indicative costs as a guide, but for reliable figures, you should obtain quotations from licensed tradespeople.

## Common issues in NSW properties

NSW has a diverse building stock and climate conditions that create specific defect patterns. Buyers should be particularly alert to the following.

### Rising damp and salt damage

Rising damp occurs when groundwater travels up through porous masonry walls. It is a persistent problem in older Sydney properties with failed or non-existent damp-proof courses. The moisture draws salt from the ground, which crystallises in the bricks and causes plaster to flake and paint to blister (efflorescence). In severe cases it can erode masonry [Source: Domain, 2017].

In Sydney's Inner West, inspectors report that rising damp is present in the vast majority of older homes. Rectification costs typically range from $3,000 to $10,000 or more, depending on the extent of the problem [Source: Vital Building Inspection, 2025].

### Subfloor ventilation

Inadequate subfloor ventilation traps moisture, creating damp conditions that promote timber rot, mould growth, and termite infestation. This issue is prevalent in Inner West terraces, Blacktown area homes, and any property with restricted airflow beneath the floor [Source: Vital Building Inspection, 2025]. Rectification with mechanical fans or passive vents typically costs $1,500 to $5,000.

### Termite risk zones

NSW, particularly the coastal and subtropical regions, is a high-risk zone for termite activity. Subterranean termites cause more damage to Australian homes than fire, floods, and storms combined. Properties with timber in ground contact, poor drainage, or inadequate subfloor ventilation are at heightened risk [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.3-2010].

### Asbestos

Homes built before 1990 may contain asbestos cement materials in roofing, eaves, wall cladding, and fencing. A building inspector may note suspect materials but cannot confirm asbestos without laboratory testing.

## Strata properties

When buying an apartment or townhouse in a strata scheme, the inspection approach is different from buying a freestanding house.

### When you need a building inspection

A building inspection for a strata property covers the individual lot you are purchasing. The inspector examines internal walls, ceilings, floors, wet areas, windows, doors, and any private outdoor areas such as balconies or courtyards. Where accessible, they may also examine the roof space or subfloor for ground floor units [Source: Standards Australia, AS 4349.1-2007 Appendix B].

The building inspection does not cover common property such as the building's main roof, lifts, stairwells, car parks, or lobbies. These areas are the responsibility of the owners corporation.

### When you need a strata inspection

A strata inspection report (also called a strata search) examines the administrative and financial records of the owners corporation. It reveals:

- Financial statements, levies, and reserve funds
- Meeting minutes showing disputes, planned works, and owner concerns
- Building insurance coverage
- Special levies for upcoming major expenses
- Compliance with fire safety and asbestos regulations
- By-laws affecting renovations, pets, and parking [Source: Owner Inspections, 2026]

You need both inspections. A strata inspection cannot identify physical defects in your unit, and a building inspection cannot reveal financial problems or special levies. In NSW, sellers must attach a strata information certificate to the contract, but a comprehensive strata inspection goes beyond this minimum requirement [Source: Owner Inspections, 2026].

## How much inspections cost

Inspection costs in NSW vary based on property size, location, and whether you bundle building and pest inspections together.

### Typical price ranges in NSW

| Inspection type | Typical cost range |
|---|---|
| Building inspection only (apartment) | $350 to $550 |
| Building inspection only (standard 3-bedroom house) | $450 to $650 |
| Building inspection only (large 4-5 bedroom house) | $600 to $850 |
| Combined building and pest (apartment) | $450 to $700 |
| Combined building and pest (standard house) | $500 to $850 |
| Combined building and pest (large house) | $750 to $1,100 |
| Very large or acreage property | $850+ |
| Strata inspection report | $200 to $400 |

Sydney prices sit at the higher end of these ranges due to higher operational costs [Source: Trade Heroes, 2025]. Combined building and pest inspections are typically more economical than booking each separately. Prices have remained relatively stable over the past decade, with most standard Sydney properties falling in the $400 to $500 range for a combined inspection [Source: Sydney PrePurchase, 2025].

## What to do if defects are found

### Negotiate

If the inspection reveals defects, you have several options. The most common approach is to negotiate with the vendor. You may request that the vendor rectifies the defects before settlement, or seek a price reduction to cover the cost of repairs yourself. Your conveyancer or solicitor can help frame these requests.

Use the report to support your position. Major defects and safety hazards carry the most weight in negotiations. Obtain quotes from licensed tradespeople for any significant repairs so you have realistic cost figures.

### Walk away

If the defects are extensive, structurally significant, or beyond your capacity to fund, you may decide not to proceed. If you are within the 5 business day cooling-off period, you can withdraw by giving written notice and forfeiting 0.25% of the purchase price [Source: NSW Fair Trading]. If you have not yet exchanged contracts, you can walk away without penalty.

For auction purchases where you inspected before bidding, you must make your decision before the auction. This is why pre-auction inspections are essential.

### Proceed with eyes open

Not every defect is a dealbreaker. Many older properties have minor defects that are manageable with regular maintenance. The key is understanding the severity of the issues, the cost of rectification, and whether the property is priced accordingly. A chat with the inspector after receiving the report is invaluable; sometimes defects that look alarming on paper are less serious in context [Source: Domain, 2017].

## Questions to ask your inspector

Before engaging a building and pest inspector, ask the following questions to ensure you are getting a qualified, experienced professional.

### Qualifications and experience

- What formal qualifications do you hold in building or construction?
- How many years of practical building industry experience do you have?
- How many inspections have you conducted in the local area?
- Are you a member of a professional body such as the Australian Institute of Building Surveyors?

### Insurance and compliance

- Do you hold professional indemnity insurance? (Ask for the coverage amount)
- Do you carry public liability insurance?
- Does your inspection comply with AS 4349.1 and AS 4349.3?
- Can you provide a sample report so I can see the level of detail and format?

### The inspection process

- How long will the inspection take? (A thorough inspection of a standard house should take 1.5 to 2 hours minimum)
- Will I be able to attend the inspection?
- When will I receive the report?
- Will you follow up with a phone call to discuss the findings?
- Do you use moisture metres, thermal imaging cameras, or other diagnostic tools?
- What happens if areas of the property are inaccessible?

### Scope and limitations

- Does your report include photographs of defects?
- Do you provide cost estimates for rectification work?
- What items are excluded from your inspection?
- If defects are found, can you recommend specialist tradespeople for further assessment?

A reputable inspector will welcome these questions and answer them transparently. Be cautious of inspectors who are reluctant to discuss their qualifications, rush the inspection, or offer prices significantly below market rates.

## Summary

A building and pest inspection is not a guarantee that a property is defect-free. It is a professional assessment of the property's visible and accessible condition at a point in time. The value lies in understanding the nature and severity of any issues so you can make an informed purchasing decision.

In NSW, where there is no mandatory licensing for building inspectors, choosing the right inspector is as important as the inspection itself. Look for formal qualifications, industry experience, professional insurance, and membership of a recognised industry body.

Always arrange inspections as early as possible in the buying process. For private treaty sales, use the cooling-off period as a safety net but aim to inspect before exchange. For auctions, inspect before you bid. The cost of an inspection (typically $400 to $850) is modest compared to the potential costs of discovering major defects after you have committed to the purchase.

For further guidance, consult NSW Fair Trading's resources on pre-purchase inspections, and speak with your solicitor or licensed conveyancer about how inspection findings interact with your contract and cooling-off rights.
