# Building Inspections and Due Diligence for First Home Buyers

Buying your first home is likely the largest purchase you will ever make. The inspection and due diligence stage is where you verify that the property is structurally sound, free from hidden defects, and suitable for your needs. This guide covers everything a first home buyer in NSW needs to know before signing on the dotted line.

## The Building Inspection: What It Covers and What It Does Not

A pre-purchase building inspection is a visual assessment of a property's condition, conducted by a licensed building inspector. In NSW, building inspectors are not required to hold a specific licence for inspections alone, but many are licensed builders or building surveyors with professional indemnity insurance. Always verify your inspector's qualifications and insurance before engaging them.

### What a Building Inspection Covers

A standard inspection examines accessible parts of the property and reports on defects according to the Australian Standard AS 4349.1. The inspector will assess:

- **Structural integrity**: Foundations, footings, load-bearing walls, and framing. Look for cracks in walls, uneven floors, or doors that do not close properly. These can indicate movement in the foundation.
- **Roof and roof space**: Condition of roof covering, gutters, downpipes, flashings, and the roof structure itself. Water stains on ceilings often point to roof leaks.
- **Exterior**: Walls, cladding, windows, doors, and external finishes. Render cracks, timber rot, and poor drainage fall are common issues.
- **Interior**: Floors, walls, ceilings, fitted kitchens, and bathrooms. The inspector checks for moisture damage, mould, and structural defects.
- **Wet areas**: Bathrooms, kitchens, and laundry areas are checked for waterproofing failures, leaking fixtures, and ventilation adequacy.
- **Subfloor**: Where accessible, the subfloor is examined for dampness, ventilation, timber pest damage, and structural adequacy.
- **Site and outbuildings**: Fences, retaining walls, driveways, paths, garages, and sheds.
- **Safety hazards**: The inspector should note any immediate safety risks such as asbestos, unsafe balconies, or electrical hazards.

### What a Building Inspection Does Not Cover

Understanding the limitations is just as important. A standard building inspection typically does not include:

- **Hidden or inaccessible areas**: Inside walls, beneath floor coverings, behind stored items, or inside roof spaces that are too small or unsafe to enter.
- **Plumbing and electrical systems**: These require specialist inspections by licensed plumbers and electricians. A building inspector may note visible signs of problems but will not test systems.
- **Swimming pools and spas**: Pool compliance and safety barriers are assessed separately.
- **Fireplaces and flues**: These require a separate assessment by an appropriately licensed tradesperson.
- **Appliances and services**: Hot water systems, air conditioning units, and other appliances are not tested.
- **Pest activity**: A separate pest inspection is required for timber pest assessment.
- **Compliance with council approvals**: The inspector does not verify whether structures have council approval.

Always read the inspection agreement carefully to understand exactly what is and is not included. If you have specific concerns about any of these areas, discuss them with your inspector before the inspection and consider engaging additional specialists.

## Pest Inspection: Why It Is Essential and What Inspectors Look For

Timber pests can cause extensive and costly damage that is often hidden from view. A pest inspection, conducted to Australian Standard AS 4349.3, is essential for every property purchase in NSW, particularly in areas with known termite activity.

### What the Pest Inspector Examines

The inspector looks for evidence of:

- **Subterranean termites**: The most destructive timber pest in Australia. They can cause major structural damage before being detected. The inspector uses moisture meters, thermal imaging (where available), and tapping tools to identify activity.
- **Timber decay fungi**: Wood rot caused by fungal growth in damp conditions. This can weaken structural timbers significantly.
- **Wood-boring insects**: Borers attack timber and can affect floorboards, furniture, and structural elements.
- **Previous pest damage**: Evidence of past termite activity, even if treated, is important to note as it may affect the property's history and future risk.
- **Conducive conditions**: The inspector identifies environmental factors that increase pest risk, such as poor drainage, timber in contact with soil, leaking taps, and inadequate subfloor ventilation.

### Understanding the Pest Report

Pest reports classify findings into categories. Active termite activity is the most serious finding and requires immediate treatment by a licensed pest controller. Previous damage that has been treated is less urgent but still material. Conducive conditions should be addressed to reduce future risk.

Many first home buyers are tempted to skip the pest inspection to save money. This is unwise. Termite damage is rarely covered by standard home insurance policies, and treatment plus repair costs can run into tens of thousands of dollars. The relatively small cost of a pest inspection is a sensible investment in protecting yourself.

## Strata Report for Apartments: What to Look For

If you are buying an apartment, townhouse, or villa within a strata scheme, a strata inspection report is essential. This report provides a detailed history of the owners corporation and can reveal problems that are not visible during a physical inspection.

### Key Elements to Review

**Sinking fund and administrative fund**

The sinking fund (now called the capital works fund) is money set aside for major capital expenditure such as roof replacement, painting, and lift maintenance. Check whether the fund is adequately provisioned for the scheme's age and condition. A well-funded sinking fund indicates responsible management. An underfunded fund may mean special levies are coming.

**Special levies**

Special levies are one-off contributions raised from owners for unexpected or underfunded expenses. Review the minutes for any recent or proposed special levies. Multiple special levies in quick succession can indicate financial mismanagement or significant maintenance issues.

**Pending maintenance and repairs**

The report should detail known defects and planned works. Look for references to concrete cancer, waterproofing failures, fire safety upgrades, or facade repairs. These can be extraordinarily expensive in apartment buildings.

**Disputes and litigation**

Check for ongoing disputes between owners, with the strata manager, or with builders. Litigation is costly and time-consuming. It can also make it harder to sell the property later, as buyers are often wary of schemes in dispute.

**By-laws and restrictions**

Review the by-laws for any restrictions that affect your intended use of the property. Pet policies, renovation approval processes, and use of common property are common areas of concern.

**Strata management quality**

The frequency of meetings, quality of minutes, and responsiveness of the strata manager all indicate how well the scheme is run. Poor record-keeping and infrequent meetings are red flags.

### How to Obtain a Strata Report

You can request a strata inspection report through your solicitor or conveyancer, or directly from a strata inspection company. Reports typically cost between $250 and $400 depending on the size and complexity of the scheme. Your conveyancer will usually arrange this as part of the due diligence process.

## Council Compliance: Unapproved Structures and Additions

One of the most common issues found during due diligence is unapproved structures or additions. Previous owners may have built a deck, pergola, granny flat, or converted a garage without obtaining the necessary council approval or complying development certificate.

### Why This Matters

Unapproved structures can create several problems for new owners:

- **Council enforcement action**: The local council may issue a building information certificate requirement or an order to demolish or rectify the structure.
- **Insurance issues**: Insurers may decline claims related to unapproved structures or may refuse to insure them altogether.
- **Valuation impact**: Unapproved works may not add value and can actually reduce the property's marketability.
- **Future sale difficulties**: You may face the same issue when you come to sell.

### How to Check Compliance

Your conveyancer or solicitor should order a Section 10.7 planning certificate (formerly Section 149 certificate) from the local council. This certificate shows the zoning of the property and any planning restrictions. Additionally, you can request copies of approved plans and development consents from council to compare against what actually exists on the property.

For properties with recent renovations or additions, ask the vendor or agent for copies of development approvals and occupation certificates. If these are not available, consider whether you are prepared to take on the risk.

## Flood and Bushfire Zone Checks

Natural hazard disclosures are a critical part of due diligence in NSW. Climate-related risks are increasingly affecting property values, insurance availability, and liveability.

### Flood Zone Checks

Flood risk varies significantly across NSW. Properties in the Hawkesbury-Nepean valley, parts of Western Sydney, the North Coast, and many regional areas have experienced repeated flooding in recent years.

To check flood risk:

1. **NSW Government Flood Data Portal**: Provides access to flood mapping for many areas across the state.
2. **Local council flood maps**: Most councils publish flood prone land maps on their websites.
3. **Insurance premium estimates**: Obtain quotes from multiple insurers. Significant premium increases or difficulty obtaining flood cover are warning signs.

Even if a property has not flooded in recent memory, changes in upstream development, stormwater infrastructure, and climate patterns can alter flood risk. Ask the selling agent directly whether the property has flooded previously, and document their response.

### Bushfire Zone Checks

Properties in bushland settings or on the urban fringe may be subject to bushfire attack level (BAL) ratings. A BAL assessment determines the construction standards required for any building on the property.

To check bushfire risk:

1. **NSW Rural Fire Service Fire Mapping**: The RFS website provides interactive mapping showing bushfire prone land.
2. **BAL rating**: If the property is in a bushfire prone area, a formal BAL assessment may be required. Higher BAL ratings (BAL-29 and above) impose significant construction costs and may affect insurance premiums.
3. **Building code requirements**: New construction and substantial renovations in bushfire prone areas must comply with AS 3959.

Insurance costs in high bushfire risk areas have increased substantially in recent years. Obtain insurance quotes before committing to a purchase in these areas.

## Easements and Restrictions: How to Find Them

An easement is a legal right that someone else holds over your property. Common easements include:

- **Drainage easements**: Allow councils or utility providers to access and maintain stormwater or sewer pipes.
- **Right of way**: Allows neighbours or the public to pass through part of the property.
- **Powerline or utility easements**: Allow electricity or telecommunications providers to access infrastructure.
- **Conservation easements**: Restrict development to protect environmental values.

Easements are recorded on the property's title and will appear in the contract of sale. Your conveyancer will identify them during the title search. Ask your conveyancer to explain the practical implications of any easements, as some may restrict where you can build or extend.

### Restrictive Covenants

Restrictive covenants are rules recorded on the title that limit what can be done with the property. Common examples include minimum building sizes, restrictions on materials, or limits on the number of dwellings. These are common in newer estates and can be difficult to remove or vary.

## Neighbourhood Due Diligence: Visiting at Different Times

A property can feel very different at 9 pm on a Saturday compared to 10 am on a Tuesday. Before committing to a purchase, visit the property and surrounding streets at different times of day and week.

### What to Observe

- **Noise levels**: Traffic, aircraft flight paths, nearby industrial activity, and entertainment venues may not be apparent during a daytime inspection.
- **Parking pressure**: Streets near train stations, hospitals, and beaches often have acute parking shortages in the evenings and weekends.
- **Street lighting and safety**: Walk the neighbourhood after dark to assess lighting and your comfort level.
- **Neighbour behaviour**: Barking dogs, loud music, and general activity levels vary by time of day.
- **Traffic patterns**: School zones, commuter routes, and delivery vehicle movements can affect noise and convenience.
- **Amenity access**: Check how busy local shops, cafes, and parks are at different times.

Talk to neighbours if possible. People who live on the street often have valuable insights about the area that agents may not volunteer.

## Insurance Cost Estimation: What Affects Premiums

Before exchanging contracts, obtain building and contents insurance quotes. Insurance costs can vary dramatically between properties and may affect your ongoing budget.

### Factors That Affect Premiums

- **Location**: Proximity to bushland, flood zones, and coastal erosion areas significantly affects premiums. Crime rates in the suburb also influence costs.
- **Building materials**: Brick construction typically costs less to insure than timber. Thatched roofs and certain cladding materials may attract loadings or exclusions.
- **Property age and condition**: Older properties with outdated wiring, plumbing, and roofing may cost more to insure.
- **Security features**: Deadlocks, security screens, alarms, and CCTV can reduce premiums.
- **Sum insured**: The cost to rebuild the property, not the purchase price. Use an online calculator to estimate an appropriate sum insured.
- **Claims history**: Previous claims on the property may affect future premiums.

If you struggle to obtain insurance or receive quotes with very high premiums, this is a red flag that other buyers may also struggle with in the future, potentially affecting resale value.

## How to Read a Building Inspection Report

Building inspection reports can be overwhelming for first home buyers. Learning to read them systematically helps you extract the information that matters.

### Report Structure

Most reports follow a standard format:

1. **Property details and inspection scope**: Verify the address, date, and what was included.
2. **Summary of major defects**: This is the most important section. Major defects are serious problems requiring urgent attention.
3. **Minor defects and maintenance items**: Less urgent but still useful for budgeting.
4. **Detailed findings by area**: Room-by-room or element-by-element assessment.
5. **Photographs**: Visual evidence of defects found.
6. **Recommendations**: Suggested further investigations or specialist assessments.

### What to Look For

- **Major structural defects**: These are the deal-breakers. Foundation movement, significant roof structure problems, and major water ingress should not be ignored.
- **Safety hazards**: Asbestos, electrical hazards, and unstable structures need immediate attention.
- **Evidence of previous problems**: Stains, repairs, and non-matching materials may indicate past issues that were not properly resolved.
- **Patterns of defects**: Multiple related defects (for example, widespread moisture damage) may indicate a systemic problem rather than isolated issues.

### Questions to Ask Your Inspector

- What is the estimated cost to rectify the major defects?
- Are any of these defects urgent safety issues?
- Would you recommend any further specialist inspections?
- If this were your purchase, would you proceed, negotiate, or walk away?
- Are the defects consistent with the property's age, or do they suggest poor maintenance?

A good inspector will talk you through the report and answer your questions patiently. Do not hesitate to ask for clarification in plain language.

## What to Do if Serious Defects Are Found

Discovering serious defects during your due diligence can be distressing, but it is far better to find them now than after you own the property.

### Your Options

1. **Negotiate a price reduction**: Obtain quotes for repairs and use the inspection report as evidence to request a lower purchase price. This is the most common outcome for material but manageable defects.
2. **Request the vendor rectifies defects before settlement**: Some vendors will agree to undertake repairs, though quality control can be an issue. Ensure any work is inspected and certified.
3. **Accept the property as-is and budget for repairs**: If the price already reflects the condition, or if you were planning renovations anyway, you may choose to proceed. Ensure you have realistic cost estimates and contingency funds.
4. **Terminate the contract**: If defects are severe, widespread, or the true condition was misrepresented, you may be able to rescind the contract. This depends on the contract terms, the cooling-off period, and whether the defects constitute a material misrepresentation.

### Document Everything

Keep copies of all inspection reports, quotes for repairs, correspondence with the agent, and any vendor disclosures. These documents are important if a dispute arises later.

### When to Walk Away

Consider walking away if:

- The cost of rectification exceeds your available funds, even with a price reduction.
- The defects suggest fundamental problems with the property (poor construction, ongoing water ingress, active termite damage to structural elements).
- The vendor or agent was clearly aware of the defects and concealed them.
- You have lost confidence in the property and will worry about it constantly.

There will always be other properties. A building inspection that saves you from a disastrous purchase is money well spent.
