# Contract Review Checklist: First Home Buyer (Owner-Occupier)

## Conveyancer engagement
- [ ] Confirm your conveyancer or solicitor is licensed and holds professional indemnity insurance
- [ ] Provide them the contract of sale at least 5 business days before you plan to exchange
- [ ] Ask them to explain every clause in plain language: not just flag issues
- [ ] Confirm their fee structure: fixed fee or hourly: and what is included
- [ ] Ask about additional costs for disbursements, title searches, and certificate requests

## Standard contract clauses
- [ ] Verify the property address and legal description match what you are expecting to buy
- [ ] Confirm the purchase price on the contract matches your agreed offer
- [ ] Check the deposit amount: typically 10%: and the payment method and timing
- [ ] Review the settlement period: usually 42 days: and confirm it works for your situation
- [ ] Check whether the contract is subject to a cooling off period and understand the penalty for withdrawal
- [ ] Verify the list of inclusions: fixtures, fittings, appliances, window coverings, floor coverings
- [ ] Confirm the list of exclusions: anything the vendor is taking with them
- [ ] Check whether the property is being sold as a going concern or vacant possession

## Special conditions to protect you
- [ ] Include a finance clause: typically 14-21 days: unless you have unconditional approval
- [ ] Include a building and pest inspection clause with clear outcomes for major defects
- [ ] Add a clause making the contract subject to a satisfactory strata report if buying an apartment
- [ ] Request a clause allowing you to do a final inspection within 48 hours of settlement
- [ ] Consider a sunset clause if buying off-the-plan: understand the risks and benefits
- [ ] Add a clause requiring the vendor to maintain the property in its current condition until settlement

## Certificates and searches
- [ ] Your conveyancer should order a title search to confirm the legal owner and any encumbrances
- [ ] Request a Section 10.7 (2) and (5) certificate from council: zoning, flood risk, bushfire risk, contamination
- [ ] Order a drainage diagram to check sewer lines, easements, and connection points
- [ ] Check for easements or restrictions on the title that affect how you can use the property
- [ ] Verify there are no outstanding council notices or orders against the property
- [ ] Check land tax clearance if the vendor is an investor: ensure no outstanding land tax liability
- [ ] Order a strata inspection report if buying a unit, townhouse, or villa

## Contract red flags
- [ ] Watch for special conditions that favour the vendor: like broad termination rights or limitations on your cooling off
- [ ] Be wary of any clause that allows the vendor to rescind if they receive a higher offer
- [ ] Check whether the contract limits your right to claim for defects discovered after exchange
- [ ] Look for any requirement to use the vendor's nominated conveyancer or settlement agent
- [ ] Be cautious of contracts that waive your right to a building inspection or due diligence
- [ ] Flag any clause that makes the deposit non-refundable under circumstances beyond your control

## Insurance
- [ ] Arrange building insurance to start from the moment you exchange contracts: not settlement
- [ ] Confirm the policy covers the full replacement cost of the building, not just market value
- [ ] Add contents insurance if you want coverage for your belongings from settlement
- [ ] Check if the property is in a flood or bushfire zone and confirm coverage is available
- [ ] Provide your conveyancer with a certificate of currency before settlement

## Final checks before exchange
- [ ] Read the contract yourself: do not rely solely on your conveyancer's summary
- [ ] Double-check the spelling of all names and the property description
- [ ] Confirm your finance approval covers the exact purchase price and property address
- [ ] Have your deposit ready to transfer immediately on exchange
- [ ] Ask your conveyancer to explain the settlement process and timeline
- [ ] Know who is holding the deposit: the agent's trust account or a solicitor's trust account
