# Shortlisting properties as a first home buyer

You have your deposit sorted, pre-approval in hand, and a clear idea of where you want to live. Now comes the hard part: turning hundreds of listings into a shortlist you can actually inspect. This stage is where most first home buyers either get overwhelmed or rush into decisions they later regret. A disciplined shortlisting process saves time, reduces stress, and helps you compare properties on equal footing.

## Creating a property scorecard: criteria that matter for owner-occupiers

A property scorecard is a simple weighted scoring system that forces you to evaluate each listing against the same criteria. Without one, it is easy to fall into the trap of comparing properties on completely different bases, one by location, another by kitchen quality, another by price.

Start by listing every factor that could influence your decision. Common categories for owner-occupiers include:

- Location and commute: proximity to work, public transport access, drive time in peak hours
- Local amenities: shops, schools, parks, healthcare facilities
- Property features: number of bedrooms, bathrooms, parking, outdoor space
- Building condition: structural integrity, age of systems, maintenance needs
- Future potential: renovation scope, rezoning potential, planned infrastructure
- Price: within budget, room to negotiate, value relative to comparable sales

Weight each category according to your priorities. If a 30-minute commute is non-negotiable, location might carry 25% of the total score. If you work from home, a dedicated study space might rate higher than proximity to a train station. The scorecard is personal. What matters is consistency.

Rate each property out of ten for every category, multiply by the weighting, and tally the scores. A property scoring 82/100 against another at 71/100 gives you a rational basis for comparison that goes beyond gut feel. The scorecard does not make the decision for you, but it prevents the shiniest kitchen from overriding a compromised location.

## Must-haves vs nice-to-haves: a framework for decision making

Every buyer starts with a wish list. The problem is that wish lists rarely match budgets. Separating must-haves from nice-to-haves before you start inspecting prevents disappointment and keeps you focused.

Must-haves are dealbreakers. If a property fails these criteria, you do not inspect it, no matter how appealing the photos look. Examples might include:

- Within 10km of your workplace
- Minimum two bedrooms
- Off-street parking for one vehicle
- Maximum purchase price of $750,000
- Not on a main road or under a flight path

Nice-to-haves are preferences, not requirements. You would take them if available but they do not determine whether a property makes your shortlist. Examples include:

- Separate laundry room
- North-facing backyard
- Walk-in wardrobe in the main bedroom
- Recently renovated kitchen
- Proximity to a specific cafe strip

The discipline comes in applying these filters before emotion enters the picture. Write your must-have list down. Share it with someone you trust. When a listing arrives in your inbox, run it against the must-haves first. If it fails, delete or archive it. Only evaluate nice-to-haves for properties that have cleared the essential bar.

Research from the Australian Bureau of Statistics shows that first home buyers typically spend 12 to 18 months actively searching before purchasing. A clear must-have filter prevents you from burning out on properties that were never suitable in the first place.

## Property types compared: house, townhouse, apartment

Each property type suits different lifestyles and budgets. Understanding the trade-offs helps you focus your search on what actually works for your situation.

### Houses

A standalone house offers the most control. You own the land and the building, which means no body corporate fees and no restrictions on renovations. For buyers planning to extend, add a deck, or keep pets without seeking permission, a house is the straightforward choice.

The downside is cost. In capital cities, the median house price is substantially higher than the median apartment price. According to CoreLogic data, the gap between house and unit median prices in Sydney and Melbourne has widened significantly over the past decade. Houses also carry higher maintenance costs: roofs, gutters, gardens, and fences are entirely your responsibility.

### Townhouses

Townhouses sit in the middle ground. They typically offer more space than apartments, often with a small courtyard or garden, while being more affordable than houses in the same suburb. Strata or body corporate arrangements may still apply, but fees are usually lower than apartment complexes because there are fewer shared facilities.

The trade-off is shared walls and often limited outdoor space. Renovations may still require body corporate approval for anything affecting external walls or common property. Parking is typically limited to one or two spaces.

### Apartments

Apartments are usually the most affordable entry point, particularly in inner-ring suburbs where houses are priced beyond most first home buyers. They offer low maintenance living: no lawns to mow, no gutters to clean. Building insurance is covered by the strata scheme.

The limitations are well documented. Body corporate fees can be substantial, particularly in buildings with lifts, pools, and gyms. Special levies for major maintenance can arrive without warning. Space is constrained, outdoor areas are often limited to balconies, and pet ownership may be restricted by by-laws. According to data from the Real Estate Institute of Australia, apartments in oversupplied markets can also face weaker price growth over the long term compared to houses in the same city.

For first home buyers, the decision often comes down to location versus space. An apartment 5km from the city centre versus a house 25km out is a lifestyle calculation only you can make.

## Red flags to watch for at open homes

Open homes are staged to present properties in their best light. Your job is to look past the styling and identify problems that could cost you later.

Structural warning signs include cracks in walls, particularly around doorframes and windows, which can indicate foundation movement. Check for doors that do not close properly or floors that slope. These can be expensive to rectify. Look for water stains on ceilings and in cupboards under sinks, which suggest leaks that may have caused hidden damage.

Smell is a reliable indicator. Mustiness suggests damp or poor ventilation. A heavy fragrance may be masking pet odours, smoke damage, or mould. Fresh paint in specific patches rather than whole rooms can be covering water damage or repairs.

External factors matter too. Visit the property at different times of day if possible. A quiet street on a Saturday morning may be a traffic nightmare at 5pm on a weekday. Check for neighbouring development applications that could block views or introduce noise. Note the distance to power lines, industrial areas, or entertainment venues.

Ask the agent directly about the property's history. How long has it been on the market? Have there been previous sales that fell through? Why are the owners selling? You may not get straight answers, but the agent's response can be informative.

## How to use online listings effectively: filtering, alerts, saved searches

Online portals are the starting point for nearly every property search. Using them efficiently saves hours each week and ensures you do not miss listings that match your criteria.

### Filtering

Set your filters tightly around your must-have list rather than your nice-to-have list. If you need two bedrooms minimum, filter for two bedrooms, not one. If your maximum price is $700,000, set the filter at $720,000 to allow for properties listed with a range that starts below your ceiling. Include surrounding suburbs if your target area is small or low in listings.

Be careful with keyword searches. Terms like "renovator's delight" and "unlimited potential" often mean the property needs substantial work. "Cosy" usually means small. "Convenient location" can mean busy road. Learn to read between the lines.

### Alerts

Set up email or push notifications for your saved searches. New listings that match your criteria will arrive daily. Review them against your scorecard as soon as they hit your inbox. Good properties in popular price ranges can attract inspection bookings within days of listing.

### Saved searches

Create multiple saved searches if you are considering different areas or property types. One search might cover houses in your preferred suburb, another might cover townhouses in neighbouring suburbs. This lets you compare listing volumes and price movements across your options.

Both major Australian portals, realestate.com.au and Domain, offer sold section data. Use this to research what comparable properties have sold for recently. This gives you a realistic benchmark before you attend inspections and helps you spot overpriced listings.

## Attending open homes: what to look for, what to ask the agent

Open homes are your opportunity to gather information that listings cannot convey. Arrive prepared with a checklist and a camera or notebook.

### What to look for

Start outside. Check the roof condition, guttering, fences, and the state of neighbouring properties. A poorly maintained street or adjoining property can affect your enjoyment and future resale value.

Inside, test taps for water pressure and check how long hot water takes to arrive. Turn lights on and off. Open and close windows. Look for adequate power points in each room. Check storage: built-in wardrobes, kitchen cupboards, linen closets. Lack of storage is a common regret for first home buyers.

Measure rooms if you have specific furniture that needs to fit. Standard real estate photography uses wide-angle lenses that make rooms appear larger than they are. Bring a tape measure.

Take photos of anything concerning for later reference. Check the aspect of living areas: north-facing rear yards and living spaces receive the most natural light in Australia.

### What to ask the agent

- What is the expected sale price or price range?
- Are there any known defects or issues with the property?
- What are the council rates and any other ongoing costs?
- Is the property subject to a body corporate or strata scheme? If so, what are the quarterly fees?
- Are there any planned developments or zoning changes in the area?
- Have any building or pest inspections already been conducted?
- Is the seller open to negotiation on price or settlement terms?

Write the answers down. Agents represent the seller, not you, but they are required to answer direct questions honestly. The information they provide can help you refine your shortlist and prepare for future stages.

## Due diligence starts at shortlisting: preliminary checks

Full due diligence comes later, but smart buyers start preliminary checks during shortlisting to avoid wasting time on properties with hidden problems.

### Planning and zoning

Check the local council website for zoning maps and planning instruments. Is the property zoned residential? Are there overlays that restrict development, such as heritage or environmental protections? Are there development applications nearby that could affect the area? Most council planning portals are free to search.

### Title and restrictions

Order a title search through the relevant state land titles office. This reveals the registered owner, any mortgages, caveats, easements, or covenants. An easement might mean a utility company has the right to access part of the land. A covenant might restrict what you can build. These are not necessarily dealbreakers, but you should know about them before proceeding.

### Flood and fire risk

Check NSW natural hazard mapping through the Department of Planning and Environment's planning portals. Also review council flood certificates and Bush Fire Prone Land maps. Properties in flood zones or bushfire-prone areas can carry higher insurance costs and resale risks.

### Infrastructure plans

State governments publish major infrastructure plans online. Check whether major roads, rail lines, or airports are planned for your target area. Infrastructure can add value when completed, but construction periods can disrupt liveability for years.

## Emotional discipline: not falling in love with the first property

The biggest risk for first home buyers is emotional attachment. You walk into a property that feels right, start imagining your furniture in the rooms, and suddenly every other option looks inferior. This is natural, and it is also dangerous.

Emotional attachment weakens your negotiating position and clouds your judgment. A buyer who has already decided they want a property will overlook flaws, stretch their budget, and rush due diligence.

Set a rule before you start inspecting: no decisions at open homes. Attend, evaluate, record your scorecard, and leave. Do not discuss offers with the agent on the spot. Sleep on it. If the property still scores highly the next day, it deserves a place on your shortlist.

Limit how many properties you inspect per weekend. Burnout is real in property searching. After three or four inspections in a day, details blur together. Quality of evaluation beats quantity every time.

Remember that there is always another property. The Australian property market turns over constantly. If you miss out, another suitable listing will appear. Acting out of fear of missing out rather than rational assessment is how buyers overpay.

## Creating a comparison spreadsheet

A comparison spreadsheet is the practical tool that brings your scorecard to life. It does not need to be complex. A simple table with properties as rows and criteria as columns is sufficient.

Include these columns as a minimum:

- Property address
- Listed price
- Property type
- Bedrooms / bathrooms / car spaces
- Land size (for houses and townhouses)
- Body corporate fees (for strata properties)
- Council rates (if known)
- Distance to work
- Distance to nearest public transport
- School zone (if relevant)
- Scorecard rating
- Notes

Update the spreadsheet after every inspection. Over time, patterns emerge. You might notice that every property scoring above 80 is in a particular pocket of your target area, which helps refine your search. You might notice that you consistently downgrade properties for the same reason, which helps clarify your true priorities.

Share the spreadsheet with a trusted friend or family member who can offer objective feedback. Someone not emotionally invested in the purchase can spot patterns you miss and challenge assumptions you have stopped questioning.

## Next steps: from shortlist to serious contenders

Once your shortlist reaches five to eight properties, it is time to narrow further. Rank them by scorecard score and personal preference. The top three to four become your serious contenders.

For each contender, request the contract of sale from the selling agent. This contains the title details, zoning certificates, and any special conditions. Take these contracts to a conveyancer or solicitor for review before you consider making an offer. This review typically costs a few hundred dollars and can reveal issues that remove a property from contention.

Arrange second inspections of your top contenders. Attend at a different time of day. Walk the surrounding streets. Visit the local shops and speak to neighbours if the opportunity arises. The more you know about a property and its context, the better your decision will be.

Keep monitoring new listings even as you focus on serious contenders. A better option may appear. Maintain discipline with your scorecard and must-have criteria until you have exchanged contracts.

The shortlisting stage is where the emotional rush of starting your search meets the reality of evaluating actual properties. The buyers who do well are those who bring structure to the process, who evaluate consistently, and who resist the pressure to decide before they are ready. Your first home is a significant purchase. The time you spend shortlisting properly is never wasted.
