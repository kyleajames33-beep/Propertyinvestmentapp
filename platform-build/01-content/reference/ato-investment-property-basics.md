# ATO investment property basics

This guide covers the fundamentals of how the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) treats residential investment property for tax purposes. It explains what counts as rental income, what you can deduct, how depreciation works, the basics of negative gearing, capital gains tax, record-keeping requirements, common mistakes, and how GST applies. Use this information to understand the framework so you can ask your accountant or tax agent better questions about your specific situation.

## What the ATO considers an investment property

The ATO treats a property as an investment property when you own it and use it to produce rental income. This includes:

- **Residential rental properties**: houses, units, townhouses, or apartments leased to tenants on a short-term or long-term basis [Source: ATO, 2026].
- **Holiday homes**: properties that are rented out to holiday makers, including through platforms like Airbnb or Stayz, even if you also use the property privately [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Mixed-use properties**: properties used partly to produce rental income and partly for private purposes, such as renting out a room in your home or using a holiday home for part of the year [Source: ATO, 2026].
- **Overseas properties**: rental properties located outside Australia are still subject to Australian tax rules for Australian residents and must be declared in your Australian tax return [Source: ATO, 2026].

Under draft guidance in TR 2025/D1, the ATO has clarified that a property must be genuinely held to produce assessable income for deductions to be available. If a holiday home is used mainly for private recreation, or is not genuinely available for rent during peak periods, ownership costs such as interest, rates, and insurance may be denied under section 26-50 of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 as a "leisure facility" [Source: TR 2025/D1, 2025].

## Rental income

You must declare all income you receive from your rental property in your tax return. Rental income is assessable in the year you are entitled to receive it, not necessarily when you receive it [Source: ATO, 2026].

### What counts as rental income

The following payments are assessable rental income:

- **Rent payments**: regular rent paid by tenants, whether paid to you directly, through a property manager, or via a sharing economy platform [Source: ATO, 2026].
- **Bond money**: amounts you retain in place of rent, or keep because of damage to the property caused by tenants [Source: ATO, 2026].
- **Letting and booking fees**: cancellation fees you retain when renters or holiday makers cancel a booking [Source: ATO, 2026].
- **Insurance payouts**: payments for loss of rental income, damage from natural disasters, or damage from unexpected events such as burst pipes [Source: ATO, 2026].
- **Reimbursements**: payments from tenants to cover repair costs for damage they caused, or government rebates for purchasing depreciating assets such as solar hot water systems [Source: ATO, 2026].
- **Goods and services**: if a tenant pays you in goods or services instead of cash, you must include the monetary value of those goods or services in your assessable income [Source: ATO, 2026].

Even if you rent the property to family or friends at below-market rates, you must still declare the income you receive. However, your deductions may be limited in these circumstances [Source: ATO, 2025].

## Immediate deductions

You can claim a deduction for certain expenses in the year you incur them, provided they relate directly to producing rental income. You cannot claim deductions for periods when the property was not genuinely available for rent [Source: ATO, 2025].

### What you can claim immediately

- **Advertising for tenants**: costs to advertise the property for rent, including online listings, signage, and agency advertising fees [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Body corporate fees**: fees paid to a body corporate administration fund for managing common property in a unit complex [Source: ATO, 2025]. Note that special contributions for capital improvements must be depreciated over time.
- **Council rates**: local government rates charged on the property, apportioned to the period the property was available for rent [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Insurance**: premiums for building, contents, landlord, and public liability insurance [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Interest on loans**: interest charged on money borrowed to purchase, improve, or maintain the rental property. You cannot claim deductions for principal repayments [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Land tax**: state-based land tax levied on the property, where applicable [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Property agent fees**: fees paid to a property manager or real estate agent for managing the property, collecting rent, and finding tenants [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Repairs and maintenance**: costs to repair damage or deterioration from normal wear and tear, or to maintain the property in its current condition [Source: ATO, 2025].
- **Utilities**: water, gas, and electricity charges where you (not the tenant) pay these bills [Source: ATO, 2025].

### Repairs versus improvements

Understanding the difference between repairs and capital improvements is critical. **Repairs** restore something to its original condition and are immediately deductible. **Improvements** enhance the property beyond its original state and must be depreciated as capital works [Source: ATO, 2025].

For example, replacing a few broken roof tiles is a repair and immediately deductible. Replacing the entire roof with a higher-quality material is a capital improvement and must be claimed over time [Source: ATO, 2025].

### Travel expenses

From 1 July 2017, you cannot claim deductions for travel expenses to inspect, maintain, or collect rent for a residential rental property unless you are carrying on a business of letting rental properties [Source: ATO, 2025].

## Capital works deductions

Capital works deductions (Division 43 of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997) allow you to deduct the cost of the building structure and fixed improvements over time [Source: ATO, 2024].

### What qualifies for capital works deductions

Capital works include:

- Building foundations, walls, floors, and roofs
- Built-in kitchen cupboards, bathroom vanities, and wardrobes
- Driveways, fences, retaining walls, and paved areas
- Structural renovations and extensions
- Common property in unit complexes [Source: ATO, 2024]

### The 2.5% rate over 40 years

For most residential properties where construction commenced on or after 15 September 1987, you can claim capital works deductions at a rate of 2.5% per year for 40 years [Source: ATO, 2024]. The deduction is calculated on the construction cost, not the purchase price of the property.

**Worked example:** If the construction cost of your rental property was $200,000, your annual capital works deduction would be $200,000 multiplied by 2.5%, which equals $5,000 per year for 40 years [Source: ATO, 2024].

### Construction commencement dates

- Construction commenced after 18 July 1985 and before 16 September 1987: deductions may be available at a rate of 4% over 25 years [Source: ATO, 2024].
- Construction commenced on or after 16 September 1987: 2.5% per year for 40 years [Source: ATO, 2024].
- If you purchase an older property, you may still be able to claim capital works deductions for any renovations or structural improvements completed after these dates [Source: ATO, 2024].

### Quantity surveyor reports

The ATO accepts estimates of construction costs from qualified quantity surveyors. A quantity surveyor can prepare a tax depreciation schedule that identifies all capital works and plant and equipment deductions available. This schedule typically covers up to 40 years of deductions and can be provided to your accountant each year [Source: ATO, 2024]. The cost of the quantity surveyor's report is itself tax deductible.

## Depreciating assets

Plant and equipment (Division 40 of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997) covers removable or mechanical assets within the property that wear out over time [Source: ATO, 2024].

### What can be depreciated

Common depreciating assets in rental properties include:

- Air conditioners and ceiling fans
- Hot water systems
- Ovens, cooktops, rangehoods, and dishwashers
- Carpets and vinyl flooring
- Blinds and curtains
- Smoke alarms
- Clothes dryers [Source: ATO, 2024]

### The second-hand asset rule

From 9 May 2017, if you purchase a residential rental property, you can no longer claim depreciation on previously used (second-hand) plant and equipment items. You can only claim depreciation on plant and equipment assets that are brand new when you install them [Source: ATO, 2025]. This restriction does not apply to capital works deductions, only to Division 40 assets.

### Effective life

Each depreciating asset has an effective life set by the ATO, which determines how quickly it declines in value. For example, a carpet might have an effective life of 10 years, while an air conditioner might have an effective life of 15 years [Source: ATO, 2024]. You can choose between the diminishing value method or the prime cost method to calculate the decline in value [Source: ATO, 2024].

### Low-value pooling

You can allocate low-cost and low-value assets to a low-value pool to simplify your calculations [Source: ATO, 2025].

- A **low-cost asset** is one that costs less than $1,000 [Source: ATO, 2025].
- A **low-value asset** is one that is not a low-cost asset but has an opening adjustable value of less than $1,000 and was previously depreciated using the diminishing value method [Source: ATO, 2025].

Assets in a low-value pool are depreciated at 37.5% per year using the diminishing value method. In the first year you add an asset to the pool, the rate is 18.75% [Source: ATO, 2025].

Assets that cost $300 or less and are used mainly to produce non-business assessable income may qualify for an immediate deduction instead of being allocated to a low-value pool [Source: ATO, 2025].

## Negative gearing

Negative gearing applies when your deductible rental expenses exceed your rental income, producing a net rental loss [Source: ATO, 2025].

### How it works

When your property is negatively geared, you can offset the net rental loss against your other assessable income, such as salary or wages. This reduces your overall taxable income and the amount of tax you pay [Source: ATO, 2025].

If your other income is not enough to absorb the loss, you can carry the remaining loss forward to future income years [Source: ATO, 2025].

### What counts towards the loss

Your rental deductions include cash expenses such as interest, council rates, insurance, and property management fees, as well as non-cash deductions such as depreciation and capital works deductions [Source: ATO, 2025].

**Worked example:** Your rental income for the year is $25,000. Your cash expenses (interest, rates, insurance, repairs, management fees) total $30,000. Your depreciation and capital works deductions total $8,000. Your total deductions are $38,000, giving you a net rental loss of $13,000. If your marginal tax rate is 32.5% (plus 2% Medicare levy), this loss would produce a tax saving of approximately $4,485 [Source: ATO, 2025].

### Important considerations

Negative gearing is a tax outcome, not an investment strategy in itself. The tax benefit reduces your cash shortfall but does not eliminate it. You still need to fund the gap between rental income and expenses from other sources. Discuss your personal situation with a registered tax agent or financial adviser.

## Capital gains tax

When you sell or otherwise dispose of a rental property, you may make a capital gain or capital loss [Source: ATO, 2025]. Capital gains tax (CGT) is not a separate tax. It forms part of your income tax.

### When CGT applies

CGT applies to rental properties acquired on or after 20 September 1985. If you acquired the property before that date, it is generally exempt from CGT, though major improvements made after 20 September 1985 may still be subject to CGT [Source: ATO, 2025].

The CGT event occurs when you enter into the contract of sale, not at settlement [Source: ATO, 2025].

### The 50% discount

If you are an Australian resident individual and you have owned the property for more than 12 months, you may be entitled to reduce your capital gain by 50% before including it in your assessable income [Source: ATO, 2025]. This is known as the CGT discount.

### Calculating your cost base

Your cost base is what it cost you to acquire and hold the property. It includes five elements:

1. **Purchase price**: the amount you paid for the property [Source: ATO, 2025].
2. **Incidental costs**: stamp duty, legal fees, valuation fees, and selling costs such as agent commissions [Source: ATO, 2025].
3. **Ownership costs**: rates, insurance, and land tax (only if you did not claim these as deductions) [Source: ATO, 2025].
4. **Capital improvements**: costs of renovations and improvements that were not claimed as deductions [Source: ATO, 2025].
5. **Title protection costs**: legal fees to defend your ownership of the property [Source: ATO, 2025].

You must reduce your cost base by any capital works deductions and decline in value deductions you have claimed (or could have claimed) [Source: ATO, 2025].

**Worked example:** Karl and Louisa bought a rental property for $750,000 and incurred $30,000 in stamp duty and legal fees. They later spent $6,000 on a new fence. Over 7 years of ownership, they claimed $35,000 in capital works deductions and $5,000 in decline in value deductions. They sold the property for $900,000 with selling costs of $10,000. Their cost base is $750,000 + $30,000 + $6,000 + $10,000 - $35,000 - $5,000 = $756,000. Their capital gain is $900,000 - $756,000 = $144,000. After applying the 50% discount, the taxable capital gain is $72,000, split equally between them as joint owners ($36,000 each) [Source: ATO, 2025].

### Main residence exemption partial application

If the property was ever your main residence, you may be entitled to a full or partial main residence exemption [Source: ATO, 2025].

- If you lived in the property first and then rented it out, you can treat it as your main residence for up to 6 years while it produces income [Source: ATO, 2025].
- If you rent out part of your main residence (for example, a room), you may be entitled to a partial main residence exemption based on the floor area and time used to produce income [Source: ATO, 2024].

## Records you must keep

The ATO requires you to keep records of all income and expenses related to your rental property [Source: ATO, 2025].

### How long to keep records

You must keep your records for 5 years from the date you lodge your tax return. For capital gains tax purposes, you must keep records for 5 years after the tax return that includes the CGT event is lodged [Source: ATO, 2025]. You must also keep records from the date you acquire the property until 5 years after the disposal to properly calculate any capital gain or loss [Source: ATO, 2025].

### What records to keep

- Purchase contract and settlement statement
- Loan documents and bank statements showing interest payments
- Receipts for all deductible expenses
- Property manager statements
- Rental income records and bank statements
- Depreciation schedule from your quantity surveyor
- Records of capital improvements and renovations
- Tenancy agreements and advertising records [Source: ATO, 2025]

Records can be kept in paper or digital form. They must be in English or readily translatable into English. The ATO recommends keeping digital backups [Source: ATO, 2025].

## Common mistakes

The ATO identifies several common errors made by rental property owners [Source: ATO, 2025].

### Claiming personal use portions

If you use the property for private purposes, even for short periods, you must apportion your expenses. You cannot claim deductions for periods when the property was used privately [Source: ATO, 2025]. Under draft PCG 2025/D6, expenses must be apportioned using time-based or area-based methods (or both) depending on your situation [Source: PCG 2025/D6, 2025].

### Mixing private and rental expenses

Interest on a loan used partly for private purposes must be apportioned. Once you redraw from an investment loan for personal use, you must apportion interest for the remaining life of the loan [Source: ATO, 2025]. Consider using separate loans for investment and private purposes to avoid complexity.

### Incorrect interest claims

You can only claim interest on the portion of a loan used to purchase, improve, or maintain the rental property. Interest on borrowings used to acquire personal assets or pay private expenses is not deductible [Source: ATO, 2025].

### Other common errors

- Claiming deductions for a property that is not genuinely available for rent
- Confusing repairs (immediately deductible) with improvements (depreciated over time)
- Claiming depreciation on second-hand plant and equipment acquired after 9 May 2017
- Not declaring insurance payouts or retained bond money as income
- Failing to keep adequate records to substantiate claims [Source: ATO, 2025]

## GST and property

The GST treatment of property depends on the type of property and the nature of the transaction [Source: GSTR 2000/20].

### When GST does not apply

The lease of residential premises for long-term accommodation is an **input-taxed supply**. This means you do not charge GST on residential rent, and you cannot claim input tax credits for GST included in expenses related to the rental [Source: ATO, 2000]. This applies to:

- Long-term residential tenancy agreements
- Holiday homes and investment properties rented to tenants
- Properties leased through sharing economy platforms where the property is used as residential accommodation [Source: GSTR 2000/20]

The sale of existing residential premises (where the property has been occupied as a residence before) is also input taxed [Source: ATO, 2000].

### When GST does apply

- **Sale of new residential premises**: GST applies to the sale of newly constructed homes or substantially renovated properties that have not been sold as residential premises before [Source: ATO, 2000].
- **Commercial property**: the sale and lease of commercial property is generally taxable and subject to GST, unless the going concern exemption applies [Source: ATO, 2000].
- **Property development**: property developers must account for GST on sales of new residential and commercial property and can claim input tax credits on construction costs [Source: ATO, 2000].

Residential rental property investors generally do not need to register for GST solely because they receive residential rent. If you also conduct a property development business or lease commercial property, GST obligations may apply [Source: ATO, 2000].
