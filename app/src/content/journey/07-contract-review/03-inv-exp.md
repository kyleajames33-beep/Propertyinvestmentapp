# Advanced contract structures and strategies for experienced investors

Experienced investors approach contract review with a focus on risk allocation, tax efficiency, portfolio coordination, and transaction speed. The standard Contract for Sale and Purchase of Land 2022 edition is merely a starting point. This guide covers the advanced contract matters that experienced investors in NSW encounter, including development conditions, commercial lease reviews, portfolio acquisitions, trust execution, off-the-plan risks, and negotiated special conditions. The content is educational only. Engage your conveyancer, accountant, and tax specialist to advise on specific transactions.

## Advanced contract structures

Experienced investors rarely accept the standard contract terms without negotiation. The contract structure should reflect your investment strategy, ownership vehicle, and risk tolerance. Key structural decisions include the timing of exchange, the settlement period, deposit arrangements, and the conditions precedent that must be satisfied before the contract becomes unconditional.

One advanced structure is the simultaneous exchange and settlement, where the contract is exchanged and settled on the same day. This eliminates the risk period between exchange and settlement and is useful when you are refinancing or completing a back-to-back transaction. However, simultaneous settlement requires all parties to be fully prepared and carries significant penalties if any party defaults.

Another structure is the nominee arrangement, where you exchange contracts in one entity's name and later nominate a different entity as the purchaser. This can be useful for portfolio restructures or when you are still finalising your ownership vehicle. Be aware that nomination clauses attract stamp duty scrutiny from Revenue NSW. The nomination must be genuine and not a device to avoid duty. Your conveyancer can structure the nomination to comply with current revenue rulings.

Options to purchase are another tool for experienced investors. An option agreement gives you the right to purchase the property at a predetermined price within a specified period. You pay an option fee for this right. Options are useful for development sites where you need time to secure approvals, or for properties where you want to lock in a price while you arrange finance. The option agreement and the contract of sale are separate documents, and both require careful drafting.

## Development-related conditions

If you are purchasing a property for development, the contract should include conditions that protect your ability to obtain the necessary approvals. A standard subject to development approval condition allows you to rescind if council refuses your development application. However, the wording of this condition is critical. Some conditions require you to submit the application within a short timeframe or to accept conditions imposed by council. Your conveyancer should negotiate terms that give you adequate time and flexibility.

Due diligence for development sites extends beyond the standard building and pest inspection. You should obtain a full suite of planning certificates, including section 10.7(2) and (5) certificates, a drainage diagram, a survey of the site, and a geotechnical report if the site has stability issues. If the site is contaminated, you may need an environmental assessment and a remediation plan. These investigations should be built into the contract as conditions precedent.

If the development involves demolition of existing structures, check whether the contract requires you to accept responsibility for asbestos or other hazardous materials. Demolition costs for asbestos-containing buildings can be substantial. Some vendors offer the property on an "as is" basis, which passes all environmental liability to you. Your conveyancer can negotiate a retention or indemnity from the vendor for known contamination.

For major developments, consider a development agreement rather than a standard contract of sale. A development agreement sets out the terms on which the vendor and developer will work together to obtain approvals and complete the project. These agreements are complex and require specialised legal advice. They typically address profit sharing, approval rights, timeframes, and what happens if the project does not proceed.

## Commercial lease review considerations

Experienced investors often acquire commercial properties with existing tenancies. The quality of the lease determines the income stream and the value of the asset. A thorough lease review should be conducted before you exchange contracts, not after. Your commercial lawyer or conveyancer should review every lease document and flag issues that affect your investment.

Key lease terms to review include the rent and rent review mechanism. Rent reviews may be fixed increases, CPI-linked, or market-based. Market reviews can lead to disputes and should specify a dispute resolution mechanism. Check the remaining lease term and any options the tenant holds. A tenant with a five-year lease and no options provides short-term income security but no long-term certainty. Conversely, a tenant with multiple options may lock in below-market rent for years.

Outgoings recovery is another critical area. Net leases require the tenant to pay all outgoings, including council rates, insurance, and maintenance. Gross leases include outgoings in the rent. Check what the lease allows you to recover and whether there are caps or exclusions. Some leases exclude capital works or structural repairs from outgoings recovery, which shifts these costs to you.

Review the tenant's make-good obligations at the end of the lease. Make-good clauses require the tenant to return the premises to their original condition. Weak make-good obligations may leave you with significant refurbishment costs between tenancies. Also check the bank guarantee or security deposit. A bank guarantee equal to three to six months' rent is standard for commercial leases. Verify that the guarantee is transferable to you at settlement.

## Portfolio acquisition contract coordination

Experienced investors often acquire multiple properties in a short period or as part of a portfolio purchase. Coordinating the contracts for these acquisitions requires careful planning to manage settlement timing, finance availability, and cash flow.

If you are purchasing multiple properties from the same vendor, consider whether to structure the transaction as separate contracts or a single contract. Separate contracts give you flexibility to settle each property independently, but they also mean separate stamp duty, separate conveyancing fees, and separate settlement processes. A single contract can streamline the process but creates interdependence between the properties. If one property fails due diligence, the entire contract may be at risk.

Settlement timing is critical for portfolio acquisitions. If you are using the equity from one property to fund the deposit on another, the settlements must be sequenced correctly. Your conveyancer can coordinate with your lender to ensure funds are available when needed and to arrange simultaneous or consecutive settlements. PEXA, the electronic settlement platform, allows for linked settlements where the proceeds from one sale are applied to a purchase on the same day.

Cross-collateralisation is another consideration. If you are using multiple properties as security for a single loan facility, the lender will register mortgages over all properties. This means a default on one property could trigger enforcement across the entire portfolio. Your mortgage broker and conveyancer can advise on whether to cross-collateralise or to keep loans separate for each property.

## Trust and corporate structures: contract execution

Experienced investors typically hold properties through complex structures involving discretionary trusts, unit trusts, self-managed superannuation funds, or proprietary limited companies. Each structure has specific requirements for contract execution that must be complied with for the contract to be enforceable.

For proprietary limited companies, the contract must be executed by directors in accordance with the Corporations Act 2001. A company with a sole director can execute by that director signing alone. A company with multiple directors requires two directors or a director and a company secretary to sign. If the company executes through an attorney, the power of attorney must be registered if it relates to land. Your conveyancer will verify that the execution clause is correct and that the signatories have authority.

For trusts, the trustee must sign in their capacity as trustee. The full name of the trust should appear on the contract, and the trustee should sign using the format "[Trustee name] as trustee for [Trust name]." The trustee should also include a statement that they have power under the trust deed to enter into the contract. If the trustee is a company, both the company execution requirements and the trust capacity must be satisfied.

Self-managed superannuation funds have additional requirements. The fund's investment strategy must permit property investment, and the purchase must comply with the sole purpose test. If the fund is borrowing through a limited recourse borrowing arrangement, the contract must be in the correct name, which is typically the trustee of the bare trust that holds the property on behalf of the fund. SMSF borrowing is heavily regulated, and incorrect contract execution can invalidate the arrangement. Your SMSF adviser and conveyancer must work together to ensure compliance.

## Off-the-plan contracts: sunset clauses and risks

Off-the-plan purchases involve buying a property that has not yet been built or subdivided. The contract is typically lengthy and heavily favours the developer. Experienced investors should approach off-the-plan contracts with caution and negotiate amendments where possible.

The sunset clause specifies the date by which the developer must complete the project and register the plan. If the developer misses this date, either party can rescind the contract. In practice, developers often invoke sunset clauses to rescind when market prices have risen, leaving buyers without a property and with only their deposit returned. The Conveyancing Legislation Amendment Act 2018 introduced protections for buyers, requiring developers to obtain the buyer's consent or a court order before rescinding under a sunset clause. However, these protections do not eliminate the risk entirely. Your conveyancer can negotiate a sunset clause that gives you, but not the developer, the right to rescind if the project is delayed.

Another risk in off-the-plan contracts is the developer's ability to vary the plans. Most contracts allow the developer to make minor variations to the design, layout, and finishes without your consent. What constitutes a "minor" variation is often vaguely defined. Your conveyancer can negotiate a clause that requires your consent for variations that exceed a specified threshold, such as changes to floor area greater than 5 percent or changes to the number of rooms.

The contract should also specify what happens if the developer defaults or becomes insolvent. Some contracts limit your remedies to a refund of your deposit, even if you have incurred other costs. Your conveyancer can negotiate for additional remedies, such as the right to claim damages or to have the deposit held by an independent stakeholder rather than the developer.

## Special conditions experienced investors negotiate

Experienced investors use special conditions to shift risk to the vendor and to create flexibility in the transaction. The following special conditions are commonly negotiated in investment transactions.

**Due diligence conditions.** A comprehensive due diligence condition gives you a period, typically 21 to 30 days, to conduct all investigations and to rescind for any reason. Unlike a finance clause, you do not need to specify what was unsatisfactory. This is valuable for commercial properties and development sites where investigations are complex.

**Rent roll verification.** For properties with multiple tenancies, such as boarding houses or apartment blocks, a condition requiring verification of the rent roll ensures that the rental income stated by the vendor is accurate. You can rescind if the actual rent roll differs materially from the disclosure.

**Vacant possession condition.** If you require vacant possession to undertake renovations or re-letting, include a condition requiring the vendor to deliver the property vacant at settlement. For commercial properties, this may involve negotiating with existing tenants for early termination.

**Non-recourse conditions.** In some transactions, particularly options and development agreements, you may negotiate a condition limiting your liability to the deposit if you exercise your right to rescind. This prevents the vendor from claiming damages beyond the forfeited deposit.

**Confidentiality conditions.** For significant acquisitions or portfolio purchases, you may require the vendor to keep the transaction terms confidential. This prevents market speculation and protects your negotiating position on other acquisitions.

Your conveyancer can draft these conditions to ensure they are enforceable and tailored to your specific transaction. Special conditions should be precise and unambiguous. Vague or poorly drafted conditions may not provide the protection you intend.

## Fast settlement strategies

Experienced investors sometimes need to settle quickly to secure a property, take advantage of a motivated vendor, or align the acquisition with other transactions. Fast settlements, typically 14 days or less, require preparation and coordination.

To execute a fast settlement, have your finance pre-approved and your loan documents ready before exchange. Use a lender with a track record of rapid processing. Some non-bank lenders specialise in fast settlements for experienced investors, albeit at higher interest rates. Your mortgage broker can advise on the best lender for your timeframe.

Engage your conveyancer early, ideally before you make an offer. Provide all identification, trust deeds, and corporate documents in advance so your conveyancer can prepare the transfer and settlement documents without delay. Conduct your due diligence before exchange rather than after. This means obtaining building reports, strata records, and planning certificates before you commit.

If you cannot complete your full due diligence before exchange, consider a split exchange where you exchange unconditionally but with a short settlement period, using the settlement period to complete your investigations. This is risky and should only be considered when you are confident in the property and have contingency funds available if issues arise.

## Risk allocation through contract terms

The contract is the primary tool for allocating risk between the buyer and the vendor. Experienced investors approach risk allocation systematically, identifying each risk and negotiating contract terms that place the risk with the party best able to manage it.

**Title risk.** The standard contract places the risk of title defects on the vendor, who warrants that they have good title. Special conditions sometimes limit this warranty. Ensure that the vendor's title obligations are not watered down and that you have the right to rescind if a title defect cannot be rectified.

**Physical risk.** Risk of damage to the property passes to you on exchange. Arrange insurance from exchange and consider negotiating a retention from the purchase price if you identify defects that the vendor agrees to repair before settlement.

**Planning risk.** For development properties, planning risk is significant. A subject to development approval condition allocates this risk to the vendor if approval is refused. You can also negotiate a due diligence condition that allows you to rescind if planning investigations reveal constraints that make the development unviable.

**Tenant risk.** For commercial properties with existing tenancies, tenant risk includes default, vacancy, and lease expiry. Review the lease terms, the tenant's financial capacity, and the rental market before committing. A rent guarantee from the vendor for a period after settlement can mitigate tenant default risk.

**Environmental risk.** Contaminated land and hazardous materials can impose significant liability on the owner. A due diligence condition with specific environmental investigations, together with an indemnity from the vendor for known contamination, can protect you from inherited liability.

Your conveyancer and tax specialist can review the risk allocation in the contract and suggest amendments. The goal is not to eliminate all risk, which is impossible, but to ensure that the risks you accept are priced into your investment analysis and that you are not taking risks that the vendor is better placed to bear.

## Conveyancer, accountant, and tax specialist referral

Advanced contract structures require specialised professional advice. A conveyancer experienced in complex transactions can negotiate and draft special conditions, coordinate multi-property settlements, and ensure your trust or corporate execution complies with all legal requirements. An accountant with property investment expertise can advise on the tax implications of your ownership structure, settlement timing, and deductions. A tax specialist can provide advice on GST, land tax, stamp duty concessions, and structuring strategies for large portfolios. These professionals work together to protect your interests and optimise your investment outcomes.

If you need to engage or upgrade your professional advisory team for an upcoming acquisition, you can request a referral to specialists who work with experienced investors on complex transactions.
