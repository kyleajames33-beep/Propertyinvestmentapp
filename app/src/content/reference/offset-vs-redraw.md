# Offset account vs redraw facility

Offset accounts and redraw facilities are two of the most common features attached to Australian home loans. Both can reduce the amount of interest you pay, but they work differently and come with distinct advantages, particularly around accessibility and tax treatment for investment properties.

This guide explains how each feature works, the key differences, and why the choice between them matters more for some borrowers than others.

---

## What is an offset account

An offset account is a transaction account linked to your home loan. The balance in this account is offset daily against your loan balance, and interest is charged only on the difference.

**How it works.** If you have a $600,000 home loan and keep $50,000 in your offset account, the lender calculates interest on $550,000, not $600,000. The $50,000 remains accessible as a normal transaction account. You can withdraw it at any time via debit card, ATM, or online transfer. When you do withdraw, the loan balance used for interest calculations returns to the full amount.

Offset accounts typically have their own BSB and account number. You can have your salary deposited directly, set up direct debits, and use the account for everyday spending. Most lenders offer a debit card linked to the offset account.

### Types of offset accounts

**100% offset.** The full balance in the account is offset against the loan. This is the most common type and offers the maximum interest reduction. Most lenders link 100% offset accounts to variable rate loans.

**Partial offset.** Only a portion of the account balance is offset against the loan. For example, a 50% partial offset on a $50,000 balance would only offset $25,000. Partial offsets are less common and are sometimes offered on fixed rate loans where a full offset is not available.

**Multiple offset accounts.** Some lenders allow you to link more than one offset account to a single loan. This can be useful for investors who want to segregate funds for different purposes (rent received, maintenance reserves, future deposits) while all balances work to reduce interest on the loan.

## What is a redraw facility

A redraw facility allows you to make repayments above the minimum required amount and then access those extra funds later if needed. The additional repayments reduce your loan balance and therefore the interest charged.

**How it works.** If your minimum monthly repayment is $2,500 and you pay $3,500 each month, the extra $1,000 accumulates as available redraw. Over 12 months, you build $12,000 in available redraw. You can request to withdraw these funds, typically via online banking, though some lenders require a formal request or branch visit.

Redraw facilities are usually available on variable rate loans. Some fixed rate loans offer limited redraw or none at all, particularly during the fixed term.

### Access methods

Access to redraw varies by lender:

- **Online banking.** Most lenders allow redraw requests through their online platform or mobile app. Processing times range from same-day to several business days.
- **Branch or phone.** Some lenders, particularly smaller institutions, require redraw requests to be made in-branch or over the phone.
- **Minimum amounts.** Many lenders impose minimum redraw amounts, commonly $500 or $1,000 per transaction.
- **Frequency limits.** Some lenders cap the number of free redraws per year, charging fees for additional withdrawals.

---

## Key differences

| Feature | Offset account | Redraw facility |
|---|---|---|
| Account structure | Separate transaction account | Funds held within the loan account |
| Access to funds | Immediate, debit card, ATM | Request-based, may have delays |
| Minimum withdrawal | Usually none | Often $500 to $1,000 |
| Interest calculation | Offset daily against loan balance | Extra repayments reduce loan principal |
| Lender restrictions | Funds are yours; lender cannot restrict access | Lender may restrict or suspend redraw access |
| Fees | Monthly account-keeping fees may apply | Per-transaction fees may apply |
| Tax treatment for investors | No effect on loan deductibility | Redraw for private use may reduce deductibility |

## Offset account advantages

**Liquidity and flexibility.** Funds in an offset account are immediately accessible. You can use a debit card for everyday purchases, withdraw cash from ATMs, and transfer funds online. This makes offset accounts suitable for emergency funds and short-term savings.

**Preserves loan purpose for tax.** Because funds in an offset account never touch the loan account, the original loan balance and purpose remain unchanged. For investment loans, this means the full interest remains tax deductible regardless of how you use the offset funds. This is the primary reason investors prefer offset accounts over redraw facilities. [Source: ATO TR 2000/2]

**Suitable for future investment property conversion.** If you plan to convert your owner-occupied home into an investment property in the future, keeping savings in an offset account (rather than making extra repayments into the loan) preserves a higher loan balance. When the property becomes an investment, the larger loan amount may be tax deductible against rental income. Consult a tax professional before making this decision.

**Multiple offsets for portfolio investors.** Investors with multiple properties can use multiple offset accounts to segregate rental income, maintenance reserves, and personal funds, all reducing interest on the linked loan.

---

## Offset account disadvantages

**Higher fees or interest rates.** Loans with offset accounts often come with higher annual fees or slightly higher interest rates than basic loans without this feature. You should compare the cost of the offset feature against the interest savings you expect to achieve.

**Requires discipline.** Because funds remain fully accessible, some borrowers find it harder to build wealth compared with making direct extra repayments into a loan.

---

## Redraw facility advantages

**Interest savings.** Extra repayments directly reduce the loan principal, which reduces the total interest paid over the life of the loan. If you are unlikely to need the funds back, redraw can be an effective forced savings mechanism.

**Lower fees.** Basic variable loans with redraw but no offset account typically have lower annual fees. If you rarely need to access extra funds and the interest rate saving is material, a redraw-only loan may be cost-effective.

**Redraw for investment purposes can create deductible debt.** If you redraw funds from your loan and use them solely to purchase income-producing assets (such as shares or an investment property), the interest on the redrawn amount may be tax deductible. This is the opposite scenario to the common contamination problem. Consult your accountant to confirm deductibility in your situation. [Source: ATO TR 2000/2]

---

## Redraw facility disadvantages

**Restricted access.** Redraw is at the lender's discretion. Some lenders have reduced or suspended redraw access during periods of market stress. Minimum redraw amounts and processing delays can also limit flexibility.

**Tax contamination risk for investors.** The Australian Taxation Office (ATO) treats redrawn funds as a new borrowing. If you redraw from an investment loan for private purposes (holidays, car purchase, home renovations on your principal residence), the interest on that portion of the loan ceases to be tax deductible. The loan becomes a "mixed purpose" account, and you must apportion interest between deductible and non-deductible portions on an ongoing basis. This is complex and can create ongoing compliance costs. [Source: ATO TR 2000/2, paragraphs 22 to 24]

**Reduced future deductibility for converted properties.** If you make extra repayments into your owner-occupied loan and later convert that property to an investment, you cannot redraw those extra funds to restore the higher loan balance for tax purposes. The reduced loan balance becomes the baseline for calculating deductible interest.

---

## Tax implications for investors: why the distinction matters

The ATO's position on loan interest deductibility is clear: deductibility depends on the purpose for which the borrowed funds are used, not the security for the loan or the type of loan product. [Source: ATO TR 2000/2]

### The redraw contamination problem

When you make extra repayments into a loan with a redraw facility, those funds become part of the loan's available credit. If you later redraw funds for a private purpose, the ATO considers this a new borrowing. If the original loan was for investment purposes, the redrawn amount used privately contaminates the loan.

**Example.** You have an $800,000 investment property loan. You make $200,000 in extra repayments, reducing the balance to $600,000. You then redraw $200,000 to renovate your own home. The ATO considers the loan now 75% deductible (the $600,000 remaining for investment) and 25% non-deductible (the $200,000 redrawn for private use). You must apportion all future interest accordingly. If you make further repayments, the apportionment becomes even more complex.

### How offset accounts preserve deductibility

With an offset account, the funds are separate from the loan. The full $800,000 loan balance remains outstanding for tax purposes, even though you only pay interest on the net amount. If you withdraw $200,000 from the offset account for a private purpose, the loan itself is unchanged. Interest on the full $800,000 remains deductible because the loan purpose has not changed.

This distinction makes offset accounts the preferred structure for most investment property loans where the borrower may need access to surplus funds.

---

## Which is better: owner-occupiers vs investors

### Owner-occupiers

For owner-occupiers, the tax implications are less relevant because the interest on a home loan for a principal residence is not tax deductible. The choice between offset and redraw comes down to flexibility, fees, and interest rates.

- Choose an offset account if you want maximum flexibility, hold significant cash reserves, or receive a large irregular income (bonuses, commissions) that you want to park against the loan while maintaining access.
- Choose a redraw-only loan if you prefer a lower interest rate or lower fees, do not need frequent access to extra funds, and are confident you will not convert the property to an investment in the future.

### Investors

For investment property loans, offset accounts are generally the preferred structure because they avoid tax contamination. The slightly higher cost of an offset feature is typically outweighed by the tax simplicity and preserved deductibility.

If you use a redraw facility on an investment loan, ensure any redrawn funds are used solely for income-producing purposes and keep detailed records of every withdrawal. Mixed purpose loans require ongoing apportionment of interest, which most investors find burdensome.

### Owner-occupiers who may become investors

If there is any chance you will convert your home to an investment property in the future (for example, you plan to upgrade and rent out your current home), an offset account is strongly preferable. Making extra repayments into the loan reduces the balance permanently; funds in an offset account can be withdrawn before conversion, leaving a higher deductible loan balance. Seek advice from an accountant before implementing this strategy. [Source: ATO]

## Multiple offset accounts: strategy for portfolio investors

Some lenders offer multiple offset accounts linked to a single loan or split loan structure. This feature can be valuable for property investors who want to:

- Segregate rental income from different properties into separate offsets
- Maintain a dedicated maintenance and repairs reserve
- Keep future deposit funds separate while reducing interest on existing loans
- Separate personal and investment funds for cleaner record-keeping

Not all lenders offer multiple offsets, and some charge additional account-keeping fees per offset account. If you are building a property portfolio, ask your broker which lenders support this structure and what the costs are.

## Questions to ask your lender or broker

1. Is the offset account a 100% offset or partial offset?
2. Does the offset account link to fixed rate, variable rate, or both?
3. What are the account-keeping fees for the offset account?
4. Is the loan interest rate higher because of the offset feature?
5. What are the minimum redraw amounts and fees?
6. Can the lender restrict or suspend redraw access?
7. For investment loans: how does the lender handle mixed purpose accounts if redraw is used for private purposes?
8. Does the lender offer multiple offset accounts? What are the costs?
9. How quickly are redraw funds available after a request?
10. Are there limits on the number of free redraw transactions per year?
