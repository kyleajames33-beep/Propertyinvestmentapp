# Making an offer and negotiating as a first home buyer in NSW

The offer and negotiation stage is where research, preparation, and emotional discipline come together. For first home buyers in New South Wales, understanding the mechanics of private treaty sales, auctions, cooling off periods, and negotiation strategy can mean the difference between securing a property at a fair price and overpaying under pressure.

This guide walks through each element of the NSW offer process so you can approach negotiation with confidence.

## Private treaty vs auction: understanding the two pathways

In NSW, residential property is sold through one of two methods: private treaty or auction. Each pathway follows different rules, timelines, and negotiation dynamics.

### Private treaty sales

A private treaty sale occurs when the vendor sets an asking price and invites offers from interested buyers. The property is listed with a price guide or "offers over" figure, and buyers submit written offers through the selling agent. The agent presents all offers to the vendor, who may accept, reject, or counter.

Under private treaty in NSW, contracts are typically exchanged with a 10% deposit paid at the time of exchange. From that point, a cooling off period begins (see below). This method allows for negotiation on price, settlement terms, and inclusions.

Private treaty sales generally move more slowly than auctions. This can work in your favour as a first home buyer because it allows time to conduct due diligence, secure formal finance approval, and negotiate conditions into the contract.

### Auction sales

An auction is a public sale where the property is sold to the highest bidder on a specified date. In NSW, auctions are governed by the Property, Stock and Business Agents Act 2002 and associated regulations. The auction must be conducted by a licensed auctioneer.

Key rules for NSW auctions include:

- The reserve price is the minimum price the vendor will accept. It is set before the auction and is not disclosed to bidders.
- If bidding does not reach the reserve, the property may be passed in. The highest bidder then has the first right to negotiate with the vendor.
- Vendor bids are permitted in NSW but must be disclosed by the auctioneer. The auctioneer may make up to three vendor bids on behalf of the vendor.
- Dummy bidding (where a non-genuine bid is made to inflate the price) is illegal in NSW.
- The auctioneer can set the bidding increments but must accept any reasonable bid in the final stages.
- Successful bidders must sign the contract and pay the deposit immediately. There is no cooling off period for auction sales.

### Key differences at a glance

The most important difference for first home buyers is the cooling off period. Private treaty purchases in NSW attract a five business day cooling off period. Auction purchases do not. Once the hammer falls at auction, you are legally bound to complete the purchase.

Private treaty sales allow conditional offers (subject to finance, building inspection, or other conditions). Auction sales are unconditional. This means you must have your finance fully approved, your deposit ready, and your due diligence complete before raising your hand at auction.

Auctions also create competitive pressure that private treaty sales do not. The public nature of bidding, the presence of other buyers, and the auctioneer’s performance can drive emotional decisions. Understanding this dynamic is essential to avoiding overpayment.

## Cooling off period in NSW

The cooling off period is one of the most important protections available to first home buyers in NSW. It applies to private treaty sales and gives you a window to reconsider your purchase after contracts have been exchanged.

### How the cooling off period works

In NSW, the cooling off period for residential property is five business days from the date contracts are exchanged. It does not apply to auctions, properties sold within two business days before a scheduled auction, or properties purchased by companies or trusts.

During the cooling off period, you may withdraw from the sale. If you do, you forfeit 0.25% of the purchase price to the vendor. On a $900,000 property, this amounts to $2,250. The remainder of your deposit must be refunded to you within 14 days.

If the five business days expire and you have not withdrawn, the contract becomes binding and you proceed to settlement.

### What the cooling off period covers and what it does not

The cooling off period gives you time to:

- Secure final unconditional finance approval from your lender
- Obtain a satisfactory building and pest inspection report
- Review the contract with your solicitor or conveyancer
- Consider any new information that has come to light since you made your offer

It does not protect you from:

- Auction purchases (no cooling off period applies)
- Changes of mind unrelated to due diligence
- Discovery of issues that were disclosed in the contract or vendor statement
- Market conditions changing after you have exchanged contracts

Some buyers attempt to negotiate a longer cooling off period (seven to ten days is common in slower markets). Others ask for the cooling off period to be waived to make their offer more attractive to the vendor. As a first home buyer, you should generally not waive the cooling off period unless you have unconditional finance approval and complete confidence in your due diligence.

### Getting a section 66W certificate

A section 66W certificate is a document signed by your solicitor or conveyancer waiving the cooling off period. Vendors and selling agents often request this when comparing offers, as it provides certainty that the sale will proceed. If you are asked to provide a section 66W certificate, speak with your legal representative about whether it is appropriate for your situation. You should only waive the cooling off period if your finance is unconditionally approved and all due diligence is complete.

## Making an offer: conditional vs unconditional

Your offer can be structured in two ways: conditional or unconditional. Each carries different weight with vendors and different levels of risk for you as the buyer.

### Conditional offers

A conditional offer is subject to specific conditions being met before the contract becomes binding. Common conditions include:

**Subject to finance:** The contract is conditional upon you obtaining formal loan approval from your lender by a specified date (typically 14 to 21 days). If finance is not approved, you may terminate the contract and your deposit is refunded. This is the most common condition for first home buyers.

**Subject to building and pest inspection:** The contract is conditional upon you obtaining a satisfactory building and pest inspection report. This allows you to identify structural defects, termite damage, or other issues before committing to the purchase.

**Subject to sale of existing property:** If you currently own a property, your offer may be conditional upon selling it. This condition is generally viewed unfavourably by vendors, particularly in competitive markets.

Conditional offers provide protection but are less attractive to vendors because they introduce uncertainty. A vendor comparing two similar offers will typically choose the unconditional one.

### Unconditional offers

An unconditional offer means you are committing to purchase the property with no conditions attached. The contract proceeds directly to exchange and settlement. Unconditional offers are preferred by vendors because they provide certainty and a faster timeline.

As a first home buyer, you should only make an unconditional offer if:

- You have formal, written, unconditional finance approval from your lender (not just pre-approval)
- You have completed a building and pest inspection prior to making the offer
- Your solicitor or conveyancer has reviewed the contract and identified no issues
- You have your deposit funds readily available
- You understand that there is no cooling off period protection (though this is less relevant since you have already done your due diligence)

### Strategies for conditional offers in competitive situations

In a competitive market, you may need to make your conditional offer more attractive. Consider these approaches:

- Reduce the timeframe for conditions. Instead of 21 days for finance approval, ask your lender if 10 to 14 days is achievable.
- Complete your building inspection before making the offer. This allows you to make the offer unconditional on inspection while still protecting yourself.
- Offer a higher deposit. A 10% deposit is standard, but offering 5% with a guarantee for the balance at settlement may be accepted.
- Accompany your offer with a personal letter to the vendor. While this does not carry legal weight, it can create an emotional connection that influences the vendor’s decision.

## Auction bidding: preparation, strategy, and what to expect

Buying at auction requires preparation that begins weeks before the auction date. The following steps will help you approach auction day with confidence.

### Pre-auction preparation

**Secure unconditional finance approval:** Meet with your mortgage broker or lender at least three to four weeks before the auction. Request formal unconditional approval, not just pre-approval. Your lender should assess your full financial position, including income verification, credit checks, and property valuation. Ask your lender to confirm the maximum amount you can borrow and ensure you have a buffer below that figure for your auction limit.

**Conduct building and pest inspections:** Arrange pre-auction inspections so you know the property’s condition before you bid. The cost of inspections (typically $400 to $800) is a small price for the confidence it provides. If serious defects are identified, factor repair costs into your auction limit or decide not to bid.

**Review the contract:** Engage a solicitor or conveyancer to review the contract of sale and vendor disclosure statement at least one week before auction. They will check for unusual clauses, easements, zoning restrictions, or special conditions that may affect your purchase.

**Register to bid:** In NSW, you must register to bid at an auction. Registration is usually available 30 minutes before the auction begins. You will need to provide identification and receive a bidder number. If you cannot attend in person, you may appoint someone to bid on your behalf using a written authority. A buyer's agent can also bid for you.

**Set your auction limit:** Determine the absolute maximum you are willing to pay based on comparable sales, your budget, and the property’s condition. Write this figure down and commit to it. Do not exceed this limit on auction day, regardless of competitive pressure.

**Observe other auctions:** Attend two to three auctions in the area in the weeks leading up to your target auction. Observe bidding patterns, how the auctioneer works the crowd, and how properties are passing in or selling. This familiarity reduces anxiety and improves your bidding technique.

### Bidding strategy

**Opening bid:** There is debate about whether to open the bidding or wait. Opening with a strong bid near the bottom of the price guide can signal confidence and knock out hesitant buyers. However, it also reveals your interest early. Consider the crowd size and atmosphere before deciding.

**Bid increments:** Bid in confident increments that reflect the property's price point. On a property priced around $900,000, bids of $10,000 to $25,000 are typical early on, reducing to $5,000 or $1,000 increments near the reserve. Bid firmly and call your bid out clearly with the amount.

**Body language:** Stand where the auctioneer can see you clearly. Make eye contact when bidding. Avoid nervous gestures or discussions with companions that reveal hesitation.

**Counter-bidding technique:** If another bidder jumps ahead of you, pause briefly before countering. This creates the impression you are reaching your limit. Bid quickly when the auctioneer calls "going once, going twice" to maintain pressure on competitors.

**Vendor bids:** When the auctioneer makes a vendor bid, this is a signal that bidding has not yet reached the reserve. Do not be deterred. Continue bidding based on your limit.

**When to stop:** When bidding reaches your pre-determined limit, stop. Do not make a final emotional bid "just in case." Properties that sell above your limit are not your property. There will be other opportunities.

### What happens if the property passes in

If bidding does not reach the reserve price, the property is passed in. The auctioneer will negotiate with the highest bidder first. As the highest bidder, you have the advantage of being the only party in negotiation at that moment. You may be able to negotiate a purchase below the reserve price if the vendor is motivated to sell.

If you are not the highest bidder, approach the selling agent after the auction and register your interest. The property may come back onto the market within days if the highest bidder and vendor cannot agree.

## Negotiation basics: anchoring, increments, and when to walk away

Negotiation is a skill that improves with practice. Understanding a few core concepts will help you negotiate more effectively as a first home buyer.

### Anchoring

Anchoring is the psychological tendency to rely heavily on the first number presented in a negotiation. In property, the asking price or price guide serves as an anchor. Your job is to reset that anchor with evidence.

Research comparable sales (comps) in the area for the past three to six months. Look for properties of similar size, condition, and location. Use these sales to justify your offer price. Presenting evidence of comparable sales to the selling agent demonstrates that your offer is based on market reality, not lowballing.

When making an offer below the asking price, provide reasoning. For example: "We are offering $850,000 based on comparable sales at 12 Smith Street ($845,000) and 8 Jones Avenue ($860,000), both sold within the last eight weeks."

### Negotiation increments

When counter-offering, use increments that signal your position. Large jumps suggest you have more room to move. Small increments signal you are near your limit.

If the asking price is $900,000 and you open at $850,000, a vendor counter of $890,000 might be met with your counter of $865,000 (a $15,000 jump showing some movement). If the vendor comes back at $885,000, your counter of $872,000 (a smaller $13,000 jump) signals you are tightening.

Avoid odd numbers that suggest you have stretched to your absolute limit (e.g., $873,450). Round numbers project confidence and room to negotiate.

### When to walk away

Walking away is a legitimate and powerful negotiation tactic. You should walk away when:

- The vendor's lowest price exceeds your pre-determined maximum based on comparable sales
- The property requires significant repairs that cannot be factored into the price
- The selling agent is using high-pressure tactics or misleading information
- You sense you are being drawn into a competitive bidding situation designed to inflate the price
- Your due diligence reveals issues that make the property unsuitable

Walking away preserves your deposit, your borrowing capacity, and your emotional wellbeing. There are always other properties.

## Building inspection results into your offer strategy

A building and pest inspection is one of the most valuable tools in your negotiation arsenal. The key is timing: whether you conduct the inspection before making an offer or make your offer conditional upon inspection.

### Pre-offer inspections

Conducting your inspection before making an offer gives you the most leverage. You know exactly what issues exist and can factor repair costs into your offer. If significant defects are found, you can reduce your offer accordingly or walk away without having committed to the purchase.

The downside is the cost. At $400 to $800 per inspection, inspecting multiple properties before purchase adds up. However, this cost is minor compared to the cost of unexpected repairs after purchase.

### Conditional inspection clauses

If you cannot inspect before making an offer (common in competitive markets), include a building and pest inspection condition in your contract. Set a short timeframe (five to seven days) for the inspection to be completed. If significant issues are found, you have three options:

1. Request that the vendor rectify the issues before settlement
2. Request a price reduction equal to the estimated repair cost
3. Terminate the contract if the issues are serious enough

Be reasonable in your requests. Minor maintenance issues (loose guttering, cracked tiles) are not grounds for termination. Structural defects, active termite infestations, or illegal structures are.

### Quantifying repair costs

Obtain written quotes for any repairs identified in the inspection report. Use these quotes as the basis for your price reduction request. For example: "The building inspection identified $18,000 in necessary roof repairs. We request a price reduction of $18,000 or that the vendor completes the repairs before settlement."

## Finance clauses: protecting yourself

The subject to finance clause is the most important protection for first home buyers making conditional offers.

### How subject to finance clauses work

A standard subject to finance clause states that the contract is conditional upon the buyer obtaining loan approval for a specified amount by a specified date. The clause should specify:

- The loan amount required
- The lender (or "a lender of the buyer's choice")
- The date by which finance must be approved
- The process for notifying the vendor if finance is not obtained

If you are unable to obtain finance by the deadline, you must notify the vendor (or their solicitor) in writing. The contract is then terminated and your deposit refunded.

### Common mistakes with finance clauses

**Assuming pre-approval is sufficient:** Pre-approval (or conditional approval) is not a guarantee of lending. Your lender will conduct a full assessment including property valuation before granting unconditional approval. Do not make unconditional offers based on pre-approval alone.

**Missing the deadline:** Mark the finance deadline in your calendar and follow up with your lender regularly. Missing the deadline without notifying the vendor can result in the contract becoming unconditional.

**Not understanding the loan amount specified:** Ensure the loan amount in the clause matches what you need to borrow. If you need to borrow more than specified, you may not be able to terminate the contract if the additional amount is not approved.

### Protecting your deposit

Your deposit is held in a trust account by the real estate agent or vendor's solicitor until settlement. In NSW, deposits are typically 10% of the purchase price. If the sale proceeds to settlement, the deposit is applied to the purchase price. If the sale does not proceed due to a valid condition not being met, the deposit is refunded.

To protect your deposit:

- Never pay a deposit directly to the vendor. Always pay to the agent's or solicitor's trust account.
- Obtain a receipt for your deposit payment.
- Ensure your deposit is covered by the deposit bond or bank guarantee if you are using one.

## Emotional discipline at auction and in negotiation

The biggest risk for first home buyers in the offer stage is emotional decision-making. Fear of missing out, attachment to a particular property, and competitive pressure can lead to overpayment and regret.

### Set your limits before you engage

Determine your maximum purchase price before entering any negotiation or auction. Base this figure on comparable sales, your budget, and your lender's pre-approval. Write it down. Share it with a trusted friend or family member who can hold you accountable.

### Recognise emotional triggers

Selling agents are skilled at creating urgency. Phrases like "we have multiple offers" or "the vendor wants this sold today" are designed to pressure you into quick decisions. While these statements may be true, they do not change your budget or the property's value.

Take time to consider any offer or counter-offer. If an agent pressures you to respond immediately, that is a signal to pause, not to act.

### Auction day discipline

Auctions are specifically designed to create emotional engagement. The crowd, the auctioneer's patter, and the competitive nature of bidding all work against rational decision-making.

Attend auctions with a support person who knows your limit and is committed to enforcing it. Do not attend alone if you are concerned about maintaining discipline.

If you reach your limit, stop bidding. Turn away from the auctioneer and leave the area if necessary. The property is not worth financial stress.

### Managing disappointment

You will lose properties. Other buyers will pay more, offer better terms, or simply act faster. This is part of the buying process. After each loss, review what happened, adjust your strategy if needed, and move on to the next property.

## What happens when your offer is accepted

When your offer is accepted (whether private treaty or auction), a specific sequence of events follows.

### Step 1: Contract exchange

The vendor's solicitor and your solicitor exchange contracts. This means each party signs an identical copy of the contract, and the copies are swapped. At this point, the sale becomes legally binding (subject to any conditions in a private treaty sale).

You pay the deposit at exchange. This is typically 10% of the purchase price, paid by bank transfer or deposit bond.

### Step 2: Cooling off period (private treaty only)

If you purchased by private treaty, the five business day cooling off period begins at exchange. During this time, your solicitor will conduct final searches, your lender will process your loan application, and you should arrange a final inspection.

### Step 3: Unconditional status

Once all conditions are satisfied (or if there were no conditions), the contract becomes unconditional. You are now committed to completing the purchase.

### Step 4: Settlement

Settlement typically occurs 42 days after exchange in NSW, though this can be negotiated. At settlement, your lender provides the loan funds, you provide the balance of the purchase price, and the vendor transfers ownership to you. Your solicitor will notify you once settlement is complete and you can collect the keys.

### Step 5: Final inspection

Arrange a final inspection in the days before settlement. Check that the property is in the same condition as when you inspected it, that all inclusions (dishwasher, light fittings) remain, and that any agreed repairs have been completed.

## Common mistakes first home buyers make in negotiation

Understanding common mistakes helps you avoid them.

**Falling in love with one property:** Emotional attachment leads to overpayment and poor negotiation. Maintain perspective. There are always other properties.

**Not doing comparable sales research:** Making offers without understanding recent sales in the area leaves you vulnerable. Agents know the market. You need to as well.

**Revealing your maximum budget to the agent:** Anything you tell the selling agent will be used to the vendor's advantage. Keep your cards close. If asked your budget, respond that you are "considering properties in this price range" without specifying your limit.

**Making lowball offers without justification:** An offer significantly below market value with no supporting evidence will be dismissed. If making a low offer, explain why (comparable sales, property condition, market conditions).

**Not reading the contract:** Your solicitor exists to protect you. Use them. Never sign a contract without legal review.

**Letting FOMO drive decisions:** Fear of missing out is the enemy of good negotiation. If you miss one property, another will come along.

## When to consider a buyer's agent

A buyer's agent represents your interests in the property purchase. For first home buyers, the most valuable service a buyer's agent provides is auction bidding support. If you are uncomfortable with the auction process, a buyer's agent can bid on your behalf using a proven strategy.

Buyer's agents also assist with property search, due diligence, and negotiation. They are particularly valuable if you are buying interstate, have limited time, or want professional guidance through your first purchase.

Buyer's agents charge either a fixed fee or a percentage of the purchase price (typically 1% to 2%). This cost should be weighed against the potential savings from better negotiation and the peace of mind from professional representation.
