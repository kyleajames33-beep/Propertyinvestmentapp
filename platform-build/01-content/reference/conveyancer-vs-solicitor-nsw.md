# Conveyancer vs solicitor for NSW property transactions

Buying or selling property in New South Wales involves a series of legal steps collectively known as conveyancing. Every property transaction in NSW must go through this process to transfer legal ownership from seller to buyer. The two types of professionals who can handle this work are licensed conveyancers and solicitors. This guide explains what each does, how they differ, and how to choose the right one for your situation.

## What does a conveyancer do

A licensed conveyancer is a specialist who focuses exclusively on property transactions. Their work is confined to the preparation of legal documents, the management of settlement processes, and the lodgement of documents with Land Registry Services NSW. [Source: Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division, 2024]

The specific tasks a conveyancer performs include:

- **Reviewing the contract for sale and purchase of land**: The conveyancer examines the contract, identifies special conditions, flags risks, and advises on necessary amendments before the buyer signs.
- **Conducting property searches**: They order searches from local councils, water authorities, and government departments to reveal easements, zoning restrictions, outstanding rates, and other matters affecting the property.
- **Preparing and certifying legal documents**: This includes the transfer of land, stamp duty declarations, and any other documentation required for registration.
- **Calculating financial adjustments**: They work out the division of council rates, water charges, strata levies, and other outgoings between buyer and seller as at the settlement date.
- **Arranging and attending settlement**: The conveyancer coordinates with the other party's representative, banks, and any other stakeholders to ensure the settlement proceeds on time.
- **Liaising with the lender**: They ensure the mortgage documents are in order and that the lender is ready to advance funds at settlement.
- **Lodging documents for registration**: After settlement, the conveyancer lodges the transfer and mortgage with NSW Land Registry Services to formally record the change of ownership.
- **Paying stamp duty**: They calculate and arrange payment of stamp duty (now called transfer duty) to Revenue NSW on the buyer's behalf.

Conveyancers are specialists. They do not handle matters outside their area of training and licensing, which is why their services are typically more affordable than those of a solicitor. [Source: Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division, 2024]

## What does a solicitor do

A solicitor is a qualified lawyer admitted to practise in NSW, holding a current practising certificate issued by the Law Society of NSW. While many solicitors handle conveyancing as part of their practice, their training and licensing allows them to advise on a far broader range of legal matters. [Source: Law Society of NSW, 2024]

In a property transaction, a solicitor can perform all the tasks listed above for a conveyancer. In addition, they can provide advice on:

- **Complex contract negotiations**: Solicitors can draft bespoke special conditions, negotiate terms with the other party's lawyer, and handle disputes that arise during the transaction.
- **Trusts and structures**: They can advise on purchasing through a family trust, self-managed superannuation fund, company, or other legal structure.
- **Commercial property**: Leases, tenancies, development agreements, and commercial contracts fall within a solicitor's scope.
- **Property disputes**: If a defect is discovered, a party breaches the contract, or a boundary dispute emerges, a solicitor can advise on legal rights and represent you in court if necessary.
- **Estate planning implications**: A solicitor can consider how the purchase or sale affects your broader estate plan, wills, and powers of attorney.
- **Tax advice (in conjunction with an accountant)**: They can advise on capital gains tax, GST on property transactions, and stamp duty concessions where legally relevant.
- **Mortgage and finance documents**: Solicitors can review loan documents, advise on guarantor arrangements, and explain complex lending terms.

A solicitor's broader legal knowledge becomes particularly valuable when something goes wrong or when the transaction involves non-standard elements. [Source: Law Society of NSW, 2024]

## Key differences

| Factor | Licensed conveyancer | Solicitor |
|---|---|---|
| **Qualification** | Diploma or Advanced Diploma of Conveyancing plus supervised practice | Law degree, practical legal training, admission to Supreme Court |
| **Regulator** | NSW Fair Trading | Law Society of NSW |
| **Scope of work** | Property transactions only | Property transactions plus all areas of law |
| **Cost** | Generally $800 to $2,000 (excluding disbursements) | Generally $1,200 to $3,500 (excluding disbursements) |
| **Professional insurance** | Required professional indemnity insurance | Required professional indemnity insurance |
| **Best for** | Straightforward residential purchases and sales | Complex transactions, trusts, disputes, commercial property |
| **Can appear in court** | No | Yes |
| **Can give general legal advice** | Limited to conveyancing matters | Yes, across all legal areas |

[Source: NSW Fair Trading, 2024; Law Society of NSW, 2024; Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division, 2024]

The fundamental difference is scope. A conveyancer is a trained technician for property transactions. A solicitor is a general legal practitioner who can also handle property matters. If your transaction is standard and nothing goes wrong, both will do the job competently. If complications arise, only a solicitor has the broader legal authority to address them.

## Qualifications in NSW

### Licensed conveyancers

To become a licensed conveyancer in NSW, a person must:

1. Hold a recognised qualification, typically the Diploma of Conveyancing (CPP41419) or Advanced Diploma of Conveyancing, obtained through a registered training organisation.
2. Complete a period of supervised employment under a licensed conveyancer or solicitor.
3. Apply to NSW Fair Trading for a conveyancer's licence under the Conveyancers Licensing Act 2003 (NSW).
4. Maintain professional indemnity insurance at the level required by the regulations.
5. Complete ongoing continuing professional development to maintain their licence.

Conveyancers must display their licence number on all correspondence and marketing materials. You can verify a conveyancer's licence through the NSW Fair Trading licence check online. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, 2024]

Licensed conveyancers in NSW are also members of the Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division (AIC NSW), which sets professional standards, provides ongoing education, and offers a complaints and disciplinary process for members. [Source: Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division, 2024]

### Solicitors

To practise as a solicitor in NSW, a person must:

1. Complete a law degree (Bachelor of Laws or Juris Doctor) at an accredited university.
2. Complete practical legal training, either through a graduate diploma in legal practice or a supervised traineeship.
3. Be admitted as a lawyer to the Supreme Court of NSW.
4. Hold a current practising certificate issued by the Law Society of NSW.
5. Complete mandatory continuing legal education each year.
6. Maintain professional indemnity insurance through the Law Society's approved insurance scheme.

You can verify a solicitor's practising certificate through the Law Society of NSW's online register. [Source: Law Society of NSW, 2024]

## When to use a conveyancer

A licensed conveyancer is the right choice for most straightforward residential property transactions in NSW. You should consider a conveyancer when:

- **The transaction is a standard residential sale or purchase**: You are buying or selling a house, townhouse, or unit under a standard Contract for Sale and Purchase of Land with no unusual conditions.
- **The contract is not heavily amended**: The contract contains only the standard conditions with minimal or no special conditions drafted by the parties.
- **No legal complications are expected**: There are no disputes about boundaries, no concerns about the title, and no trust or business structures involved.
- **You want to minimise costs**: Conveyancers typically charge less than solicitors for the same transaction because their scope is narrower.
- **You are refinancing**: A conveyancer can handle the discharge of one mortgage and registration of a new one.
- **You are buying off the plan**: Provided the contract is standard and you only need a specialist to review it and manage the settlement process.

Many thousands of property transactions in NSW each year are handled by conveyancers without issue. Their specialisation in property work means they are often highly efficient at managing the process. [Source: Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division, 2024]

## When to use a solicitor

You should engage a solicitor rather than a conveyancer when the transaction involves complexity or risk beyond the standard residential sale or purchase. Specific situations include:

- **Complex special conditions**: The contract has been heavily negotiated with bespoke clauses, or you need a contract drafted from scratch.
- **Purchasing through a trust, company, or SMSF**: These structures create additional legal obligations and tax considerations that a solicitor can advise on.
- **Commercial property**: Shops, offices, industrial premises, and development sites involve leases, tenancy issues, and zoning questions outside a conveyancer's scope.
- **Properties with title defects or disputes**: Encroachment, easement disputes, caveats, or adverse possession claims require legal advice beyond conveyancing.
- **Contractual disputes**: If the seller or buyer breaches the contract, or if a dispute arises about the condition of the property or inclusions, a solicitor can negotiate and, if necessary, commence court proceedings.
- **Rural or farming property**: These transactions often involve water licences, fencing obligations, and agistment agreements that require broader legal knowledge.
- **Deceased estate sales**: Where the property is being sold by an executor, probate issues may arise that a solicitor is better equipped to handle.
- **Property development or subdivision**: These transactions involve planning law, environmental regulations, and development approvals.

In these situations, engaging a solicitor from the outset avoids the cost and delay of transferring the file to a solicitor mid-transaction if complications emerge. [Source: Law Society of NSW, 2024]

## The conveyancing process step by step

Whether you engage a conveyancer or solicitor, the conveyancing process follows the same general sequence in NSW:

### 1. Pre-contract stage

Before signing the contract, your conveyancer or solicitor reviews the contract for sale and the supporting documents (title search, zoning certificate, drainage diagram, and strata reports for units). They identify risks, advise on conditions, and negotiate amendments with the seller's representative. [Source: Law Society of NSW, 2024]

### 2. Exchange of contracts

Once both parties are satisfied, contracts are exchanged. At this point the transaction becomes legally binding. The buyer pays the deposit, typically 10% of the purchase price, which is held in a trust account by the seller's agent or solicitor.

### 3. Cooling-off period

In NSW, residential buyers have a five-business-day cooling-off period after exchange unless they have waived it. During this period the buyer can cancel the contract by giving written notice, forfeiting 0.25% of the purchase price. There is no cooling-off period for sales at auction or where the buyer has received a s 66W certificate from their solicitor. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, 2024]

### 4. Post-exchange due diligence

After exchange, your representative conducts additional searches and enquiries, such as building and pest inspections, survey reports, and final title searches. Any issues discovered are raised with the seller's representative.

### 5. Finance approval

If the contract is subject to finance, the buyer must secure formal loan approval from their lender before the finance deadline. Your representative liaises with the lender to ensure mortgage documents are prepared.

### 6. Requisitions on title

Your representative may send formal questions to the seller about the property. In practice, most information is now obtained through searches rather than traditional requisitions.

### 7. Pre-settlement

In the days before settlement, your representative prepares settlement figures, calculates adjustments for rates and levies, and books the settlement through PEXA (the electronic conveyancing platform). [Source: PEXA, 2024]

### 8. Settlement

On settlement day, the balance of the purchase price is paid in exchange for the title documents and keys. In NSW, virtually all settlements now occur electronically through PEXA. Your representative attends the electronic settlement and confirms completion.

### 9. Post-settlement

After settlement, your representative lodges the transfer and mortgage documents with NSW Land Registry Services to register you as the new owner. They also notify the council, water authority, and strata manager (if applicable) of the change of ownership. [Source: NSW Land Registry Services, 2024]

## What you'll pay

Conveyancing fees in NSW vary depending on the complexity of the transaction, the property value, and the professional's experience.

### Conveyancer fees

For a standard residential purchase or sale, conveyancer fees in NSW typically range from:

- **Purchase**: $800 to $2,000 plus GST
- **Sale**: $600 to $1,500 plus GST

These figures are for professional fees only. Disbursements (out-of-pocket costs for searches, certificates, and registration fees) are additional and typically range from $300 to $600 for a standard transaction.

### Solicitor fees

Solicitor fees for conveyancing in NSW typically range from:

- **Purchase**: $1,200 to $3,500 plus GST
- **Sale**: $900 to $2,500 plus GST

Complex transactions, commercial property, and matters requiring additional legal advice will attract higher fees.

### Additional costs to budget for

- Title searches and council certificates: $200 to $400
- Strata report (for units): $250 to $400
- Building and pest inspection: $400 to $700
- Registration fees to NSW Land Registry Services: approximately $150
- PEXA fees: approximately $120 to $150
- Stamp duty (transfer duty): calculated on purchase price, this is the largest additional cost

Always request a written quote that clearly separates professional fees from disbursements before engaging a conveyancer or solicitor. [Source: Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division, 2024; Law Society of NSW, 2024]

## Questions to ask before engaging

Before you choose a conveyancer or solicitor, ask the following questions:

1. **Are you licensed to practise in NSW?** Request their licence number or practising certificate details and verify them online.
2. **How many years of experience do you have?** Experience in NSW conveyancing matters. Interstate experience may not translate directly due to differences in state laws.
3. **What is your fee structure?** Ask for a written quote itemising professional fees and estimated disbursements.
4. **Who will handle my file?** Will you deal directly with the licensed person, or will a junior staff member manage the day-to-day work?
5. **How do you communicate?** Email, phone, or a client portal? How quickly do you typically respond?
6. **Do you carry professional indemnity insurance?** This is mandatory, but confirming coverage is prudent.
7. **What are your office hours?** Property transactions often require urgent responses. Ensure they are accessible when needed.
8. **Can you provide references or reviews?** Check online reviews on independent platforms.
9. **Do you have experience with this type of transaction?** If the matter involves a trust, SMSF, commercial property, or off-the-plan purchase, ask about relevant experience.
10. **What happens if something goes wrong?** Understand their process for handling disputes, delays, and unexpected complications.

[Source: Australian Institute of Conveyancers NSW Division, 2024; Law Society of NSW, 2024]

## Red flags

Be cautious of the following warning signs when choosing a conveyancer or solicitor:

- **Fees that seem too low**: Unusually cheap quotes may indicate hidden charges, excessive delegation to unqualified staff, or cut corners on searches.
- **No written quote**: A reluctance to provide a detailed written quote is a warning sign.
- **Poor communication**: Slow responses, vague answers, or difficulty reaching the person handling your file during the enquiry stage suggest service issues will continue.
- **Pressure to sign quickly**: You should have adequate time to review the contract and ask questions before committing.
- **No professional body membership**: While not mandatory, membership of the AIC NSW or Law Society demonstrates professional commitment.
- **Unverified credentials**: Always check that the person's licence or practising certificate is current.
- **Handling the transaction from interstate**: NSW conveyancing has specific state-based requirements. A practitioner based in another state may lack familiarity with NSW procedures.

[Source: NSW Fair Trading, 2024]

## Electronic conveyancing (PEXA)

Since 2018, NSW has moved to a mandatory electronic conveyancing system through Property Exchange Australia (PEXA). This means the vast majority of property settlements in NSW are now conducted electronically rather than with paper documents and physical cheque exchanges. [Source: PEXA, 2024]

### What PEXA means for you

- **No physical settlement meetings**: Settlement occurs online with all parties connected simultaneously.
- **Faster registration**: Documents are lodged electronically with the Land Registry, typically within minutes of settlement.
- **Reduced risk of errors**: The system performs automatic checks on documents before settlement.
- **Funds transferred electronically**: The balance of the purchase price moves between bank accounts electronically, reducing the risk of lost or delayed cheques.
- **Your representative must be PEXA certified**: Both conveyancers and solicitors must complete PEXA training and certification to conduct electronic settlements.

Buyers and sellers do not need PEXA accounts themselves. Your conveyancer or solicitor manages the process through their PEXA workspace. However, you should confirm that your chosen representative is experienced with electronic settlements, as the system requires proficiency with digital workflows. [Source: Law Society of NSW, 2024]

## DIY conveyancing

In NSW, it is legally possible to do your own conveyancing. NSW Fair Trading provides information kits for individuals who wish to handle their own property transfer. [Source: NSW Fair Trading, 2024]

However, DIY conveyancing is generally not recommended for the following reasons:

- **Risk of error**: A single mistake in a legal document, calculation, or deadline can have serious financial consequences. You may lose your deposit, face penalties, or suffer delays that cost far more than professional fees.
- **No professional indemnity insurance**: If you make an error, you bear the full cost. A licensed conveyancer or solicitor carries insurance that protects you.
- **Limited access to systems**: DIY conveyancers cannot access PEXA directly. They must use a PEXA subscriber (typically a bank or law firm) to lodge documents, which adds cost and complexity.
- **No legal advice**: Without professional training, you cannot identify risks in the contract that an experienced practitioner would spot immediately.
- **Time and stress**: Conveyancing involves strict deadlines, multiple parties, and detailed documentation. Managing this alongside work and personal commitments is demanding.
- **Complexity of modern transactions**: Electronic conveyancing, mandatory verification of identity requirements, and increasingly complex standard contracts make self-conveyancing more difficult than in the past.

The fees charged by a licensed conveyancer or solicitor represent a small fraction of the total property transaction value. For most buyers and sellers, the peace of mind and risk protection professional representation provides far outweighs the cost. [Source: Law Society of NSW, 2024]
