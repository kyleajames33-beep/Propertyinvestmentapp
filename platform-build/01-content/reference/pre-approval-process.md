# Understanding the pre-approval process

Home loan pre-approval (also called conditional approval) is a practical step for buyers who want clarity on their borrowing capacity before they start searching for a property. This guide explains what pre-approval is, how the process works, what can go wrong, and how to use pre-approval effectively without damaging your credit profile.

---

## What pre-approval actually is

Pre-approval is an assessment of your financial situation, income, expenses, and credit history that gives you an indication of how much a lender may be willing to lend you. It is issued before you have found a property to purchase.

Pre-approval is **conditional**. It is not a guarantee that the lender will provide the loan. It indicates that based on the information assessed, the lender is prepared to offer finance up to a specified amount, subject to certain conditions being met. The most significant conditions are:

- The lender's valuation of the specific property you intend to purchase must meet their requirements.
- Your financial circumstances must not materially change between pre-approval and formal approval.
- The loan must meet the lender's credit policy at the time of formal approval, which may change.

Some lenders refer to pre-approval as "conditional approval" or "conditional pre-approval." The terminology varies but the concept is the same: an assessment of you as a borrower, not a final commitment to lend. [Source: ASIC Moneysmart]

### What pre-approval is not

Pre-approval is not:

- A binding loan offer. The lender can still decline the application when you find a property.
- A lock on an interest rate. Pre-approval does not fix the rate. Interest rates can change between pre-approval and settlement.
- A substitute for a "subject to finance" clause in your contract of sale.
- Unconditional approval. The lender has not yet assessed the property or completed final credit checks.

## Conditional vs unconditional approval

Understanding the difference between these two stages is critical for buyers.

**Conditional (pre-)approval.** The lender has assessed your income, expenses, credit history, and overall capacity to service a loan. They have indicated a maximum loan amount. Conditions remain outstanding, primarily property valuation and verification that your circumstances have not changed.

**Unconditional (formal) approval.** The lender has completed all assessments, including a valuation of the specific property, and has confirmed they will lend you the money. You will receive a Letter of Offer and loan contract documents. This is the final stage before settlement.

Some contracts of sale contain a clause stating the sale is conditional on the buyer obtaining finance. This clause provides protection if formal approval is not granted. Note that auction sales are typically unconditional: if you bid at auction and win, you are committed to the purchase regardless of finance approval. [Source: Qantas Money Home Loans]

## The pre-approval process step by step

While the exact process varies between lenders, the standard steps are:

**1. Review the market.** Research lenders and loan products. A mortgage broker can compare options across multiple lenders and identify products suited to your situation.

**2. Check your financial position.** Before applying, review your deposit size, savings history, existing debts, credit score, and monthly expenses. Clean up any issues on your credit report and reduce unnecessary spending in the months before applying.

**3. Gather your documents.** You will need to provide documentation to prove your identity, income, assets, and liabilities. See the documents section below for a full list.

**4. Submit your application.** Complete the pre-approval application with your chosen lender or broker. At this stage, you do not need to provide the property address (unless the lender requires a preferred postcode). You will specify your desired loan amount.

**5. Credit assessment.** The lender conducts a credit check through one or more credit reporting bodies (Equifax, Experian, or Illion). They assess your income against their serviceability criteria, verify your expenses, and review your credit history. Responsible lending obligations under the National Consumer Credit Protection Act require lenders to make reasonable inquiries about your financial situation. [Source: ASIC Regulatory Guide 209]

**6. Outcome.** If successful, you receive a pre-approval letter stating the maximum loan amount, any conditions, and the expiry date. If declined, the lender should explain why.

## Documents you will need to provide

Lenders typically require the following documentation for pre-approval. Requirements vary by lender and may be more extensive for self-employed borrowers or complex income structures.

**Identity and personal details.**

- Driver licence, passport, or other government-issued photo identification
- Medicare card or additional ID for verification

**Proof of income.**

- Two most recent payslips for employed borrowers
- Group certificates or payment summaries for the most recent financial year
- Tax returns and notices of assessment for the last two years for self-employed borrowers
- Rental income statements or lease agreements for investment properties
- Evidence of other income sources (dividends, government benefits, child support)

**Evidence of assets and liabilities.**

- Bank statements for the last three to six months (all accounts)
- Statements for existing loans, credit cards, and lines of credit
- Evidence of your deposit (savings account statements)
- Superannuation statements
- Vehicle registration and asset valuations where applicable

**Property information (if applicable).**

- For pre-approval, you typically only need to nominate a preferred postcode or suburb. The specific property details are provided later when you seek formal approval.

APRA expects lenders to apply minimum discounts of 20 per cent on most types of non-salary income, including rental income, bonuses, commissions, and overtime. Lenders must also make reasonable inquiries about your living expenses and cannot rely solely on benchmark figures like the Household Expenditure Measure. [Source: APRA Prudential Practice Guide APG 223]

## How long pre-approval lasts

Most pre-approvals expire between 60 and 120 days after issue, with 90 days being the most common duration. Some lenders offer shorter periods (60 days) while others extend to six months. The expiry date will be stated on your pre-approval letter.

Pre-approval expiry periods vary across lenders:

- **CommBank:** typically 90 days
- **ANZ:** typically 90 days
- **Westpac:** typically 90 days
- **NAB:** typically 90 days
- **Qantas Money Home Loans:** 60 days with option to renew for a further 60 days

You should only apply for pre-approval when you are genuinely ready to purchase. Applying too early means the pre-approval may expire before you find a property.

---

## What happens when pre-approval expires

If your pre-approval expires before you purchase a property, you have several options:

**Renew with the same lender.** Most lenders allow you to renew or extend pre-approval. You may need to provide updated payslips and bank statements to confirm your financial situation has not changed. Some lenders treat a renewal as a new application, which may trigger a new credit enquiry.

**Let it lapse and reapply later.** If your financial situation is stable and you are not in a rush, you can allow the pre-approval to lapse and submit a fresh application when you are closer to purchasing. This means providing all documentation again.

**Apply with a different lender.** If you are unhappy with your current pre-approval terms or have found a better product, you can apply elsewhere. Be aware that this will result in a new credit enquiry on your file.

Some lenders will honour the original pre-approval assessment if you find a property shortly after expiry, provided your circumstances have not changed. Check with your lender or broker.

## Pre-approval vs formal approval: the gap between them

Pre-approval and formal (unconditional) approval are not the same. The gap between them is where many property purchases encounter problems.

**Property valuation.** Pre-approval assesses you as a borrower. Formal approval requires the lender to value the specific property. If the valuation comes in below the purchase price, the lender may reduce the loan amount or decline the application. This commonly occurs in rapidly cooling markets, for off-the-plan purchases where the completed property is worth less than the contract price, or for unique properties that are difficult to value.

**Credit department review.** Some pre-approvals are issued through automated systems and have not been reviewed by the lender's credit department. Full assessment pre-approvals are more reliable because they involve manual review. Digital or instant pre-approvals may be withdrawn at the credit department stage if issues are identified.

**Mortgage insurer assessment.** If your loan requires lenders mortgage insurance (LMI) because you are borrowing more than 80 per cent of the property value, the mortgage insurer must also approve the application. The insurer's guidelines may be stricter than the lender's, and they may decline applications that the lender would otherwise approve.

**Changed circumstances.** Any material change to your financial situation between pre-approval and formal approval can result in a decline. Common issues include job loss, reduction in income, taking on new debt (car loan, credit card), missed repayments on existing debts, or changes to your household (expecting a child).

## Rate locking: does pre-approval lock in a rate

No. Pre-approval does not lock in an interest rate. The rate applicable to your loan is typically the rate on the day of settlement or the day formal approval is granted, depending on the lender's policy.

**Rate lock is a separate feature.** A rate lock allows you to fix a specific interest rate for a set period, usually 90 days, for a fee. Rate lock is only available on fixed rate loans, not variable rate loans. If you want certainty about your rate, you need to request a rate lock separately from pre-approval.

Rate lock fees vary by lender:

- **CommBank:** $750
- **ANZ:** $750
- **Westpac:** 0.10% of loan amount (e.g., $500 on a $500,000 loan)
- **NAB:** 0.15% of loan amount (e.g., $750 on a $500,000 loan)
- **Great Southern Bank:** $600 per fixed rate loan
- **loans.com.au:** $350

Most rate locks expire after 90 days. If settlement does not occur within that period, you may need to pay the fee again to re-lock. Some lenders offer a "win-win" policy where you receive the lower of the locked rate or the rate at settlement if rates fall. Others require you to break the lock manually to access a lower rate, and the fee is not refunded. [Source: Great Southern Bank; Canstar; loans.com.au]

## Why pre-approvals can fall through

Even with a pre-approval in hand, formal approval is not guaranteed. The most common reasons for a pre-approved loan being declined at the formal approval stage are:

**Property valuation below purchase price.** If the lender's valuer assesses the property at less than the contract price, your loan-to-value ratio (LVR) may exceed the lender's limit or trigger LMI requirements you do not meet.

**Changed employment or income.** Losing your job, changing employers, moving from full-time to casual work, or a reduction in hours can all result in a decline. Lenders verify employment just before settlement.

**New credit applications.** Taking out a car loan, credit card, or personal loan after pre-approval increases your debt commitments and reduces your borrowing capacity.

**Adverse credit events.** Missing repayments, defaulting on accounts, or new listings on your credit file between pre-approval and settlement can derail the application.

**Changes in lender policy.** Lenders can change their credit policies, interest rate buffers, or serviceability criteria between your pre-approval and formal application. Pre-approval does not protect you from policy changes.

**Lenders mortgage insurance decline.** If LMI is required and the mortgage insurer declines the application, the lender cannot proceed even if they would otherwise approve the loan.

**Auction purchases.** If you buy at auction, the sale is unconditional. If formal approval is not granted after the auction, you remain legally bound to complete the purchase. You should seek full approval before bidding at auction.

## Shopping around: should you get multiple pre-approvals

You should not apply for multiple pre-approvals with different lenders simultaneously. Each pre-approval application typically triggers a "hard enquiry" on your credit report, which is visible to all future lenders.

**How credit enquiries affect you.**

- A single pre-approval enquiry has a minimal, temporary effect on your credit score.
- Multiple enquiries in a short period can significantly lower your credit score. Credit reporting bodies like Equifax consider multiple applications a risk factor.
- Lenders reviewing your credit file may interpret multiple recent enquiries as evidence you have been rejected by other lenders. Some lenders decline applications outright if they see more than one or two enquiries in a six-month period.

**A better approach.**

- Research products and compare lenders using comparison sites, broker advice, or lender websites before applying.
- Apply for pre-approval with one lender that best matches your needs.
- If declined, address the issue before applying elsewhere.
- A mortgage broker can help you identify the most suitable lender for your circumstances without submitting multiple applications. [Source: Equifax; Canstar]

## Digital pre-approval vs full assessment

The speed and reliability of pre-approval varies significantly depending on how it is processed.

**Digital or system-generated pre-approval.** Some lenders offer instant or same-day pre-approval through online platforms. These are typically generated by automated decision engines and rely on algorithms and credit scoring. While fast, they may not involve a detailed review by a credit assessor. Digital pre-approvals are more likely to be withdrawn or modified at the formal approval stage if complexities arise.

**Full assessment pre-approval.** This involves a human credit assessor manually reviewing your application, verifying your documents, and applying the lender's serviceability policy. Full assessment pre-approvals typically take two to five business days but are more robust. They are less likely to be overturned at the formal approval stage because they have already been through detailed scrutiny.

For most buyers, a full assessment pre-approval provides greater confidence when making offers. If you are considering an auction purchase or need a short finance clause, full assessment pre-approval is strongly preferable. [Source: iBuyNew]

## Questions to ask your broker about pre-approval

1. Is this pre-approval a full assessment or a system-generated pre-approval?
2. How long does the pre-approval last, and what is the renewal process?
3. Will the lender conduct a full credit check now, or only at formal approval?
4. What conditions are attached to this pre-approval?
5. If I find a property above or below the pre-approved amount, what happens?
6. Does the lender offer rate lock, and what is the fee?
7. What is the lender's current valuation policy (in-house, panel valuers, AVMs)?
8. If my circumstances change slightly, how much flexibility does the lender offer?
9. Will renewing the pre-approval trigger another credit enquiry?
10. What is the typical turnaround time from application to formal approval?

This information is general in nature and does not constitute financial advice or lending advice. It does not take into account your individual objectives, financial situation, or needs. Pre-approval policies, fees, and terms vary between lenders and change over time. Before applying for pre-approval, you should consider your personal circumstances and consult a licensed mortgage broker or financial adviser.
