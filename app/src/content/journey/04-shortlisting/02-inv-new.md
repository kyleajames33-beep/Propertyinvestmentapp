# Shortlisting investment properties as a new investor

Shortlisting investment properties is a fundamentally different exercise to shortlisting a home. Where an owner-occupier can factor in emotional preferences, an investor must focus on numbers. The goal is not to find a property you would live in. It is to find a property that delivers the financial outcome you are targeting, whether that is rental income, capital growth, or a combination of both.

## Investment property scorecard: numbers-driven evaluation

A scorecard for investment properties weights financial criteria far more heavily than personal preference. Every property you consider should be evaluated against the same metrics so you can compare opportunities on equal terms.

Build your scorecard around these core categories:

- Purchase price relative to median: is the property below, at, or above the suburb median?
- Gross rental yield: annual rent divided by purchase price
- Net rental yield: annual rent minus expenses, divided by purchase price
- Estimated cashflow: rent received minus all outgoings including mortgage repayments
- Vacancy rate in the suburb: from SQM Research or similar data providers
- Historical capital growth: median price changes over 5 and 10 years
- Proximity to demand drivers: employment centres, universities, hospitals, transport
- Property condition: estimated maintenance and renovation costs
- Holding costs: council rates, insurance, property management fees, land tax

Each category should be weighted according to your strategy. A cashflow-focused investor might weight yield and vacancy rate higher. A growth-focused investor might weight historical capital growth and demand drivers higher. The scorecard keeps your strategy front and centre rather than letting a stylish kitchen distract you from a poor yield.

## Cashflow analysis: estimating rental income vs expenses

Cashflow is the single most important metric for many new investors because it determines whether you can afford to hold the property. Positive cashflow means the property pays for itself. Negative cashflow means you contribute from your own income each month.

### Estimating rental income

Start with advertised rents for comparable properties in the same suburb. Check listings on major portals, but also look at actual leased prices where available, as these reflect what tenants actually pay rather than what landlords hope for. Property managers can provide rental appraisals before you purchase, and these are worth requesting for any serious contender.

Assume a small buffer below the appraisal figure. Markets soften, and overestimating rent is one of the most common mistakes new investors make. If the appraisal is $600 per week, model your cashflow on $580 to create a margin for error.

### Identifying expenses

Expenses fall into two categories: fixed and variable.

Fixed expenses recur regardless of whether the property is tenanted. These include:

- Council rates
- Water rates (where the landlord pays these)
- Land tax (depending on NSW and property value)
- Building and landlord insurance
- Property management fees (typically 6% to 10% of rent collected)
- Mortgage repayments (principal and interest, or interest only)

Variable expenses depend on circumstances. These include:

- Maintenance and repairs
- Body corporate or strata fees
- Property advertising and leasing fees
- Accounting and tax preparation fees
- Vacancy periods where no rent is received

Add a contingency buffer of 10% to 15% of annual rent for unexpected repairs and vacancies. Properties always cost more than the initial spreadsheet suggests.

## Gross and net yield calculations with worked examples

Yield is the standard metric for comparing investment properties across different price points and locations. Understanding how to calculate it correctly prevents misleading comparisons.

### Gross yield

Gross yield is the simplest measure. It shows rental income as a percentage of the property value before any expenses.

The formula is: (Annual rent / Property value) x 100

Example: a property purchased for $650,000 that rents for $550 per week.

- Annual rent: $550 x 52 = $28,600
- Gross yield: ($28,600 / $650,000) x 100 = 4.4%

### Net yield

Net yield accounts for operating expenses, giving a more realistic picture of what the property returns.

The formula is: (Annual rent minus Annual expenses / Property value) x 100

Using the same property, with estimated annual expenses of:

- Council rates: $2,000
- Insurance: $1,500
- Property management: $2,860 (10% of rent)
- Maintenance allowance: $2,500
- Water rates: $800
- Body corporate fees: $0 (house)

Total annual expenses: $9,660

- Net annual income: $28,600 minus $9,660 = $18,940
- Net yield: ($18,940 / $650,000) x 100 = 2.91%

The gap between gross and net yield is substantial. Always calculate net yield before making decisions. A property advertising a 5.5% gross yield might deliver a 3.2% net yield once expenses are included.

According to CoreLogic data published in early 2026, gross rental yields across Australian capital cities range from under 3% in parts of Sydney to over 6% in selected regional centres. National gross yields have compressed in major cities as property prices have outpaced rental growth, making regional markets increasingly attractive for cashflow-focused investors.

## Capital growth indicators: what to look for in the property itself

While yield measures income, capital growth measures wealth creation. Growth is harder to predict than yield, but certain indicators suggest a property has better prospects than alternatives.

### Supply constraints

Markets with limited ability to add new housing tend to perform better over time. Look for suburbs surrounded by natural barriers such as national parks, coastlines, or topography that prevents development. Also look for council zoning restrictions that limit density. If the supply of land is fixed and demand is growing, prices tend to rise.

### Infrastructure investment

Government and private infrastructure spending drives demand. New rail lines, hospitals, universities, and commercial centres attract workers and residents. Check state government infrastructure plans and council development strategies for your target areas. Projects that are funded and under construction are more reliable indicators than projects that are merely proposed.

### Economic diversity

Suburbs dependent on a single employer or industry carry higher risk. A mining town with one major employer can see prices collapse when commodity prices fall. A suburb with a mix of healthcare, education, government, and private sector employment is more resilient.

### Population trends

The Australian Bureau of Statistics publishes regional population growth data. Suburbs with growing populations and household formation rates tend to experience stronger rental demand and price growth over time.

## Property condition and renovation potential

The condition of a property directly affects your upfront costs, ongoing maintenance, and ability to add value. During shortlisting, you need to assess condition quickly without yet engaging professionals.

### Assessing condition at open homes

Look for properties where cosmetic updates would significantly improve appeal. Fresh paint, new floor coverings, updated kitchen cabinetry, and improved landscaping can increase rent and value for relatively modest investment. These are the renovations new investors can typically manage.

Avoid properties with major structural issues unless you have expertise and significant capital reserves. Foundation problems, asbestos, extensive termite damage, and outdated electrical or plumbing systems can consume budgets quickly. The renovation shows on television rarely show the full cost and timeline.

### Renovation feasibility

Before shortlisting a property as a renovation candidate, check whether the existing structure allows for the changes you envisage. Load-bearing walls cannot be removed without engineering reports. Heritage overlays may prevent external changes. Setback requirements may prevent extensions. A quick check of the council planning portal can eliminate properties where your value-add strategy is not permitted.

## Body corporate and strata: what to check before shortlisting apartments

Apartments and townhouses with strata titles introduce an additional layer of cost and governance that detached houses do not have. Understanding these before shortlisting prevents surprises.

### Key documents to review

The strata records contain critical information about the building's financial and physical health. These records typically include:

- Minutes of recent annual general meetings and executive committee meetings
- The sinking fund or capital works plan showing future maintenance liabilities
- The administrative fund balance and levies charged
- Building defect reports and insurance claims history
- By-laws affecting tenants and landlords

Request these records early in the shortlisting process for any strata property you are seriously considering. A building with a healthy sinking fund and no major maintenance issues is a very different proposition to one facing a $50,000 special levy for cladding remediation or concrete cancer repairs.

### Strata fees and special levies

Factor body corporate fees into your cashflow calculations precisely. Fees vary enormously based on building age, size, and facilities. A small brick complex with no lift and no pool might charge $500 per quarter. A large building with a gym, pool, concierge, and lift might charge $2,000 per quarter or more.

## Comparable rental analysis

Comparable rental analysis validates your estimated rent figure and helps you understand the local rental market dynamics.

To conduct a reliable analysis:

- Select comparable properties within 1 to 2km of your target property
- Match property type, bedroom count, bathroom count, and condition
- Use properties leased within the past three months for current market relevance
- Adjust for differences: a property with air conditioning might rent for $20 more per week, one without parking might rent for $30 less

Record the comparable rents and calculate the median. If your estimate sits within 5% of this median, it is reasonable. If it is significantly higher, question whether you are being optimistic.

Property managers are a valuable resource for this analysis. They deal with actual tenants and know what properties lease for and how long they sit vacant. Speak to two or three managers in the area to build a realistic picture.

## Vacancy risk assessment

Vacancy is the silent killer of investment returns. A property that sits empty for four weeks each year has effectively lost nearly 8% of its annual rental income.

### Suburb-level vacancy rates

SQM Research publishes vacancy rates by suburb monthly. As a general guide:

- Below 2%: tight rental market, low vacancy risk
- 2% to 3%: balanced market
- Above 3%: oversupplied market, higher vacancy risk

These are guidelines, not rules. A vacancy rate of 3.5% in a suburb with strong long-term fundamentals may still be acceptable. A rate of 1.5% in a one-industry town with a major employer reviewing operations is riskier than the number suggests.

### Property-level vacancy factors

Some properties within a low-vacancy suburb still struggle to attract tenants. Common causes include:

- Poor condition relative to competing rentals
- Awkward layout or small bedrooms
- No parking in an area where parking is essential
- Proximity to noise sources: main roads, train lines, commercial areas
- Lack of air conditioning in hot climates or heating in cold climates

Walk through the property from a tenant's perspective. Would you rent it? If not, either budget for improvements or remove it from your shortlist.

## Building a shortlist of 3 to 5 properties for inspection

With all this analysis complete, your shortlist should contain three to five properties that represent genuine opportunities. Each should have:

- Passed your investment scorecard with a competitive score
- Demonstrated acceptable yield after net calculations
- Aligned with your cashflow requirements
- Satisfied your vacancy risk threshold
- Cleared preliminary due diligence on title, zoning, and strata records where applicable
- Offered a clear value proposition relative to the suburb median

Rank them from strongest to weakest. Your top two or three become the focus of your next stage: detailed due diligence, building and pest inspections, contract review, and negotiation.

Do not keep marginal properties on your shortlist out of indecision. A property that barely passes your criteria is likely to underperform. Better to reject it now and continue searching than to buy it and regret it.

The shortlisting stage for a new investor is where you separate emotion from economics. The properties that make your final list should do so on the strength of numbers: yield, cashflow, growth indicators, and risk metrics. Everything else is decoration.
