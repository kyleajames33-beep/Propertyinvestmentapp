# How to research a suburb before you buy

Buying your first home is likely the largest purchase you will ever make. The property you choose will shape your daily life, your commute, your access to services and, over time, your financial position. Many first home buyers spend weeks scrolling listings but only a few hours researching the suburb itself. This is a mistake. The macro view, the suburb, matters more than the micro view, the individual property. A well-researched suburb with strong fundamentals will lift the value of an average property over time. A poorly chosen suburb will drag down even a well-maintained home.

This guide will walk you through how to research a suburb systematically, using free and low-cost data sources, on-the-ground inspections and practical frameworks to build confidence in your decision.

## Why you research the suburb before the property

Every property market operates at two levels. The macro level is the suburb and its surrounding region. The micro level is the individual street and property. Macro factors, population growth, infrastructure investment, employment access and supply constraints, drive long-term price trends. Micro factors, the condition of the home, its orientation and block size, determine relative value within that suburb.

If you buy in a suburb with declining population, rising vacancy rates and oversupply, even a renovated three-bedroom home will struggle to appreciate. Conversely, a modest property in a suburb with strong demand drivers and constrained supply is more likely to benefit from market growth over time. Research the suburb first. Once you have a shortlist of two to four target suburbs, you can focus your property search with confidence.

## The fundamentals: median prices, days on market and auction clearance

Start with the numbers. These three metrics will give you a clear picture of how a suburb is performing and whether it sits within your budget.

### Median house and unit prices

The median price is the midpoint of all sales in a suburb over a given period. Half the properties sold for more, half for less. CoreLogic publishes median price data monthly for suburbs across Australia, broken down by houses and units. REA Group and Domain also publish quarterly market reports.

Look at the 12-month median rather than a single month, as monthly figures can jump around with a small number of sales. Compare the suburb's median to neighbouring suburbs and to the broader city median. A suburb priced 20 per cent below the city median may offer relative affordability, but ask yourself why. Is it genuinely undervalued, or is it in structural decline?

Sources: CoreLogic Hedonic Home Value Index; REA Group Market Trends; Domain House Price Report.

### Days on market

Days on market measures how long, on average, a listed property takes to sell. A low figure, under 30 days, suggests strong buyer demand and limited stock. A high figure, over 60 days, suggests buyers are cautious or the suburb is oversupplied.

You can find days on market data on Domain and realestate.com.au suburb profile pages. SQM Research also publishes this data at a suburb level. Watch for trends. If days on market has been rising for six months, buyer interest may be cooling. If it is falling, competition is heating up.

Source: SQM Research Property Market Indicators.

### Auction clearance rates

Auction clearance rates show the percentage of scheduled auctions that result in a sale on the day. Rates above 70 per cent typically indicate a seller's market. Rates below 60 per cent suggest a buyer's market. Rates between 60 and 70 per cent suggest balanced conditions.

SQM Research publishes weekly clearance rate data for Sydney and regional NSW. Remember that clearance rates are most relevant for suburbs where auctions are common. In many regional areas, private treaty sales dominate and clearance rates are less useful.

Source: SQM Research Weekly Auction Results.

## Free data sources every first home buyer should use

You do not need to pay thousands for research platforms. The following free or low-cost sources provide more than enough data for most first home buyers.

### CoreLogic

CoreLogic is Australia's largest property data provider. While its full research suite is subscription-based, CoreLogic publishes free monthly indices, commentary and suburb-level median price data through its media releases and partner websites. Many mortgage brokers also have access to CoreLogic reports and can share suburb-level data with clients.

Source: CoreLogic Research.

### Domain and realestate.com.au

Both portals offer free suburb profiles that include median prices, price trends over one, five and ten years, demographic data, nearby schools and transport links. The profiles also show the number of listings and average days on market. These are a good starting point for any suburb you are considering.

Sources: Domain Suburb Profile; realestate.com.au Suburb Insights.

### Australian Bureau of Statistics

The ABS provides free demographic and economic data through its Census and regular releases. Key datasets for property research include:

- Regional Population Growth: shows population change by local government area.
- Building Approvals: indicates future housing supply.
- Labour Force Data: shows unemployment trends by region.
- Census QuickStats: provides demographic snapshots including income, household composition and tenure.

Source: Australian Bureau of Statistics.

### SQM Research

SQM Research publishes a free weekly newsletter with vacancy rates, auction clearance rates and rental data. Its website also offers a searchable database of vacancy rates by suburb, stock on market levels and rental yields. The vacancy rate is particularly useful for first home buyers considering rentvesting.

Source: SQM Research.

### NSW Planning Portal

The NSW Planning Portal allows you to search for development applications, zoning maps and planning proposals in any local government area. This is essential for understanding what development is planned or approved near a property you are considering.

Source: NSW Planning Portal.

## Infrastructure and development: what to look for

Infrastructure investment is one of the strongest drivers of long-term property value. New transport links, schools and hospitals improve liveability and attract new residents. Here is what to research.

### Transport infrastructure

Check the Infrastructure Australia Priority List for major projects planned or underway in NSW. At a state level, Transport for NSW publishes project updates on road, rail and bus upgrades. Key questions to ask:

- Is there a new rail station or metro line planned within five kilometres?
- Are major road upgrades underway to reduce congestion?
- Is the suburb serviced by frequent public transport to employment centres?

Properties within walking distance of new or upgraded transport links often see stronger demand over time, as noted in infrastructure corridor studies. Be cautious, though. Major projects can take a decade to complete, and construction disruption can affect liveability in the short term.

Sources: Infrastructure Australia Priority List; Transport for NSW.

### Schools and education

School catchment zones affect demand for family homes. Use the NSW Education website to check which public schools service a suburb and whether they are rated high-performing. New school announcements are also worth watching. A new public school in a growth area is a positive signal for young family demand.

Source: NSW Department of Education School Locator.

### Hospitals and health services

Proximity to hospitals and medical centres matters for liveability, particularly for older buyers and families. Check the NSW Health website for major hospital upgrades or new facilities planned for the region.

Source: NSW Health Infrastructure.

## Supply indicators: development applications and zoning changes

Oversupply is a risk for any property buyer. When too many new homes are built in a short period, prices and rents can stagnate. Here is how to assess supply risk.

### Development applications and approvals

Search the NSW Planning Portal for development applications in your target suburb. Look at the number and scale of residential projects approved or in the pipeline. A small number of infill developments is normal. Dozens of high-rise apartment approvals in a concentrated area signal potential oversupply.

The ABS also publishes building approval data by local government area. A sustained spike in approvals, above the long-term average, is a warning sign.

Sources: NSW Planning Portal; ABS Building Approvals.

### Zoning changes

Zoning changes can increase the potential supply of housing in a suburb overnight. Check the local council's Local Environmental Plan and any planning proposals under review. A rezoning from low-density to medium-density residential may increase development pressure on established streets.

Source: NSW Planning Portal.

## Visiting suburbs: what to do on the ground

Data tells part of the story. Your own observations tell the rest. Plan multiple visits to each suburb on your shortlist at different times of day and week.

### Attend open homes

Go to at least five open homes in your target suburb, even if you are not ready to buy. This will calibrate your expectations on price, condition and what is available at your budget level. Take notes on the number of attendees, the agent's tone and how long the property has been listed.

### Walk the streets

Visit on a Saturday morning, a weekday evening and a Sunday afternoon. Listen for noise from main roads, train lines or industrial areas. Check the condition of surrounding homes. Are they well maintained? Are there many rental properties with neglected gardens? Talk to locals at cafes or parks. Ask what they like about living there and what they would change.

### Check the commute

Drive or take public transport from the suburb to your workplace during peak hour. A 30-kilometre commute that takes 90 minutes in traffic may wear thin after six months. Test the commute on multiple days to get a realistic sense.

### Visit local amenities

Walk to the nearest shops, park and transport stop. Are they well-maintained and well-used? New cafes, gyms and small retail openings can signal gentrification and rising demand. Empty storefronts and closed businesses can signal the opposite.

## Rentvesting as an alternative strategy

If your preferred suburbs are out of reach, rentvesting is worth considering. Rentvesting means renting a home in a location you want to live while owning an investment property in a location you can afford. This strategy has grown in popularity among first home buyers in Sydney and Melbourne, where owner-occupied homes in desirable suburbs are often priced well above what first home buyers can borrow.

The advantages include flexibility. You can live where you want without being tied to a mortgage on an expensive property. Your investment property can be chosen purely on growth or yield criteria, not emotional factors. You may also benefit from tax deductions on the investment property, though you should speak to a tax professional about your specific circumstances.

The disadvantages include missing the First Home Owner Grant and stamp duty concessions in some states if you buy an investment first. You also need to be comfortable with the instability of renting, lease renewals and potential relocations.

If rentvesting appeals to you, the research process is similar, but you will prioritise rental yield and growth drivers over liveability factors for the investment property. SQM Research vacancy rate data is particularly useful here. Suburbs with vacancy rates below 2 per cent typically have tight rental markets and stronger landlord negotiating power.

Source: SQM Research Vacancy Rates.

## Creating a shortlist of target suburbs

By now you should have data and observations on several suburbs. It is time to narrow down. Create a simple spreadsheet with the following columns:

- Suburb name
- Median house price
- Median unit price
- Days on market
- Auction clearance rate
- Vacancy rate
- Distance to CBD or employment centre
- Transport links
- School catchments
- Major infrastructure planned
- Supply risk (low, medium, high)
- Your liveability score (1 to 10)

Score each suburb against your personal priorities. If you work from home, commute distance may matter less. If you have young children, school catchments matter more. If you are budget-constrained, median price and days on market will carry more weight.

Aim for a shortlist of two to four suburbs. Any more and your search becomes unfocused. Any fewer and you risk missing good opportunities or overpaying in a single market.

## Macro before micro: the research sequence

A common mistake is to fall in love with a single property and then try to justify the suburb. Reverse this. Research the suburb first. Confirm it meets your budget, lifestyle needs and risk tolerance. Only then should you inspect individual properties within that suburb.

The macro research filters out suburbs that do not fit. The micro research, property inspections, building reports and contract review, confirms whether a specific property offers fair value within that suburb. This sequence protects you from emotional decisions and ensures you are comparing like with like.

## Common research mistakes first home buyers make

### Falling for the median without understanding distribution

A suburb median of $900,000 might include pockets ranging from $700,000 to $1.4 million. Dig into the sales data. Look at what sold recently at the lower end and why. Was it a small block? A busy street? A dated interior? Understanding the distribution helps you spot value and avoid overpaying.

### Ignoring the rental market

Even if you plan to live in the home, the rental market matters. A suburb with high vacancy rates and falling rents may indicate oversupply or declining demand. If you need to relocate for work, you want the option to lease your property without prolonged vacancy.

### Buying on a main road to afford the suburb

Some buyers purchase on a busy road to get into a desirable suburb at a lower price. This rarely pays off. The discount on purchase is often permanent, and resale can be difficult. Unless the property has exceptional compensating features, avoid main road locations.

### Assuming all infrastructure announcements are guaranteed

Politicians regularly announce transport projects that never receive funding. Check whether the project is on the Infrastructure Australia Priority List with committed funding, or whether it remains an unfunded proposal. Unfunded announcements should not factor into your decision.

### Researching for too long without acting

Analysis paralysis is real. Set a deadline for your research phase, eight to twelve weeks is reasonable. By that point you should have a shortlist, a pre-approval and a clear budget. The market will not wait for you to read every report.

## When to consider a buyer's agent

Most first home buyers research and purchase without professional assistance. This is entirely reasonable in stable markets with adequate stock levels. However, there are situations where a buyer's agent may be worth considering:

- You are time-poor and cannot attend dozens of inspections.
- You are buying in a competitive market where properties sell within days.
- You are relocating from interstate and cannot visit suburbs in person.
- You want access to off-market opportunities not listed on portals.

A buyer's agent acts solely for the buyer, unlike a selling agent who represents the vendor. They charge a fixed fee or a percentage of the purchase price, typically 1.5 to 2.5 per cent. For a $700,000 purchase, this is roughly $10,500 to $17,500. Whether this represents value depends on how much time you have, how competitive the market is and whether the agent can secure a property below your maximum budget.

If you engage a buyer's agent, ensure they are licensed in NSW and specialise in your target area. Ask for recent comparable purchases they have made for clients and check reviews independently. The Real Estate Institute of NSW maintains a directory of licensed buyer's agents.

Source: Real Estate Institute of NSW.
